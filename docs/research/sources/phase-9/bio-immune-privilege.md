					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-subjectpageheader">
</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Concept in biology</div>
<p>Certain sites of the mammalian body have <b>immune privilege</b> (no immune response), meaning they are able to tolerate the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigens</a> without eliciting an <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammatory</a> immune response. <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">Tissue</a> <a href="/wiki/Medical_grafting" class="mw-redirect" title="Medical grafting">grafts</a> are normally recognised as foreign antigens by the body and attacked by the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>. However, in immune privileged sites, tissue grafts can survive for extended periods of time without <a href="/wiki/Transplant_rejection" title="Transplant rejection">rejection</a> occurring.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Hong&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hong-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Immunologically privileged sites include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Eye" title="Eye">Eyes</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Placenta" title="Placenta">Placenta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Testicles" class="mw-redirect" title="Testicles">Testicles</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">Central nervous system</a></li></ul>
<p>It is thought that immune privilege also occurs to some extent—or is able to be induced in—<a href="/wiki/Articular_cartilage#Articular_cartilage" class="mw-redirect" title="Articular cartilage">articular cartilage</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid23696917&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid23696917-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid24446149&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid24446149-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid23499618&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid23499618-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was once thought that, theoretically, it could also occur (or be inducible) in the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>, but this is now known to be incorrect,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Category:All_self-contradictory_articles" title="Category:All self-contradictory articles"><span title="The brain is part of the central nervous system, so this directly contradicts the assertion that the CNS is immunologically privileged. (March 2026)">contradictory</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> as it has been shown that immune cells of the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> contribute to the maintenance of neurogenesis and spatial learning abilities in adulthood.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Ziv&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ziv-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Immune privilege is thought to be an evolutionary adaptation to protect vital structures from the potentially lethal effects of an inflammatory immune response in those regions. Inflammation in the brain or eye could cause the loss of organ functions, while immune responses directed against a fetus could cause <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">miscarriage</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Immune privilege allows doctors to perform <a href="/wiki/Cornea_transplant" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornea transplant">cornea transplants</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Meniscus_(anatomy)" title="Meniscus (anatomy)">knee meniscal</a> transplantation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid2009121&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid2009121-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Mechanisms">Mechanisms</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immune_privilege&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mechanisms"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Antigens from immune privileged regions have been found to interact with <a href="/wiki/T_cell" title="T cell">T cells</a> in an unusual way: inducing tolerance of normally rejected stimuli.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Janeway&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Janeway-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Immune privilege has emerged as an active rather than a passive process.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Physical structures surrounding privileged sites cause a lack of <a href="/wiki/Lymph" title="Lymph">lymphatic</a> drainage, limiting the immune system's ability to enter the site. Other factors that contribute to the maintenance of immune privilege include:
</p>
<ul><li>Low expression of classical <a href="/wiki/Major_histocompatibility_complex" title="Major histocompatibility complex">MHC class Ia</a> molecules</li>
<li>Expression of immunoregulatory <a href="/wiki/Major_histocompatibility_complex" title="Major histocompatibility complex">nonclassical, low polymorphic class Ib MHC</a> molecules</li>
<li>Increased expression of surface molecules that inhibit <a href="/wiki/Complement_system" title="Complement system">complement</a> activation</li>
<li>Local production of <a href="/wiki/Immunosuppression" title="Immunosuppression">immunosuppressive</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytokine" title="Cytokine">cytokines</a> such as <a href="/wiki/TGF-%CE%B2" class="mw-redirect" title="TGF-β">TGF-β</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-microvet&#95;10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-microvet-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Presence of <a href="/wiki/Neuropeptide" title="Neuropeptide">neuropeptides</a></li>
<li>Constitutive expression of <a href="/wiki/Fas_ligand" title="Fas ligand">Fas ligand</a> that controls the entry of <a href="/wiki/Fas_receptor" title="Fas receptor">Fas-expressing</a> lymphoid cells.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Hong&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hong-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The nature of isolation of immunologically privileged sites from the rest of the body's immune system can cause them to become targets of <a href="/wiki/Autoimmune" class="mw-redirect" title="Autoimmune">autoimmune</a> diseases or conditions, including <a href="/wiki/Sympathetic_ophthalmia" title="Sympathetic ophthalmia">sympathetic ophthalmia</a> in the eye.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Immunologically_privileged_sites">Immunologically privileged sites</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immune_privilege&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Immunologically privileged sites"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Eye">Eye</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immune_privilege&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Eye"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ocular_immune_system" title="Ocular immune system">Ocular immune system</a></div>
<p>As well as the mechanisms that limit immune cell entry and induce immune suppression, the eye contains active immune cells that act upon the detection of foreign antigens. These cells interact with the immune system to induce unusual suppression of the systemic immune system response to an antigen introduced into the eye. This is known as <i>anterior chamber associated immune deviation</i> (ACAID).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Keino&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keino-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Streilein&#95;13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Streilein-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sympathetic_ophthalmia" title="Sympathetic ophthalmia">Sympathetic ophthalmia</a> is a rare disease which results from the isolation of the eye from the systemic immune system. Usually, trauma to one eye induces the release of eye antigens which are recognized and picked up by local antigen presenting cells (APC) such as <a href="/wiki/Macrophages" class="mw-redirect" title="Macrophages">macrophages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dendritic_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Dendritic cells">dendritic cells</a>. These APC carry the antigen to local lymph nodes to be sampled by <a href="/wiki/T_cell" title="T cell">T cells</a> and <a href="/wiki/B_cell" title="B cell">B cells</a>. Entering the systemic immune system, these antigens are recognized as foreign and an immune response is mounted against them. The result is the sensitization of immune cells against a self-protein, causing an autoimmune attack on both the damaged eye and the non-damaged eye.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Janeway&#95;9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Janeway-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In this manner, the immune-privileged property has served to work against the eye instead. T cells normally encounter self-antigens during their development, when they move to the tissue draining <a href="/wiki/Lymph_node" title="Lymph node">lymph nodes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Anergy" class="mw-redirect" title="Anergy">Anergy</a> is induced in T cells which bind to self-antigens, deactivating them and preventing an autoimmune response in the future. However, the physical isolation of eye antigens results in the body's T cells never having encountered them at any time during development.
Studies in mice have shown that the lack of presentation of eye self-antigens to specific T cells will fail to induce a sufficient amount of anergy to the self-antigens. While the lack of antigen presentation (due to the physical barriers) is sufficient to prevent the activation of autoreactive immune cells to the eye, the failure to induce sufficient anergy to T cells has detrimental results. In the case of damage or chance presentation to the immune system, the antigen presentation and immune response will occur at elevated rates.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Lambe&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lambe-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Placenta_and_fetus">Placenta and fetus</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immune_privilege&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Placenta and fetus"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Immune_tolerance_in_pregnancy" title="Immune tolerance in pregnancy">Immune tolerance in pregnancy</a></div>
<p>The mother's immune system is able to provide protection from microbial infections without mounting an immune response against fetal tissues expressing <a href="/wiki/Paternal" class="mw-redirect" title="Paternal">paternally</a> inherited <a href="/wiki/Alloantigen" class="mw-redirect" title="Alloantigen">alloantigens</a>. A better understanding of the immunology of pregnancy may lead to the discovery of reasons for <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">miscarriage</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Regulatory_T_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Regulatory T cells">Regulatory T cells</a> (Tregs) appear to be important in the maintenance of tolerance to fetal antigen. Increased numbers of Tregs are found during normal pregnancy. In both mouse models and humans diminished numbers of Tregs were associated with immunological rejection of the fetus and miscarriage. Experiments in mice involving the transfer of CD4+/CD25+ Treg cells from normal pregnant mice into abortion-prone animals resulted in the prevention of abortion.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Zenclussen2006&#95;15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zenclussen2006-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This confirmed the importance of these cells in maintaining immune privilege in the womb.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>A number of theories exist as to the exact mechanism by which fetal tolerance is maintained. It has been proposed in recent literature<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Zenclussen2007&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zenclussen2007-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> that a tolerant microenvironment is created at the interface between the mother and fetus by regulatory T-cells producing "tolerant molecules". These molecules including heme oxygenase 1 (HO-1), leukaemia inhibitory factor (LIF), transforming growth factor β (TGF-β) and interleukin 10 (IL-10) have all been implicated in the induction of immune tolerance. Foxp3 and neuropillin are markers expressed by the regulatory T-cells by which they are identified.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Testes">Testes</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immune_privilege&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Testes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Testicular_immunology" title="Testicular immunology">Testicular immunology</a></div>
<p>Sperm are immunogenic – that is they will cause an autoimmune reaction if transplanted from the testis into a different part of the body. This has been demonstrated in experiments using rats by Lansteiner (1899) and Metchinikoff (1900),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-HedgerBook&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HedgerBook-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Fijak&#95;18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fijak-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> mice<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kohno&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kohno-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and guinea pigs.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Teuscher&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Teuscher-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The likely reason for their <a href="/wiki/Immunogenicity" title="Immunogenicity">immunogenicity</a> or rather <a href="/wiki/Antigenicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Antigenicity">antigenicity</a> is that sperm first mature at puberty, after <a href="/wiki/Central_tolerance" title="Central tolerance">central tolerance</a> has been established, therefore the body recognizes them as foreign and mounts an immune reaction against them.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kaur14&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaur14-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Therefore, mechanisms for their protection must exist in this organ to prevent any autoimmune reaction. The <a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93testis_barrier" title="Blood–testis barrier">blood–testis barrier</a> is likely to contribute to the survival of sperm. However, it is believed in the field of testicular immunology that the blood–testis barrier cannot account for all immune suppression in the testis, due to (1) its incompleteness at a region called the <a href="/wiki/Rete_testis" title="Rete testis">rete testis</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Fijak&#95;18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fijak-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and (2) the presence of immunogenic molecules outside the blood–testis barrier, on the surface of <a href="/wiki/Spermatogonia" class="mw-redirect" title="Spermatogonia">spermatogonia</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-HedgerBook&#95;17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HedgerBook-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Fijak&#95;18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fijak-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The Sertoli cells play a crucial role in the protection of sperm from the immune system. They create the Sertoli cell barrier, which complements the blood-testis barrier.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kaur14&#95;21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaur14-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The protection is ensured by <a href="/wiki/Tight_junction" title="Tight junction">tight junctions</a>, which appear between two neighboring Sertoli cells.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Another mechanism which is likely to protect sperm is the suppression of immune responses in the testis.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kern&#95;23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kern-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-O&#39;Bryana&#95;24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O&#39;Bryana-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Central_nervous_system">Central nervous system</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immune_privilege&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Central nervous system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Neuroimmune_system" title="Neuroimmune system">Neuroimmune system</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> (CNS), which includes the brain and spinal cord, is a sensitive system with limited capacity for <a href="/wiki/Regeneration_(biology)" title="Regeneration (biology)">regeneration</a>. In that regard, the concept of "immune privilege" within the CNS was once thought to be critical in limiting inflammation.  The <a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier" title="Blood–brain barrier">blood–brain barrier</a> plays an important role in maintaining the separation of CNS from the systemic immune system but the presence of the blood–brain barrier, does not, on its own, provide immune privilege.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Galea&#95;25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Galea-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is thought that immune privilege within the CNS varies throughout the different compartments of the system, being most pronounced in the <a href="/wiki/Parenchyma" title="Parenchyma">parenchyma</a> tissue or "white matter".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Galea&#95;25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Galea-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of CNS as an "immune-privileged" organ system, however, has been overwhelmingly challenged and re-evaluated over the last two decades.  Current data not only indicate the presence of resident CNS <a href="/wiki/Macrophages" class="mw-redirect" title="Macrophages">macrophages</a> (known as <a href="/wiki/Microglia" title="Microglia">microglia</a>) within the CNS, but there is also a wide body of evidence suggesting the active interaction of the CNS with peripheral immune cells.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid16972896&#95;26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16972896-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Generally, in normal (uninjured) tissue, antigens are taken up by antigen presenting cells (<a href="/wiki/Dendritic_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Dendritic cells">dendritic cells</a>), and subsequently transported to the lymph nodes.  Alternatively, soluble antigens can drain into the lymph nodes. In contrast, in the CNS, dendritic cells are not thought to be present in normal parenchymal tissue or <a href="/wiki/Pericyte" title="Pericyte">perivascular space</a> although they are present in the <a href="/wiki/Meninges" title="Meninges">meninges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Choroids" class="mw-redirect" title="Choroids">choroids plexus</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Galea&#95;25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Galea-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  Thus, the CNS is thought to be limited in its capacity to deliver antigens to local lymph nodes and cause T-cell activation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Mendez-Fernandez&#95;27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mendez-Fernandez-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Although there is no conventional lymphatic system in the CNS, the drainage of antigens from CNS tissue into the cervical lymph nodes has been demonstrated. The response elicited in the lymph nodes to CNS antigens is skewed towards B-cells. Dendritic cells from <a href="/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid" title="Cerebrospinal fluid">cerebrospinal fluid</a> have been found to migrate to B-cell follicles of cervical lymph nodes.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Hatterer&#95;28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hatterer-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The skewing of the response to antigen from the CNS towards a <a href="/wiki/Humoral_immunity" title="Humoral immunity">humoral response</a> means that a more dangerous inflammatory T-cell response can be avoided.
</p><p>The induction of systemic tolerance to an antigen introduced into the CNS has been previously shown.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Wenkel&#95;29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wenkel-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This was seen in the absence of the T-cell mediated inflammatory "delayed type hypersensitivity reaction" (DTH) when the antigen was reintroduced in another part of the body. This response is analogous to ACAID in the eye.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2022)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Clinical_applications">Clinical applications</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immune_privilege&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Clinical applications"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>There is great potential for use of molecular mechanisms present in immune privileged sites in transplantations, especially <a href="/wiki/Allotransplantation" title="Allotransplantation">allotransplantations</a>. Compared to skin allografts, which are rejected in almost 100% of cases, <a href="/wiki/Corneal_transplantation" title="Corneal transplantation">corneal allografts</a> survive long-term in 50–90% of cases. Immune privileged allografts survive even without <a href="/wiki/Immunosuppression" title="Immunosuppression">immunosuppression</a>, which is routinely applied to different tissue/organ recipients.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Research suggests that the exploitation of anterior chamber-associated immune deviation (ACAID), aqueous humor and its anti-inflammatory properties and the induction of <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_T_cell" title="Regulatory T cell">regulatory T cells</a> (Treg) may lead to increased survival of allotransplants.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Another option of exploitation of immune privilege is <a href="/wiki/Gene_therapy" title="Gene therapy">gene therapy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sertoli_cell" title="Sertoli cell">Sertoli cells</a> have already been used in research to produce <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a> in live diabetic mice. The Sertoli cells were genetically engineered using <a href="/wiki/Recombinant_organism" class="mw-redirect" title="Recombinant organism">recombinant</a> <a href="/wiki/Lentivirus" title="Lentivirus">lentivirus</a> to produce insulin and then transplanted into mice. Even though the results were only short-term, the research team established that it is possible to use genetically engineered Sertoli cells in cell therapy.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Sertoli cells were also exploited in experiments for their <a href="/wiki/Immunosuppression" title="Immunosuppression">immunosuppressive</a> function. They were used to protect and nurture islets producing insulin to treat <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_1" class="mw-redirect" title="Diabetes mellitus type 1">type I diabetes</a>. The exploitation of Sertoli cells significantly increased the survival of transplanted islets. However, more experiments must be conducted before this method may be tested in human medicine as part of clinical trials.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In another study on <a href="/wiki/Type_II_diabetes" class="mw-redirect" title="Type II diabetes">type II diabetic</a> and obese mice, the transplantation of microencapsulated Sertoli cells in the <a href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_tissue" title="Subcutaneous tissue">subcutaneous</a> abdominal fat depot lead to the return of normal glucose levels in 60% of the animals.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History_of_research">History of research</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Immune_privilege&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History of research"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The existence of immune privileged regions of the eye was recognized as early as the late 19th century and investigated by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Medawar" title="Peter Medawar">Peter Medawar</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The original explanation of this phenomenon was that physical barriers around the immune privileged site enabled it to avoid detection from the immune system altogether, preventing the immune system from responding to any antigens present. More recent investigations have revealed a number of different mechanisms by which immune privileged sites interact with the immune system.
