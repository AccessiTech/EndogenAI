					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-subjectpageheader">
</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Phenomenon observed in several animals</div>
<p><b>Hippocampal replay</b> is a phenomenon observed in <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbirds</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkeys</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Although replay was first characterized in non-human animals, replay-like sequential reactivation has also been reported in humans using non-invasive methods such as simultaneous EEG–fMRI, where transient replay events are associated with hippocampal activity and coordinated changes across large-scale brain networks.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Huang2024&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huang2024-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
During <a href="/wiki/Sleep" title="Sleep">sleep</a> or awake rest, replay refers to the re-occurrence of a sequence of cell activations that also occurred during activity, but the replay has a much faster time scale. It may be in the same order, or in reverse. Cases were also found where a sequence of activations occurs before the actual activity, but it is still the same sequence. This is called <b>preplay</b>.
</p><p>The phenomenon has mostly been observed in the <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a>, a brain region associated with <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Navigation#Navigation_in_spatial_cognition" title="Navigation">spatial navigation</a>. Specifically, the cells that exhibit this behavior are <a href="/wiki/Place_cell" title="Place cell">place cells</a>, characterized by reliably increasing their activity when the animal is in a certain location in space. During navigation, the place cells fire in a sequence according to the path of the animal. In a replay instance, the cells are activated as if in response to the same spatial path, but at a much faster rate than the animal actually moved in. Hippocampal replay has been proposed to support memory consolidation and the construction of internal "cognitive maps" of space and events.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> More recent computational and behavioral work suggests that replay may also contribute to planning and flexible decision making by allowing prospective sequences of states or actions to be evaluated before they are executed.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Jensen2024&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen2024-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Background">Background</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hippocampal_replay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Place_cell" title="Place cell">Place cell</a> activity was already well established when the first study explored this phenomenon in 1989.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They showed that neural activity of single place cells during <a href="/wiki/Sleep" title="Sleep">sleep</a> resembled the activity during the awake state. This activity was greater than that of other cells and this study was only the first step towards understanding replay. Subsequent studies showed that large groups of cells also demonstrated this type of increased activity during sleep. In addition, it was discovered that the order of activity of place cells was also replicated during sleep.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Firing sequences of three and more neurons observed in the hippocampus during locomotion were shown to recur selectively during subsequent slow-wave sleep more likely than during the preceding sleep, and the sequence replay was compressed during <a href="/wiki/High_frequency_oscillations" class="mw-redirect" title="High frequency oscillations">high frequency oscillations</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These high frequency field oscillations called <b>ripples</b> were observed in the sleep state and later shown to play a causal role in <a href="/wiki/Memory_consolidation" title="Memory consolidation">memory consolidation</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The next step was the discovery of replay during the awake state. In 1999, ten years after the initial discovery, neural recordings in the awake state were also shown to have replay activity.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is considerably more difficult to detect this activity in the awake state and several methods including <a href="/wiki/Neural_decoding" title="Neural decoding">Bayesian decoding</a> have been used to quantify replay events that occur during short wave ripples.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Recent advances include finding that replay can occur in reverse<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and that it has also been found to occur in different environments.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The role of replay in memory consolidation in these different conditions and environments is still being explored and several theories attempt to answer this question.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Location_and_behavioral_state">Location and behavioral state</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hippocampal_replay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Location and behavioral state"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Replay can occur in several different behavioral, physiological, and environmental conditions. The first distinction between awake and sleep states may represent different roles in memory consolidation. In the sleep state, the ripple events and place cell activity similar to that of the activity in the environment define the replay events. In the sleep state, there is also a distinction between <a href="/wiki/REM_sleep" class="mw-redirect" title="REM sleep">REM</a> (rapid eye movement) and <a href="/wiki/Slow-wave_sleep" title="Slow-wave sleep">SWS</a> (slow wave sleep) which has implications for replay events.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During SWS the place cells fire in a sequential order indicating replay and possibly indicate memory consolidation. However, during REM sleep where <a href="/wiki/Dream" title="Dream">dreams</a> occur in humans, replay events also occurred suggesting a possible role for place cells in dreams.
</p><p>In the awake state the same activity occurs, however it is more difficult to detect and the animal must be in a resting state. Lastly, there are many environments for replay events in the awake animal. The length of the track can be short or long and still be replayed by a population of place cells.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In addition, replay of a single environment can occur when the animal is in that environment or in different environments.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This may show that consolidation of memory is a persistent process that may occur in several different types of environments and behavioral conditions. The robustness of the replay events indicates the importance of this process.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Preplay">Preplay</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hippocampal_replay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preplay"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>As mentioned above, the sequential activation of hippocampal place cells according to their place fields may occur during rest periods before the animal is actually traversing the activated path, even if the animal has never experienced it before.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This suggests that hippocampal activation during rest may have a function not only in memory consolidation and retrieval, but also in <a href="/wiki/Planning" title="Planning">planning</a>: it contributes to the organization of the <a href="/wiki/Biological_neural_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological neural network">network</a> for improving the <a href="/wiki/Neural_encoding" class="mw-redirect" title="Neural encoding">encoding</a> of future events.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Sensory_cue_for_activation">Sensory cue for activation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hippocampal_replay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sensory cue for activation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">Sensory stimuli</a> can induce replay events or enhance the replay:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> in the awake state, replay often begins from the current location and continue either forward or backward in time, and nearby locations are more likely to be the <a href="/wiki/Place_field" class="mw-redirect" title="Place field">place fields</a> of neurons exhibiting replay than far away locations. This is like <a href="/wiki/Cued_recall" class="mw-redirect" title="Cued recall">cued memory retrieval</a>, where a sensory input triggers retrieval of similar or relevant memories. The cue may even trigger a replay in a different environment, if the place cells cued represent a location in a different environment in addition to the current location of the animal.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Interaction_with_cortex">Interaction with cortex</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hippocampal_replay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Interaction with cortex"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Several studies are beginning to understand that replay may not only occur in the <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a>. Replay has been linked to coherent activity in cortical regions of the <a href="/wiki/Default_mode_network" title="Default mode network">default mode network</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In humans, simultaneous EEG–fMRI measurements have shown that replay-like events are accompanied by widespread activation across the hippocampus, <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">medial prefrontal cortex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Posterior_cingulate_cortex" title="Posterior cingulate cortex">posterior cingulate cortex</a>, and other regions of the default mode network, indicating that replay is embedded in coordinated activity patterns across large-scale brain networks.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Huang2024&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huang2024-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After learning, similar replay events occurred in both the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neocortex" title="Neocortex">cortex</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In addition, the <a href="/wiki/Visual_cortex" title="Visual cortex">visual cortex</a> showed population activity that was both coordinated into discrete time regions and that it occurred simultaneously with activity in the hippocampus.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These findings, together with the large-scale activation patterns reported in humans, suggest that replay-related activity is not confined to the hippocampus but reflects coordinated interactions between hippocampal circuits and distributed cortical networks.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Huang2024&#95;5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huang2024-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Concurrent re-activation in the hippocampus and cortex may demonstrate that memory consolidation requires cortical input/output to maintain a memory. This hypothesis fits well with the idea that the cortex plays an integral role in memory retrieval after consolidation occurs. Therefore, hippocampal replay may play the role of information transfer between the hippocampus and cortex, yet this idea requires further verification. Human neuroimaging results demonstrating replay-triggered activation across distributed cortical regions further support the idea that replay contributes to information exchange within large-scale networks involved in memory and internally directed cognition.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Huang2024&#95;5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huang2024-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>}
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Function_in_memory">Function in memory</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hippocampal_replay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Function in memory"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Hippocampal replay in the awake state has been implicated, though not demonstrated, to correlate with performance in <a href="/wiki/Navigation#Navigation_in_spatial_cognition" title="Navigation">navigation</a> tasks after the replay event.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Replay may play a role in consolidation of memories related to spatial location,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> although a clear causal relationship between replay and memory consolidation is still unproven. Furthermore, replay also seems to be related to <a href="/wiki/Memory_retrieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Memory retrieval">memory retrieval</a>: it is activated by cues that also trigger memory retrieval, and in situations that require it, such as <a href="/wiki/Planning" title="Planning">planning</a> a trajectory based on the consequences of past choices. This relationship is also still only correlative, but there are studies showing evidence for the necessity of replay in successful memory retrieval.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In addition to retrieval, hippocampal replay has been proposed to support prospective evaluation during decision making, where internally generated sequences allow an organism to compare possible future actions before choosing a path.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Jensen2024&#95;7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen2024-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Computational modeling has shown that replay-like sequences can act as internal simulations (“rollouts”) that support goal-directed behavior by passing predictive information to frontal control circuits involved in planning.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Jensen2024&#95;7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen2024-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Experimental work in humans also indicates that the timing and frequency of replay-like events during rest can relate to subsequent planning performance, consistent with the idea that replay contributes to flexible, model-based decision making.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Jensen2024&#95;7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jensen2024-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>}
</p><p>Replay is likely to participate in both the consolidation of memories and in building a <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_map" title="Cognitive map">cognitive map</a>. Replay is also likely to play an important role in generating and maintaining a value map, which is a proposed variation of cognitive map in which memories are reinforced according to their values. <sup id="cite&#95;ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
