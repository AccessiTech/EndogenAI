					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-subjectpageheader">
</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Formation of myelin sheaths in the nervous system</div>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Periferal_nerve_myelination.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Periferal_nerve_myelination.jpg/500px-Periferal_nerve_myelination.jpg" decoding="async" width="500" height="141" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a8/Periferal_nerve_myelination.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="822" data-file-height="232" /></a><figcaption>Myelination of a peripheral nerve by a <a href="/wiki/Schwann_cell" title="Schwann cell">Schwann cell</a></figcaption></figure>
<p><b>Myelination</b>, or <b>myelinogenesis</b>, is the formation and development of <a href="/wiki/Myelin_sheaths" class="mw-redirect" title="Myelin sheaths">myelin sheaths</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a>, typically initiated in late prenatal <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_nervous_system" title="Development of the nervous system">neurodevelopment</a> and continuing throughout postnatal development.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The term <i>myelinogenesis</i> is also sometimes used to differentiate the very early stages of embryonic myelination.
</p><p>Myelin is formed by <a href="/wiki/Oligodendrocytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Oligodendrocytes">oligodendrocytes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schwann_cell" title="Schwann cell">Schwann cells</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system" title="Peripheral nervous system">peripheral nervous system</a>.  Myelination continues throughout the lifespan to support learning and memory via <a href="/wiki/Neuroplasticity" title="Neuroplasticity">neural circuit plasticity</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Remyelination" title="Remyelination">remyelination</a> following injury.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid30510502&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid30510502-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Successful myelination of <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">axons</a> increases <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potential</a> speed by enabling <a href="/wiki/Saltatory_conduction" title="Saltatory conduction">saltatory conduction</a>, which is essential for timely signal conduction between spatially separate brain regions, as well as provides metabolic support to neurons.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid28862639&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid28862639-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Stages">Stages</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myelination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Stages"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Myelin is formed by <a href="/wiki/Oligodendrocytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Oligodendrocytes">oligodendrocytes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schwann_cell" title="Schwann cell">Schwann cells</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system" title="Peripheral nervous system">peripheral nervous system</a>. Therefore, the first stage of myelinogenesis is often defined as the <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">differentiation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oligodendrocyte_progenitor_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Oligodendrocyte progenitor cells">oligodendrocyte progenitor cells</a> (OPCs) or Schwann cell progenitors into their mature counterparts,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid31349008&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid31349008-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> followed by myelin formation around axons.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Watkins&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watkins-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The oligodendrocyte lineage can be further classified into four stages based on their relation to the onset of myelination:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kinney08&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kinney08-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li><b>Differentiation:</b> OPCs exit their proliferative, self-renewing state and begin to express genes and proteins associated with oligodendrocyte fate commitment.</li>
<li><b>Preoligodendrocyte:</b> These cells express the O4 antigen and develop multiple processes which extend radially with no particular organization.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-friedrich96&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-friedrich96-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Immature oligodendrocyte:</b> Sometimes referred to as premyelinating oligodendrocytes, these cells extend "pioneer processes" which contact axons and anchor premyelinating oligodendrocytes to neurons such that they are poised to commence myelinogenesis in response to axonal signals. These pioneer processes grow longitudinally along their target axons.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-friedrich96&#95;7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-friedrich96-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Mature oligodendrocyte:</b> After myelinogenesis, mature oligodendrocytes surround axons in organized, multilamellar myelin sheaths that contain <a href="/wiki/Myelin_basic_protein" title="Myelin basic protein">myelin basic protein</a> (MBP) and <a href="/wiki/Myelin_proteolipid_protein" title="Myelin proteolipid protein">myelin proteolipid protein</a> (PLP).</li></ol>
<p>Myelinogenesis thus encompasses the process of transition between phases 3 and 4.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kinney08&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kinney08-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Upon initiation of myelinogenesis, each pioneer process forms lamellar extensions which extend and elaborate circumferentially around the target axon. This forms the first turn of the myelin sheath.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-friedrich96&#95;7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-friedrich96-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The sheath continues to expand along the length of the target axon while new membrane is synthesized at the leading edge of the inner tongue of the developing myelin sheath, which begins to take on a spiral cross-sectional structure.
</p>

<p>To drive proper assembly of membrane layers, PLP is inserted into the membrane to stabilize interactions between external leaflets of the myelin membranes; MBP is locally <a href="/wiki/Translation_(biology)" title="Translation (biology)">translated</a> and inserted into the cytoplasmic membrane leaflets to strengthen myelin membranes internally.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid19896485&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19896485-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In concert with the formation of axonal <a href="/wiki/Node_of_Ranvier" title="Node of Ranvier">nodes of Ranvier</a>, the myelin sheath's edges form paranodal loops.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid24439382&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid24439382-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Mechanism">Mechanism</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myelination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mechanism"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Myelinated_neuron.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Myelinated_neuron.jpg/250px-Myelinated_neuron.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="250" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Myelinated_neuron.jpg/500px-Myelinated_neuron.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="600" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Transmission_electron_micrograph" class="mw-redirect" title="Transmission electron micrograph">Transmission electron micrograph</a> of a myelinated axon</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Neuron_with_oligodendrocyte_and_myelin_sheath.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Neuron_with_oligodendrocyte_and_myelin_sheath.svg/250px-Neuron_with_oligodendrocyte_and_myelin_sheath.svg.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="296" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Neuron_with_oligodendrocyte_and_myelin_sheath.svg/500px-Neuron_with_oligodendrocyte_and_myelin_sheath.svg.png 1.5x" data-file-width="410" data-file-height="485" /></a><figcaption>Neuron with oligodendrocyte and myelin sheath showing cytoskeletal structures at a <a href="/wiki/Node_of_Ranvier" title="Node of Ranvier">node of Ranvier</a></figcaption></figure>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Basic_helix%E2%80%93loop%E2%80%93helix" title="Basic helix–loop–helix">basic helix–loop–helix</a> transcription factor <a href="/wiki/OLIG1" title="OLIG1">OLIG1</a> plays an integral role in the process of oligodendrocyte myelinogenesis by regulating expression of myelin-related genes. OLIG1 is necessary in order to initiate myelination by oligodendrocytes in the brain, but is somewhat dispensable in the spinal cord.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Axon-derived signals regulate the onset of myelinogenesis. Researchers studied regenerating PNS axons for 28 weeks in order to investigate whether or not peripheral axons stimulate oligodendrocytes to begin myelination. Experimental induction of myelination by regenerating peripheral axons demonstrated that Schwann cells and oligodendrocytes have a shared mechanism to stimulate myelination.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-HWeinbergSpencer&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HWeinbergSpencer-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A similar study working to provide evidence for neuronal regulation of myelinogenesis suggested that myelin formation was due to Schwann cells that were controlled by an undefined property of an associated axon.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-HWeinbergSpencer&#95;11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HWeinbergSpencer-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Recent research in rats has suggested that <a href="/wiki/Apotransferrin" class="mw-redirect" title="Apotransferrin">apotransferrin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thyroid_hormone" class="mw-redirect" title="Thyroid hormone">thyroid hormone</a> act both separately and synergistically to promote myelinogenesis, as apotransferrin promotes expression of <a href="/wiki/Thyroid_hormone_receptor_alpha" title="Thyroid hormone receptor alpha">thyroid hormone receptor alpha</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Peripheral_myelinogenesis">Peripheral myelinogenesis</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myelination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Peripheral myelinogenesis"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Myelin_sheath_(1).svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Myelin_sheath_%281%29.svg/250px-Myelin_sheath_%281%29.svg.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="343" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Myelin_sheath_%281%29.svg/500px-Myelin_sheath_%281%29.svg.png 1.5x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="703" /></a><figcaption>1. Axon 2. Nucleus of Schwann cell 3. Schwann cell 4. Myelin sheath 5. Neurilemma</figcaption></figure>
<p>Peripheral myelinogenesis is controlled by the synthesis of proteins P1, P2, and P0.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Politis&#95;13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politis-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> By using <a href="/wiki/SDS-PAGE" title="SDS-PAGE">SDS-PAGE</a>, researchers revealed distinct bands with band sizes of 27,000 daltons (P1), 19,000 daltons (P2), and 14,000 daltons (P0). Studies have also shown that P1 and P2 are active before P0 since this protein comes from the peripheral nervous system.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Politis&#95;13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politis-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the process of regeneration, <a href="/wiki/Schwann_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Schwann cells">Schwann cells</a> re-synthesize proteins associated with myelin-specific proteins when axonal presence is re-established. Synthesis of myelin-specific proteins only occurs in Schwann cells associated with axons.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Politis&#95;13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politis-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Furthermore, membrane-membrane interactions between axons may be required to promote the synthesis of P1, P2, and P0.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Myelinogenesis_in_the_optic_nerve">Myelinogenesis in the optic nerve</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myelination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Myelinogenesis in the optic nerve"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The process and mechanistic function of myelinogenesis has traditionally been studied using <a href="/wiki/Ultrastructure" title="Ultrastructure">ultrastructure</a> and biochemical techniques in rat <a href="/wiki/Optic_nerves" class="mw-redirect" title="Optic nerves">optic nerves</a>. The implementation of this method of study has long allowed for experimental observation of myelinogenesis in a <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a> nerve that consists entirely of unmyelinated axons. Furthermore, the use of the rat optic nerve helped provide insight for early myelinogenesis researchers into improper and atypical courses of myelinogenesis.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TennekoonCohenPrice&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TennekoonCohenPrice-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 	
</p><p>One early study showed that in the developing rat optic nerves, formation of <a href="/wiki/Oligodendrocytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Oligodendrocytes">oligodendrocytes</a> and subsequent myelination occurs <a href="/wiki/Postnatal" class="mw-redirect" title="Postnatal">postnatal</a>. In the optic nerve, the oligodendrocyte cells divided for the final time at five days, with the onset of myelin formation occurring on or around day 6 or 7. However, the exact process by which the oligodendrocytes were stimulated to produce myelin was not yet fully understood, but early myelination in the optic nerve has been linked to a rise in the production of various lipids – cholesterol, cerebroside, and sulfatide.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TennekoonCohenPrice&#95;14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TennekoonCohenPrice-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>As researchers began to do postnatal research, they found that myelinogenesis in the rat optic nerve initially commences with axons the largest diameters before proceeding to the remaining smaller axons. In the second week postnatal, oligodendrocyte formation slowed – at this point, 15% of axons have been myelinated – however, myelinogenesis continued to rapidly increase. During the fourth week postnatal, nearly 85% of the axons in the rat optic had been myelinated.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TennekoonCohenPrice&#95;14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TennekoonCohenPrice-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During the fifth week and onward toward week sixteen, the myelination decelerated and the remaining unmyelinated axons were ensheathed in myelin.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Through the rat optic nerve, early research made significant contributions to knowledge in the field of myelinogenesis.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Role_of_sulfatides">Role of sulfatides</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myelination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Role of sulfatides"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Studies on the developing optic nerve revealed that <a href="/wiki/Galactocerebroside" class="mw-redirect" title="Galactocerebroside">galactocerebroside</a> (which forms sulfatide) appeared on the 9th post-natal day and reached a peak on the 15th post-natal day.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TennekoonCohenPrice&#95;14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TennekoonCohenPrice-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This expression was similar to a period where the optic nerve showed a maximal myelination period of the axon. As the activity of axon myelination decreased, and one could conclude that the activity of the enzyme is paralleled with the incorporation of <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> ([35S]) into sulfatide in vivo.
</p><p>The studies on a rat optic nerve revealed that 15 days post-natal is when an increase in myelination is observed. Before this time period, most of the axons, roughly about 70%, are not myelinated. At this time, [35S] Sulfate was incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Sulfatide" title="Sulfatide">sulfatide</a> and the activity of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cerebroside_sulfotransferase&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cerebroside sulfotransferase (page does not exist)">cerebroside sulfotransferase</a> reached a peak in enzyme activity. This time frame also showed a period of maximal myelination based on the biochemical data.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TennekoonCohenPrice&#95;14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TennekoonCohenPrice-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In the CNS, <a href="/wiki/Sulfatide" title="Sulfatide">sulfatide</a>, sulfated glycoproteins, and sulfated mucopolysaccharides appear to be associated with neurons rather than myelin. When graphing the amount of sulfatide made from [35S] and the activity of <a href="/wiki/Sulfotransferase" title="Sulfotransferase">sulfotransferase</a>, we get to distinguished peaks.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TennekoonCohenPrice&#95;14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TennekoonCohenPrice-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The peaks occur on the 15th post-natal day. These peaks corresponded with the maximal myelination period of the optic nerve that has been seen throughout the experiment.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TennekoonCohenPrice&#95;14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TennekoonCohenPrice-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In conclusion, the early phase of myelination was correlated with the increases synthesis of lipids, cholesterol, cerebroside, and sulfatide.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TennekoonCohenPrice&#95;14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TennekoonCohenPrice-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is likely that these compounds are synthesized and packaged in the Golgi Apparatus of oligodendroglia.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-TennekoonCohenPrice&#95;14-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TennekoonCohenPrice-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Even though the transport of these lipids is unknown, it appears that myelination is delayed without their synthesis.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myelination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Clinical significance"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Myelin provides axons with the ability for fast conduction of <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potentials</a>, and its damage can cause a number of <a href="/wiki/Demyelinating_disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Demyelinating disorder">demyelinating disorders</a> that negatively affect the functioning of the nervous system. One such disease is <a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">multiple sclerosis</a>, where demyelination occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NIH2015&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NIH2015-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Strategies have targeted the protection of oligodendrocytes and the promotion of <a href="/wiki/Remyelination" title="Remyelination">remyelination</a>, and others have examined the role of the immune system  in demyelination in multiple sclerosis.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rodgers&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rodgers-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid19597083&#95;18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid19597083-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Aging_brain" title="Aging brain">aging brain</a> the build up of <a href="/wiki/Amyloid_fibrils" class="mw-redirect" title="Amyloid fibrils">amyloid fibrils</a> in chronic <a href="/wiki/Amyloidosis" title="Amyloidosis">amyloidosis</a>,
has a toxic effect on the myelin sheath, and axons, leading to <a href="/wiki/Mild_cognitive_disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Mild cognitive disorder">mild cognitive disorder</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Maitre2023&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maitre2023-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Myelination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:FlechsigSaggital4.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/ff/FlechsigSaggital4.jpg" decoding="async" width="400" height="354" class="mw-file-element" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="443" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Primary_somatosensory_cortex" title="Primary somatosensory cortex">Primary somatosensory cortex</a> (CP: posterior centrale) and <a href="/wiki/Primary_motor_cortex" title="Primary motor cortex">primary motor cortex</a> (CA: anterior centrale) of a 7-month-old human fetus. Nissl-stained parasagittal section (Flechsig 1921)</figcaption></figure>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paul_Flechsig" title="Paul Flechsig">Paul Flechsig</a> spent most of his career studying and publishing the details of the process in the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a> of humans. This takes place mostly between two months before and after <a href="/wiki/Birth" title="Birth">birth</a>. He identified 45 separate cortical areas and, in fact, mapped the cerebral cortex by the myelination pattern. The first cortical region to myelinate is in the <a href="/wiki/Motor_cortex" title="Motor cortex">motor cortex</a> (part of <a href="/wiki/Korbinian_Brodmann" title="Korbinian Brodmann">Brodmann</a>'s area 4), the second is the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_cortex" class="mw-redirect" title="Olfactory cortex">olfactory cortex</a> and the third is part of the <a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_cortex" class="mw-redirect" title="Somatosensory cortex">somatosensory cortex</a> (BA 3,1,2).
</p><p>The last areas to myelinate are the <a href="/wiki/Anterior_cingulate" class="mw-redirect" title="Anterior cingulate">anterior cingulate</a> cortex (F#43), the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_temporal" class="mw-redirect" title="Inferior temporal">inferior temporal</a> cortex (F#44) and the <a href="/wiki/Dorsolateral_prefrontal" class="mw-redirect" title="Dorsolateral prefrontal">dorsolateral prefrontal</a> cortex (F#45).
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>In the cerebral convolutions, as in all other parts of the central nervous system, the nerve-fibres do not develop everywhere simultaneously, but step by step in a definite succession, this order of events being particularly maintained in regard to the appearance of the medullary substance. In the convolutions of the <a href="/wiki/Cerebrum" title="Cerebrum">cerebrum</a> the investment with medullary substance (myelinisation) has already begun in some places three months before the maturity of the foetus, whilst in other places numerous fibres are devoid of medullary substance even three months after birth. The order of succession in the convolutions is governed by a law identical with the law which I have shown holds good for the <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Medulla_oblongata" title="Medulla oblongata">medulla oblongata</a>, and the mesocephalon, and which may be stated somewhat in this way- that, speaking approximately, equally important nerve-fibres are developed simultaneously, but those of dissimilar importance are developed one after another in a succession defined by an imperative law (Fundamental Law of Myelogenesis). The formation of medullary substance is almost completed in certain convolutions at a time when in some it is not even begun and in others has made only slight progress.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>
