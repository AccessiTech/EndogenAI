					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-subjectpageheader">
</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Group of neurons in the cortex of the brain</div>
<p>A <b>cortical column</b> is a group of <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a> forming a cylindrical structure through the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a> of the brain perpendicular to the cortical surface.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Mountcastle1957&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mountcastle1957-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The structure was first identified by <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Lorente_de_N%C3%B3" title="Rafael Lorente de Nó">Rafael Lorente de Nó</a> in 1949 while <a href="/wiki/Vernon_Benjamin_Mountcastle" title="Vernon Benjamin Mountcastle">Vernon Benjamin Mountcastle</a> began to disentangle its physiology in 1957.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He later identified <a href="/wiki/Cortical_minicolumn" title="Cortical minicolumn">minicolumns</a> as the basic units of the neocortex which were arranged into columns.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Each contains the same types of neurons, connectivity, and firing properties.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bennett2020&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett2020-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Columns are also called <b>hypercolumn</b>, <b>macrocolumn</b>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <b>functional column</b><sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or sometimes <b>cortical module</b>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Neurons within a minicolumn (microcolumn) encode similar features, whereas a hypercolumn "denotes a unit containing a full set of values for any given set of receptive field parameters".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-horton&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-horton-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A cortical module is defined as either synonymous with a hypercolumn or as a tissue block of multiple overlapping hypercolumns.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Cortical columns are proposed to be the canonical microcircuits for <a href="/wiki/Predictive_coding" title="Predictive coding">predictive coding</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> in which the process of cognition is implemented through a hierarchy of identical microcircuits.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bennett2020&#95;4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett2020-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The evolutionary benefit to this duplication allowed human neocortex to increase in size by almost 3-fold over just the last 3 million years.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bennett2020&#95;4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett2020-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Cortical_Columns.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Cortical_Columns.jpg/250px-Cortical_Columns.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="195" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Cortical_Columns.jpg/500px-Cortical_Columns.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1800" /></a><figcaption>3D reconstruction of five cortical columns in rat vibrissal cortex</figcaption></figure>
<p>The columnar hypothesis states that the cortex is composed of discrete, modular columns of neurons, characterized by a consistent connectivity profile.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The columnar organization hypothesis is currently the most widely adopted to explain the cortical processing of information.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Mammalian_cerebral_cortex">Mammalian cerebral cortex</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cortical_column&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mammalian cerebral cortex"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">Cerebral cortex</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mammalian" class="mw-redirect" title="Mammalian">mammalian</a> <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Grey_matter" title="Grey matter">grey matter</a> encapsulating the <a href="/wiki/White_matter" title="White matter">white matter</a>, is composed of <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex#Laminar_pattern" title="Cerebral cortex">layers</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> cortex is between 2 and 3&#160;mm thick.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Saladin11&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saladin11-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The number of layers is the same in most mammals, but varies throughout the cortex. <a href="/wiki/Neocortex#Anatomy" title="Neocortex">6 layers</a> can be recognized in the neocortex, although many regions lack one or more layers. For example, fewer layers are present in the <a href="/wiki/Archipallium" class="mw-redirect" title="Archipallium">archipallium</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Paleopallium" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleopallium">paleopallium</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Columnar_functional_organization">Columnar functional organization</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cortical_column&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Columnar functional organization"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The columnar functional organization, as originally framed by <a href="/wiki/Vernon_Mountcastle" class="mw-redirect" title="Vernon Mountcastle">Vernon Mountcastle</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Mountcastle1957&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mountcastle1957-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> suggests that neurons that are horizontally more than 0.5&#160;mm (500&#160;μm) from each other do not have overlapping sensory receptive fields, and other experiments give similar results: 200–800&#160;μm.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  Various estimates suggest there are 50 to 100 <a href="/wiki/Cortical_minicolumn" title="Cortical minicolumn">cortical minicolumns</a> in a hypercolumn, each comprising around 80 neurons. Their role is best understood as 'functional units of information processing.'
</p><p>An important distinction is that the columnar organization is functional by definition, and reflects the local connectivity of the cerebral cortex.  Connections "up" and "down" within the thickness of the cortex are much denser than connections that spread from side to side.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Hubel_and_Wiesel_studies">Hubel and Wiesel studies</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cortical_column&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hubel and Wiesel studies"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/David_H._Hubel" title="David H. Hubel">David Hubel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Torsten_Wiesel" title="Torsten Wiesel">Torsten Wiesel</a> followed up on Mountcastle's discoveries in the <a href="/wiki/Postcentral_gyrus" title="Postcentral gyrus">somatic sensory cortex</a> with their own studies in vision. A part of the discoveries that resulted in them winning the 1981 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> was that there were cortical columns in <a href="/wiki/Visual_cortex" title="Visual cortex">vision</a> as well, and that the neighboring columns were also related in function in terms of the orientation of lines that evoked the maximal discharge. Hubel and Wiesel followed up on their own studies with work demonstrating the impact of environmental changes on cortical organization, and the sum total of these works resulted in their Nobel Prize.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Number_of_cortical_columns">Number of cortical columns</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cortical_column&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Number of cortical columns"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>There are about 200 million (2×10<sup>8</sup>) cortical minicolumns in the human neocortex with up to about 110 neurons each,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and with estimates of 21–26 billion (2.1×10<sup>10</sup>–2.6×10<sup>10</sup>) neurons in the neocortex. With 50 to 100 cortical minicolumns per cortical column a human would have 2–4 million (2×10<sup>6</sup>–4×10<sup>6</sup>) cortical columns. There may be more if the columns can overlap, as suggested by Tsunoda <i>et al</i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Jeff Hawkins claims that there are only 150,000 columns in the human neocortex, based on research made by his company Numenta.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Hawkins&#95;J.&#95;2021&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawkins_J._2021-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>There are claims that minicolumns may have as many as 400 principal cells,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> but it is not clear if that includes glia cells.
</p><p>Some contradict the previous estimates,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> claiming the original research is too arbitrary.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rakic&#95;12099–12100&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rakic_12099–12100-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The authors propose a uniform neocortex, and choose a fixed width and length to calculate the cell numbers. Later research pointed out that the neocortex is indeed not uniform for other species,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and studying nine primate species they found that "the number of neurons underneath 1 mm<sup>2</sup> of the cerebral cortical surface ... varies by three times across species." The neocortex is not uniform across species.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rakic&#95;12099–12100&#95;22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rakic_12099–12100-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  The actual number of neurons within a single column is variable, and depends on the cerebral areas and thus the function of the column.
</p>
