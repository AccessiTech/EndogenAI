<p><b>Hebbian theory</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Neuropsychological" class="mw-redirect" title="Neuropsychological">neuropsychological</a> theory claiming that an increase in <a href="/wiki/Synapse" title="Synapse">synaptic</a> efficacy arises from a <a href="/wiki/Presynaptic_cell" class="mw-redirect" title="Presynaptic cell">presynaptic cell</a>'s repeated and persistent stimulation of a postsynaptic cell. It is an attempt to explain <a href="/wiki/Synaptic_plasticity" title="Synaptic plasticity">synaptic plasticity</a>, the adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a> during the learning process. Hebbian theory was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Donald_Hebb" class="mw-redirect" title="Donald Hebb">Donald Hebb</a> in his 1949 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Organization_of_Behavior" class="mw-redirect" title="The Organization of Behavior">The Organization of Behavior</a>.</i><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Hebb&#95;1949&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hebb_1949-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The theory is also called <b>Hebb's rule</b>, <b>Hebb's law</b>, <b>Hebb's postulate</b>, and <b>cell assembly theory</b>. Hebb states it as follows:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Let us assume that the persistence or repetition of a reverberatory activity (or "trace") tends to induce lasting cellular changes that add to its stability. ... When an <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">axon</a> of cell <i>A</i> is near enough to excite a cell <i>B</i> and repeatedly or persistently takes part in firing it, some growth process or metabolic change takes place in one or both cells such that <i>A</i>'s efficiency, as one of the cells firing <i>B</i>, is increased.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Hebb&#95;1949&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hebb_1949-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 62">&#58;&#8202;62&#8202;</span></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The theory is often summarized as "<b>Neurons that fire together, wire together</b>."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, Hebb emphasized that cell <i>A</i> needs to "take part in firing" cell <i>B</i>, and such causality can occur only if cell <i>A</i> fires just before, not at the same time as, cell <i>B</i>. This aspect of causation in Hebb's work foreshadowed what is now known about <a href="/wiki/Spike-timing-dependent_plasticity" title="Spike-timing-dependent plasticity">spike-timing-dependent plasticity</a>, which requires temporal precedence.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:2&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Hebbian theory attempts to explain <a href="/wiki/Associative_learning" class="mw-redirect" title="Associative learning">associative</a> or <i>Hebbian learning</i>, in which simultaneous activation of cells leads to pronounced increases in <a href="/wiki/Synaptic_strength" class="mw-redirect" title="Synaptic strength">synaptic strength</a> between those cells. It also provides a biological basis for <a href="/wiki/Errorless_learning" title="Errorless learning">errorless learning</a> methods for education and memory rehabilitation. In the study of <a href="/wiki/Artificial_neural_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial neural network">neural networks</a> in cognitive function, it is often regarded as the neuronal basis of <a href="/wiki/Unsupervised_learning" title="Unsupervised learning">unsupervised learning</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Engrams,_cell_assembly_theory,_and_learning"><span id="Engrams.2C_cell_assembly_theory.2C_and_learning"></span>Engrams, cell assembly theory, and learning</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebbian_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Engrams, cell assembly theory, and learning"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Hebbian theory provides an explanation for how neurons might connect to become <a href="/wiki/Engram_(neuropsychology)" title="Engram (neuropsychology)">engrams</a>, which may be stored in overlapping cell assemblies, or groups of neurons that encode specific information.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Initially created as a way to explain recurrent activity in specific groups of cortical neurons, Hebb's theories on the form and function of cell assemblies can be understood from the following:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Hebb&#95;1949&#95;1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hebb_1949-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 70">&#58;&#8202;70&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>The general idea is an old one, that any two cells or systems of cells that are repeatedly active at the same time will tend to become 'associated' so that activity in one facilitates activity in the other.</p></blockquote>
<p>Hebb also wrote:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Hebb&#95;1949&#95;1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hebb_1949-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>When one cell repeatedly assists in firing another, the axon of the first cell develops synaptic knobs (or enlarges them if they already exist) in contact with the soma of the second cell.</p></blockquote>
<p>D. Alan Allport posits additional ideas regarding cell assembly theory and its role in forming engrams using the concept of auto-association, or the brain's ability to retrieve information based on a partial cue, described as follows:
</p>
<blockquote><p>If the inputs to a system cause the same pattern of activity to occur repeatedly, the set of active elements constituting that pattern will become increasingly strongly inter-associated. That is, each element will tend to turn on every other element and (with negative weights) to turn off the elements that do not form part of the pattern. To put it another way, the pattern as a whole will become 'auto-associated'.  We may call a learned (auto-associated) pattern an engram.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Research conducted in the laboratory of Nobel laureate <a href="/wiki/Eric_Kandel" title="Eric Kandel">Eric Kandel</a> has provided evidence supporting the role of Hebbian learning mechanisms at synapses in the marine <a href="/wiki/Gastropoda" title="Gastropoda">gastropod</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Aplysia_californica" class="mw-redirect" title="Aplysia californica">Aplysia californica</a></i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Because synapses in the <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system" title="Peripheral nervous system">peripheral nervous system</a> of marine invertebrates are much easier to control in experiments, Kandel's research found that Hebbian <a href="/wiki/Long-term_potentiation" title="Long-term potentiation">long-term potentiation</a> along with activity-dependent presynaptic facilitation are both necessary for <a href="/wiki/Synaptic_plasticity" title="Synaptic plasticity">synaptic plasticity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Classical_conditioning" title="Classical conditioning">classical conditioning</a> in <i>Aplysia californica</i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>While research on invertebrates has established fundamental mechanisms of learning and memory, much of the work on long-lasting synaptic changes between vertebrate neurons involves the use of non-physiological experimental stimulation of brain cells. However, some of the physiologically relevant synapse modification mechanisms that have been studied in vertebrate brains do seem to be examples of Hebbian processes. One such review indicates that long-lasting changes in synaptic strengths can be induced by physiologically relevant synaptic activity using both Hebbian and non-Hebbian mechanisms.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Principles">Principles</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebbian_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Principles"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Artificial_neuron" title="Artificial neuron">artificial neurons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Artificial_neural_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial neural network">artificial neural networks</a>, Hebb's principle can be described as a method of determining how to alter the weights between model neurons. The weight between two neurons increases if the two neurons activate simultaneously, and reduces if they activate separately. Nodes that tend to be either both positive or both negative at the same time have strong positive weights, while those that tend to be opposite have strong negative weights.

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Hebbian_learning_and_mirror_neurons">Hebbian learning and mirror neurons</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebbian_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hebbian learning and mirror neurons"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Hebbian learning and spike-timing-dependent plasticity have been used in an influential theory of how <a href="/wiki/Mirror_neuron" title="Mirror neuron">mirror neurons</a> emerge.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Mirror neurons are neurons that fire both when an individual performs an action and when the individual sees or hears another perform a similar action.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The discovery of these neurons has been very influential in explaining how individuals make sense of the actions of others, since when a person perceives the actions of others, motor programs in the person's brain which they would use to perform similar actions are activated, which add information to the perception and help to predict what the person will do next based on the perceiver's own motor program. One limitation of this idea of mirror neuron functions is explaining how individuals develop neurons that respond both while performing an action and while hearing or seeing another perform similar actions.
</p><p>Neuroscientist <a href="/wiki/Christian_Keysers" title="Christian Keysers">Christian Keysers</a> and psychologist <a href="/wiki/David_Perrett" title="David Perrett">David Perrett</a> suggested that observing or hearing an individual perform an action activates brain regions as if performing the action oneself.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These re-afferent sensory signals trigger activity in neurons responding to the sight, sound, and feel of the action. Because the activity of these sensory neurons will consistently overlap in time with those of the motor neurons that caused the action, Hebbian learning predicts that the synapses connecting neurons responding to the sight, sound, and feel of an action and those of the neurons triggering the action should be potentiated. The same is true while people look at themselves in the mirror, hear themselves babble, or are imitated by others. After repeated occurrences of this re-afference, the synapses connecting the sensory and motor representations of an action are so strong that the motor neurons start firing to the sound or the vision of the action, and a mirror neuron is created.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Numerous experiments provide evidence for the idea that Hebbian learning is crucial to the formation of mirror neurons. Evidence reveals that motor programs can be triggered by novel auditory or visual stimuli after repeated pairing of the stimulus with the execution of the motor program.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> For instance, people who have never played the piano do not activate brain regions involved in playing the piano when listening to piano music. Five hours of piano lessons, in which the participant is exposed to the sound of the piano each time they press a key is proven sufficient to trigger activity in motor regions of the brain upon listening to piano music when heard at a later time.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Consistent with the fact that spike-timing-dependent plasticity occurs only if the presynaptic neuron's firing predicts the post-synaptic neuron's firing,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> the link between sensory stimuli and motor programs also only seem to be potentiated if the stimulus is contingent on the motor program.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Hebbian_theory_and_cognitive_neuroscience">Hebbian theory and cognitive neuroscience</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebbian_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hebbian theory and cognitive neuroscience"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Hebbian learning is linked to cognitive processes like decision-making and social learning. The field of cognitive neuroscience has started to explore the intersection of Hebbian theory with brain regions responsible for reward processing and social cognition, such as the striatum and prefrontal cortex.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In particular, striatal projections exposed to Hebbian models exhibit long-term potentiation and long-term depression <i>in vivo</i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Additionally, models of the prefrontal cortex to stimuli ("mixed selectivity") are not entirely explained by random connectivity, but when a Hebbian paradigm is incorporated, the levels of mixed selectivity in the model are reached.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is hypothesized (e.g., by Peter Putnam and <a href="/wiki/Robert_W._Fuller" title="Robert W. Fuller">Robert W. Fuller</a>) that Hebbian plasticity in these areas may underlie behaviors like habit formation, reinforcement learning, and even the development of social bonds.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Gefter&#95;2025&#95;26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gefter_2025-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Putnam&#95;Fuller&#95;1964&#95;27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Putnam_Fuller_1964-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Limitations">Limitations</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebbian_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Limitations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Despite the common use of Hebbian models for long-term potentiation, Hebbian theory does not cover all forms of long-term synaptic plasticity. Hebb did not propose any rules for inhibitory synapses or predictions for anti-causal spike sequences (where the presynaptic neuron fires <i>after</i> the postsynaptic neuron). Synaptic modification may not simply occur only between activated neurons A and B, but at neighboring synapses as well.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Therefore, all forms of <a href="/wiki/Heterosynaptic_plasticity" title="Heterosynaptic plasticity">heterosynaptic plasticity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Homeostatic_plasticity" title="Homeostatic plasticity">homeostatic plasticity</a> are considered non-Hebbian. One example is <a href="/wiki/Retrograde_signaling" title="Retrograde signaling">retrograde signaling</a> to presynaptic terminals.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The compound most frequently recognized as a retrograde transmitter is <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>, which, due to its high solubility and diffusivity, often exerts effects on nearby neurons.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This type of diffuse synaptic modification, known as volume learning, is not included in the traditional Hebbian model.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Contemporary_developments,_artificial_intelligence,_and_computational_advancements"><span id="Contemporary_developments.2C_artificial_intelligence.2C_and_computational_advancements"></span>Contemporary developments, artificial intelligence, and computational advancements</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebbian_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Contemporary developments, artificial intelligence, and computational advancements"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Modern research has expanded upon Hebb's original ideas. <a href="/wiki/Spike-timing-dependent_plasticity" title="Spike-timing-dependent plasticity">Spike-timing-dependent plasticity</a> (STDP), for example, refines Hebbian principles by incorporating the precise timing of neuronal spikes to Hebbian theory. Experimental advancements have also linked Hebbian learning to complex behaviors, such as decision-making and emotional regulation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:3&#95;13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Current studies in <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> (AI) and quantum computing continue to leverage Hebbian concepts for developing adaptive algorithms and improving machine learning models.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In AI, Hebbian learning has seen applications beyond traditional neural networks. One significant advancement is in reinforcement learning algorithms, where Hebbian-like learning is used to update the weights based on the timing and strength of stimuli during training phases. Some researchers have adapted Hebbian principles to develop more biologically plausible models for learning in artificial systems, which may improve model efficiency and convergence in AI applications.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A growing area of interest is the application of Hebbian learning in quantum computing. While classical neural networks are the primary area of application for Hebbian theory, recent studies have begun exploring the potential for quantum-inspired algorithms. These algorithms leverage the principles of quantum superposition and entanglement to enhance learning processes in quantum systems.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Current research is exploring how Hebbian principles could inform the development of more efficient quantum machine learning models.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:2&#95;3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>New computational models have emerged that refine or extend Hebbian learning. For example, some models now account for the precise timing of neural spikes (as in spike-timing-dependent plasticity), while others have integrated aspects of neuromodulation to account for how neurotransmitters like dopamine affect the strength of synaptic connections. These advanced models provide a more nuanced understanding of how Hebbian learning operates in the brain and are contributing to the development of more realistic computational models.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Recent research on Hebbian learning has focused on the role of inhibitory neurons, which are often overlooked in traditional Hebbian models. While classic Hebbian theory primarily focuses on excitatory neurons, more comprehensive models of neural learning now consider the balanced interaction between excitatory and inhibitory synapses. Studies suggest that inhibitory neurons can provide critical regulation for maintaining stability in neural circuits and might prevent runaway positive feedback in Hebbian learning.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, Jeff Magee and colleagues identified <i>behavioral timescale synaptic plasticity</i> (BTSP), a form of learning in hippocampal CA1 neurons in which synaptic inputs active several seconds before or after a dendritic plateau potential are strengthened, even without coincident postsynaptic spiking.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bittner2017&#95;40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bittner2017-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This mechanism operates on a much longer timescale than traditional Hebbian or spike-timing-dependent plasticity and provides a means for linking events separated in time during behavior.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bittner2017&#95;40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bittner2017-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> BTSP has been proposed as a modern framework for understanding how Hebbian-like associative processes can occur over behavioral timescales, suggesting that the timing window for synaptic modification may extend far beyond the millisecond range described in classical Hebbian learning.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Grienberger2020&#95;41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grienberger2020-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
