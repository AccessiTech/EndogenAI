<p><b>Synaptic pruning</b> is the process of <a href="/wiki/Synapse" title="Synapse">synapse</a> elimination or weakening.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:3&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Though it occurs throughout the lifespan of a mammal, the most active period of synaptic pruning in the <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_nervous_system" title="Development of the nervous system">development of the nervous system</a> occurs between early childhood and the onset of puberty in many <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Chechik&#95;1998&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chechik_1998-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Pruning starts near the time of birth and continues into one's late 20s.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During elimination of a synapse, the <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">axon</a> withdraws<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or dies off, and the <a href="/wiki/Dendrite" title="Dendrite">dendrite</a> decays and dies off. Synaptic pruning was traditionally considered to be complete by the time of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_maturation" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexual maturation">sexual maturation</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a> studies have discounted this idea.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Iglesias&#95;1&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Iglesias_1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The infant brain will increase in size by a factor of up to 5 by adulthood.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Azevedo&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Azevedo-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Two factors contribute to this growth: the growth of synaptic connections between neurons and the <a href="/wiki/Myelination" title="Myelination">myelination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nerve_fibers" class="mw-redirect" title="Nerve fibers">nerve fibers</a>. The total number of neurons, however, remains approximately the same, containing approximately 86 (± 8) billion <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Azevedo&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Azevedo-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After adolescence, the volume of the synaptic connections decreases again due to synaptic pruning.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-craik&#95;1&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-craik_1-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Pruning is influenced by environmental factors. For instance, if the eyes are sewn shut in the critical period when synaptic pruning of the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> takes place, the lack of input of light will cause the synaptic connections required for vision to die off, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Visual_impairment" title="Visual impairment">blindness</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:3&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Variations">Variations</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synaptic_pruning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Regulatory_pruning">Regulatory pruning</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synaptic_pruning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Regulatory pruning"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>At birth, the neurons in the visual and motor cortices have connections to the <a href="/wiki/Superior_colliculus" title="Superior colliculus">superior colliculus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pons" title="Pons">pons</a>. The neurons in each cortex are selectively pruned, leaving connections that are made with the functionally appropriate processing centers. Therefore, the neurons in the <a href="/wiki/Visual_cortex" title="Visual cortex">visual cortex</a> prune the synapses with neurons in the spinal cord, and the <a href="/wiki/Motor_cortex" title="Motor cortex">motor cortex</a> severs connections with the superior colliculus. This variation of pruning is known as large-scaled stereotyped axon pruning. Neurons send long axon branches to appropriate and inappropriate target areas, and the inappropriate connections are eventually pruned away.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-vander&#95;1&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vander_1-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Regressive events refine the abundance of connections, seen in <a href="/wiki/Neurogenesis" title="Neurogenesis">neurogenesis</a>, to create a specific and mature circuitry. <a href="/wiki/Apoptosis" title="Apoptosis">Apoptosis</a> and pruning are the two main methods of severing the undesired connections. In apoptosis, the neuron is killed and all connections associated with the neuron are also eliminated. In contrast, the neuron does not die in pruning, but requires the retraction of <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">axons</a> from synaptic connections that are not functionally appropriate.
</p><p>It is believed that the purpose of synaptic pruning is to remove unnecessary neuronal structures from the brain; as the human brain develops, the need to understand more complex structures becomes much more pertinent, and simpler associations formed at childhood are thought to be replaced by complex structures.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-chechik&#95;1&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chechik_1-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the fact it has several connotations with regulation of cognitive childhood development, pruning is thought to be a process of removing neurons which may have become damaged or degraded in order to further improve the "networking" capacity of a particular area of the brain.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-chechik&#95;1&#95;12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chechik_1-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Furthermore, it has been stipulated that the mechanism not only works in regard to development and reparation, but also as a means of continually maintaining more efficient brain function by removing neurons by their synaptic efficiency.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-chechik&#95;1&#95;12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chechik_1-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Pruning_in_the_maturing_brain">Pruning in the maturing brain</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synaptic_pruning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pruning in the maturing brain"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The pruning that is associated with learning is known as small-scale axon terminal arbor pruning. Axons extend short axon terminal arbors toward neurons within a target area. Certain terminal arbors are pruned by competition. The selection of the pruned terminal arbors follow the "use it or lose it" principle seen in <a href="/wiki/Synaptic_plasticity" title="Synaptic plasticity">synaptic plasticity</a>. This means synapses that are frequently used have strong connections while the rarely used synapses are eliminated. Examples seen in vertebrate include pruning of axon terminals in the <a href="/wiki/Neuromuscular_junction" title="Neuromuscular junction">neuromuscular junction</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system" title="Peripheral nervous system">peripheral nervous system</a> and the pruning of <a href="/wiki/Climbing_fiber" title="Climbing fiber">climbing fiber</a> inputs to the cerebellum in the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-vander&#95;1&#95;11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vander_1-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In terms of humans, synaptic pruning has been observed through the inference of differences in the estimated numbers of <a href="/wiki/Glial_cell" class="mw-redirect" title="Glial cell">glial cells</a> and neurons between children and adults, which differs greatly in the <a href="/wiki/Medial_dorsal_nucleus" title="Medial dorsal nucleus">mediodorsal thalamic nucleus</a>.
</p><p>In a study conducted in 2007 by <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a>, researchers compared 8 newborn human brains with those of 8 adults using estimates based upon size and evidence gathered from <a href="/wiki/Stereology" title="Stereology">stereological</a> <a href="/wiki/Fractionator" class="mw-redirect" title="Fractionator">fractionation</a>. They showed that, on average, estimates of adult neuron populations were 41% lower than those of the newborns in the region they measured, the mediodorsal thalamic nucleus.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-abitz&#95;p1&#95;13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abitz_p1-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>However, in terms of glial cells, adults had far larger estimates than those in newborns; 36.3 million on average in adult brains, compared to 10.6 million in the newborn samples.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-abitz&#95;p1&#95;13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abitz_p1-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The structure of the brain is thought to change when <a href="/wiki/Degenerative_disease" title="Degenerative disease">degeneration</a> and deafferentation occur in postnatal situations, although these phenomena have not been observed in some studies.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-abitz&#95;p1&#95;13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abitz_p1-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the case of development, neurons which are in the process of loss via programmed cell death are unlikely to be re-used, but rather replaced by new neuronal structures or synaptic structures, and have been found to occur alongside the structural change in the sub-cortical <a href="/wiki/Gray_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Gray matter">gray matter</a>.
</p><p>Synaptic pruning is classified separately from the regressive events seen during older ages. While developmental pruning is experience dependent, the deteriorating connections that are synonymous with old age are not. The stereotyped pruning can be compared to the process of chiseling and molding of stone into a statue. Once the statue is complete, the weather will begin to erode the statue and this represents the experience-independent deletion of connections.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Forgetting_problems_with_learning_through_pruning">Forgetting problems with learning through pruning</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synaptic_pruning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Forgetting problems with learning through pruning"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>All attempts to construct <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> systems that learn by pruning connections that are disused have the problem that every time they learn something new, they <a href="/wiki/Catastrophic_interference" title="Catastrophic interference">forget everything they learned before</a>. Since biological brains follow the same laws of physics as artificial intelligences, as all physical objects do, these researchers argue that if biological brains learned by pruning they would face the same catastrophic forgetting issues. This is pointed out as an especially severe problem if the learning is supposed to be part of a developmental process since retention of older knowledge is necessary for developmental types of learning, and as such it is argued that synaptic pruning cannot be a mechanism of mental development. It is argued that developmental types of learning must use other mechanisms that do not rely on synaptic pruning.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Energy_saving_for_reproduction_and_discontinuous_differences">Energy saving for reproduction and discontinuous differences</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synaptic_pruning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Energy saving for reproduction and discontinuous differences"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>One theory of why many brains are synaptically pruned when a human or other primate grows up is that maintenance of synapses consume nutrients which may be needed elsewhere in the body during growth and sexual maturation. Citing that most of the nutrient costs in the brain are in maintaining the brain cells and their synapses, rather than the firing itself, this theory explains the observation that some brains appear to continue pruning years after sexual maturation as a result of some brains having more robust synapses, allowing them to take years of neglect before the synaptic spines finally disintegrate.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Mechanisms">Mechanisms</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synaptic_pruning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mechanisms"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The three models explaining synaptic pruning are axon degeneration, axon retraction, and axon shedding.  In all cases, the <a href="/wiki/Synapses" class="mw-redirect" title="Synapses">synapses</a> are formed by a transient <a href="/wiki/Axon_terminal" title="Axon terminal">axon terminal</a>, and synapse elimination is caused by the axon pruning. Each model offers a different method in which the axon is removed to delete the synapse. In small-scale axon arbor pruning, patterns of neural activity help determine which synapses are maintained and which are removed, and recent work has linked this activity-dependent pruning to specific signaling pathways involving glial cells and the immune system.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hormones" class="mw-redirect" title="Hormones">Hormones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trophic_factor" class="mw-redirect" title="Trophic factor">trophic factors</a> are thought to be the main extrinsic factors regulating large-scale stereotyped axon pruning.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-vander&#95;1&#95;11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vander_1-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Axon_degeneration">Axon degeneration</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synaptic_pruning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Axon degeneration"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Drosophila" title="Drosophila">Drosophila</a></i>, there are extensive changes made to the nervous system during <a href="/wiki/Metamorphosis" title="Metamorphosis">metamorphosis</a>. Metamorphosis is triggered by <a href="/wiki/Ecdysone" title="Ecdysone">ecdysone</a>, and during this period, extensive pruning and reorganization of the neural network occurs. Therefore, it is theorized that pruning in <i>Drosophila</i> is triggered by the activation of ecdysone receptors. <a href="/wiki/Denervation" title="Denervation">Denervation</a> studies at the neuromuscular junction of vertebrates have shown that the axon removal mechanism closely resembles <a href="/wiki/Wallerian_degeneration" title="Wallerian degeneration">Wallerian degeneration</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-low&#95;1&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-low_1-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, the global and simultaneous pruning seen in <i>Drosophilia</i> differs from the mammalian nervous system pruning, which occurs locally and over multiple stages of development.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-vander&#95;1&#95;11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vander_1-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Axon_retraction">Axon retraction</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synaptic_pruning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Axon retraction"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Axon branches retract in a <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Proximal_and_distal" title="Anatomical terms of location">distal</a> to <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Proximal_and_distal" title="Anatomical terms of location">proximal</a> manner. The axonal contents that are retracted are thought to be recycled to other parts of the axon. The biological mechanism with which axonal pruning occurs still remains unclear for the mammalian central nervous system. However, pruning has been associated with guidance molecules in mice. Guidance molecules serve to control axon pathfinding through repulsion, and also initiate pruning of exuberant synaptic connections. <a href="/wiki/Semaphorin" title="Semaphorin">Semaphorin</a> <a href="/wiki/Ligands" class="mw-redirect" title="Ligands">ligands</a> and the receptors <a href="/wiki/Neuropilins" class="mw-redirect" title="Neuropilins">neuropilins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plexins" class="mw-redirect" title="Plexins">plexins</a> are used to induce retraction of the axons to initiate hippocampo-septal and infrapyramidal bundle (IPB) pruning. Stereotyped pruning of the hippocampal projections have been found to be significantly impaired in mice that have a Plexin-A3 defect.  Specifically, axons that are connected to a transient target will retract once the Plexin-A3 receptors are activated by class 3 semaphorin ligands.  In IPB, the expression of mRNA for Sema3F is present in the <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a> prenatally, lost postnatally and returns in the <a href="/wiki/Stratum_oriens" class="mw-redirect" title="Stratum oriens">stratum oriens</a>. Coincidentally, onset IPB pruning occurs around the same time. In the case of the hippocampal-septal projections, expression of mRNA for Sema3A was followed by the initiation of pruning after 3 days. This suggests that pruning is triggered once the ligand reaches threshold protein levels within a few days after detectable <a href="/wiki/MRNA" class="mw-redirect" title="MRNA">mRNA</a> expression.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-bagri&#95;1&#95;18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bagri_1-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Pruning of axons along the visual <a href="/wiki/Corticospinal_tract" title="Corticospinal tract">corticospinal tract</a>  (CST) is defective in neuropilin-2 mutants and plexin-A3 and plexin-A4 double mutant mice.  Sema3F is also expressed in the dorsal spinal cord during the pruning process. There is no motor CST pruning defect observed in these mutants.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-vander&#95;1&#95;11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vander_1-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Stereotyped pruning has also been observed in the tailoring of overextended axon branches from the <a href="/wiki/Retinotopy" title="Retinotopy">retinotopy</a> formation. <a href="/wiki/Ephrin" title="Ephrin">Ephrin</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ephrin_receptor" title="Ephrin receptor">ephrin receptors</a>, Eph, have been found to regulate and direct retinal axon branches. Forward signaling between ephrin-A and EphA, along the <a href="/wiki/Anterior" class="mw-redirect" title="Anterior">anterior</a>-<a href="/wiki/Posterior_(anatomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Posterior (anatomy)">posterior</a> axis, has been found to inhibit retinal axon branch formation posterior to a terminal zone. The forward signaling also promotes pruning of the axons that have reached into the terminal zone. However, it remains unclear whether the retraction mechanism seen in IPB pruning is applied in retinal axons.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Luo&#95;1&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luo_1-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Reverse signaling between ephrin-B proteins and their Eph <a href="/wiki/Receptor_tyrosine_kinases" class="mw-redirect" title="Receptor tyrosine kinases">receptor tyrosine kinases</a> have been found to initiate the retraction mechanism in the IPB. Ephrin-B3 is observed to transduce tyrosine phosphorylation-dependent reverse signals into hippocampal axons that trigger pruning of excessive IPB fibers. The proposed pathway involves EphB being expressed on the surface of target cells that results in tyrosine phosphorylation of ephrin-B3.  Ensuing binding of ephrin-B3 to the cytoplasmic adaptor protein, Grb4, leads to the recruitment and binding of <a href="/wiki/Dock180" class="mw-redirect" title="Dock180">Dock180</a> and <a href="/wiki/P21_activated_kinases" class="mw-redirect" title="P21 activated kinases">p21 activated kinases</a> (PAK). The binding of Dock180 increases Rac-GTP levels, and PAK mediates the downstream signaling of active <a href="/wiki/Rac_(GTPase)" title="Rac (GTPase)">Rac</a> that leads to the retraction of the axon and eventual pruning.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-xu&#95;1&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xu_1-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Axon_shedding">Axon shedding</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synaptic_pruning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Axon shedding"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Time-lapse imaging of retreating axons in <a href="/wiki/Neuromuscular_junctions" class="mw-redirect" title="Neuromuscular junctions">neuromuscular junctions</a> of mice have shown axonal shedding as a possible mechanism of pruning. The retreating axon moved in a <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Proximal_and_distal" title="Anatomical terms of location">distal</a> to <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Proximal_and_distal" title="Anatomical terms of location">proximal</a> order and resembled retraction. However, there were many cases in which remnants were shed as the axons were retracting. The remnants, named axosomes, contained the same organelles seen in the bulbs attached to the end of axons and were commonly found around the proximity of the bulbs. This indicates that axosomes are derived from the bulbs. Furthermore, axosomes did not have electron-dense <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasms</a> or disrupted <a href="/wiki/Mitochondria" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> indicating that they were not formed through Wallerian degeneration.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-bishop&#95;1&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bishop_1-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Potential_role_in_schizophrenia">Potential role in schizophrenia</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synaptic_pruning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Potential role in schizophrenia"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Synaptic pruning has been suggested to have a role in the pathology of neurodevelopmental disorders such as <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a>, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Autism-spectrum_disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Autism-spectrum disorder">autism spectrum disorder</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Microglia" title="Microglia">Microglia</a> have been implicated in synaptic pruning, as they have roles in both the immune response as <a href="/wiki/Macrophage" title="Macrophage">macrophages</a> as well as in neuronal upkeep and <a href="/wiki/Synaptic_plasticity" title="Synaptic plasticity">synaptic plasticity</a> in the CNS during fetal development, early postnatal development, and adolescence, in which they engulf unneeded or redundant synapses via <a href="/wiki/Phagocytosis" title="Phagocytosis">phagocytosis</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Microglial synapse engulfment and uptake has been specifically observed to be upregulated in the isolated <a href="/wiki/Synaptosome" title="Synaptosome">synaptosomes</a> of male patients with schizophrenia compared to healthy controls, suggesting upregulated microglia-induced synaptic pruning in these individuals. Microglia-mediated synaptic pruning has also been observed to be upregulated during late adolescence and early adulthood, which could also account for the age of onset for schizophrenia often being reported around this time in development (late teens to early 20s for men, and mid-to-late 20s for women)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:2&#95;24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The drug minocycline, a semisynthetic brain-penetrant tetracycline antibiotic, has been found to somewhat reverse these changes made to patient synaptosomes by downregulating synaptic pruning.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:2&#95;24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Genes in the <a href="/wiki/Complement_component_4" title="Complement component 4">Complement Component 4</a> (C4) locus of the <a href="/wiki/Major_histocompatibility_complex" title="Major histocompatibility complex">major histocompatibility complex</a> (MHC), which encode for <a href="/wiki/Complement_factors" class="mw-redirect" title="Complement factors">complement factors</a>, have also been tied to schizophrenia risk through <a href="/wiki/Gene_linkage_studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Gene linkage studies">gene linkage studies</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:2&#95;24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The fact that some of these complement factors are involved in signaling during synaptic pruning also seems to suggest that schizophrenia risk may be linked to synaptic pruning.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Specifically, complement factors C1q and C3 have been found to have a role in microglia-mediated synaptic pruning.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Carriers of C4 risk variants have also been found to be tied to this kind of synapse overpruning in microglia.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:2&#95;24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Experimental studies in mice have shown that microglia, the resident immune cells of the central nervous system, actively engulf synaptic material during normal postnatal development and are required for the elimination of excess synapses. Disrupting microglial function in these models leads to abnormal numbers of synapses and altered circuit maturation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In autism spectrum disorder, postmortem analyses and animal models suggest that reduced synaptic pruning may contribute to atypical brain connectivity. Children and mice with autism-associated mutations can show increased numbers of dendritic spines, the tiny protrusions that receive most excitatory synapses, along with impaired elimination of these spines during development; these changes have been linked to hyperactivation of the mTOR pathway and defective autophagy in neurons.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
