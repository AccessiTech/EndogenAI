<p>In <a href="/wiki/Human_brains" class="mw-redirect" title="Human brains">human brains</a>, the <b>anterior cingulate cortex</b> (<b>ACC</b>) is the frontal part of the <a href="/wiki/Cingulate_cortex" title="Cingulate cortex">cingulate cortex</a> that resembles a "collar" surrounding the frontal part of the <a href="/wiki/Corpus_callosum" title="Corpus callosum">corpus callosum</a>. It consists of <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area" title="Brodmann area">Brodmann areas</a> <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area_24" title="Brodmann area 24">24</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area_32" title="Brodmann area 32">32</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area_33" title="Brodmann area 33">33</a>.
</p><p>It is involved in certain higher-level functions, such as <a href="/wiki/Attention" title="Attention">attention</a> allocation,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pavlovian_conditioning" class="mw-redirect" title="Pavlovian conditioning">reward anticipation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decision-making" title="Decision-making">decision-making</a>, impulse control (e.g. performance monitoring and error detection),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotion</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Anatomy">Anatomy</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Anatomy"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Anterior_cingulate_gyrus_animation.gif" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Anterior_cingulate_gyrus_animation.gif/250px-Anterior_cingulate_gyrus_animation.gif" decoding="async" width="250" height="250" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6a/Anterior_cingulate_gyrus_animation.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="300" /></a><figcaption>Anterior cingulate gyrus of left cerebral hemisphere, shown in red</figcaption></figure>
<p>The anterior cingulate cortex can be divided anatomically based on cognitive (<a href="/wiki/Dorsum_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dorsum (biology)">dorsal</a>), and emotional (<a href="/wiki/Ventral" class="mw-redirect" title="Ventral">ventral</a>) components.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bush00&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush00-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The dorsal part of the ACC is connected with the <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">prefrontal cortex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parietal_cortex" class="mw-redirect" title="Parietal cortex">parietal cortex</a>, as well as the motor system and the <a href="/wiki/Frontal_eye_fields" title="Frontal eye fields">frontal eye fields</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Posner98&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Posner98-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> making it a central station for processing <a href="/wiki/Top-down_and_bottom-up_design" class="mw-redirect" title="Top-down and bottom-up design">top-down</a> and <a href="/wiki/Top-down_and_bottom-up_design" class="mw-redirect" title="Top-down and bottom-up design">bottom-up</a> stimuli and assigning appropriate control to other areas in the brain. By contrast, the ventral part of the ACC is connected with the <a href="/wiki/Amygdala" title="Amygdala">amygdala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nucleus_accumbens" title="Nucleus accumbens">nucleus accumbens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a>, and anterior <a href="/wiki/Insular_cortex" title="Insular cortex">insula</a>, and is involved in assessing the salience of emotion and motivational information. The ACC seems to be especially involved when effort is needed to carry out a task, such as in early learning and problem-solving.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>On a cellular level, the ACC is unique in its abundance of specialized neurons called <a href="/wiki/Spindle_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Spindle cells">spindle cells</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Von_Economo_neuron" title="Von Economo neuron">von Economo neurons</a>. These cells are a relatively recent occurrence in evolutionary terms (found only in humans and other <a href="/wiki/Primates" class="mw-redirect" title="Primates">primates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cetaceans" class="mw-redirect" title="Cetaceans">cetaceans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Elephants" class="mw-redirect" title="Elephants">elephants</a>) and contribute to this brain region's emphasis on addressing difficult problems, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Spindle_neuron#Related_pathologies" class="mw-redirect" title="Spindle neuron">pathologies</a> related to the ACC.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Tasks">Tasks</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tasks"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A typical task that activates the ACC involves eliciting some form of conflict within the participant that can potentially result in an error. One such task is called the <a href="/wiki/Eriksen_flanker_task" title="Eriksen flanker task">Eriksen flanker task</a> and consists of an arrow pointing to the left or right, which is flanked by two distractor arrows creating either compatible (&lt;&lt;&lt;&lt;&lt;) or incompatible (&gt;&gt;&lt;&gt;&gt;) trials.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Botvin99&#95;10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Botvin99-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Another very common conflict-inducing stimulus that activates the ACC is the <a href="/wiki/Stroop_task" class="mw-redirect" title="Stroop task">Stroop task</a>, which involves naming the color ink of words that are either congruent (<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1239334494">@media screen{html.skin-theme-clientpref-night .mw-parser-output div:not(.notheme)>.tmp-color,html.skin-theme-clientpref-night .mw-parser-output p>.tmp-color,html.skin-theme-clientpref-night .mw-parser-output table:not(.notheme) .tmp-color{color:inherit!important}}@media screen and (prefers-color-scheme:dark){html.skin-theme-clientpref-os .mw-parser-output div:not(.notheme)>.tmp-color,html.skin-theme-clientpref-os .mw-parser-output p>.tmp-color,html.skin-theme-clientpref-os .mw-parser-output table:not(.notheme) .tmp-color{color:inherit!important}}</style><span class="tmp-color" style="color:#ff0000">RED</span> written in red) or incongruent (<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1239334494" /><span class="tmp-color" style="color:#0000ff">RED</span> written in blue).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Pardo90&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pardo90-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Conflict occurs because people's reading abilities interfere with their attempt to correctly name the word's ink color. A variation of this task is the <i>Counting-Stroop</i>, during which people count either neutral stimuli ('dog' presented four times) or interfering stimuli ('three' presented four times) by pressing a button. Another version of the <i>Stroop task</i> named the <i>Emotional Counting Stroop</i> is identical to the <i>Counting Stroop</i> test, except that it also uses segmented or repeated emotional words such as "murder" during the interference part of the task. Thus, ACC affects decision making of a task.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Functions">Functions</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Functions"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Many studies attribute specific functions such as <a href="/wiki/Error_detection" class="mw-redirect" title="Error detection">error detection</a>, anticipation of tasks, <a href="/wiki/Attention" title="Attention">attention</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Pardo90&#95;11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pardo90-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Motivation" title="Motivation">motivation</a>, and modulation of emotional responses to the ACC.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bush00&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush00-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Posner98&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Posner98-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Nieuw01&#95;13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nieuw01-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Error_detection_and_conflict_monitoring">Error detection and conflict monitoring</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Error detection and conflict monitoring"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The most basic form of ACC theory states that the ACC is involved with <a href="/wiki/Error_detection" class="mw-redirect" title="Error detection">error detection</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bush00&#95;5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush00-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Evidence has been derived from studies involving a <a href="/wiki/Stroop_task" class="mw-redirect" title="Stroop task">Stroop task</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Posner98&#95;6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Posner98-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, ACC is also active during correct response, and this has been shown using a letter task, whereby participants had to respond to the letter X after an A was presented and ignore all other letter combinations with some letters more competitive than others.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> They found that for more competitive stimuli ACC activation was greater.
</p><p>A similar theory poses that the ACC's primary function is the monitoring of conflict. In <a href="/wiki/Eriksen_flanker_task" title="Eriksen flanker task">Eriksen flanker task</a>, incompatible trials produce the most conflict and the most activation by the ACC. Upon detection of a conflict, the ACC then provides cues to other areas in the brain to cope with the conflicting control systems.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Evidence_from_electrical_studies">Evidence from electrical studies</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evidence from electrical studies"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Evidence for ACC as having an error detection function comes from observations of <a href="/wiki/Error-related_negativity" title="Error-related negativity">error-related negativity</a> (ERN) uniquely generated within the ACC upon error occurrences.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bush00&#95;5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush00-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Holroyd04&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holroyd04-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Luu04&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luu04-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A distinction has been made between an <a href="/wiki/Event-related_potential" title="Event-related potential">ERP</a> following incorrect responses (response ERN) and a signal after subjects receive feedback after erroneous responses (feedback ERN).
</p><p>Patients with lateral prefrontal cingulate (PFC) damage show reduced ERNs.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Reinforcement learning ERN theory poses that there is a mismatch between actual response execution and appropriate response execution, which results in an ERN discharge.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bush00&#95;5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush00-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Holroyd04&#95;16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holroyd04-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Furthermore, this theory predicts that, when the ACC receives conflicting input from control areas in the brain, it determines and allocates which area should be given control over the motor system. Varying levels of dopamine are believed to influence the optimization of this filter system by providing expectations about the outcomes of an event. The ERN, then, serves as a beacon to highlight the violation of an expectation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Luu04&#95;17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luu04-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Research on the occurrence of the feedback ERN shows evidence that this potential has larger amplitudes when violations of expectancy are large. In other words, if an event is not likely to happen, the feedback ERN will be larger if no error is detected. Other studies have examined whether the ERN is elicited by varying the cost of an error and the evaluation of a response.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Holroyd04&#95;16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holroyd04-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In these trials, feedback is given about whether the participant has gained or lost money after a response. Amplitudes of ERN responses with small gains and small losses were similar. No ERN was elicited for any losses as opposed to an ERN for no wins, even though both outcomes are the same. The finding in this paradigm suggests that monitoring for wins and losses is based on the relative expected gains and losses. If you get a different outcome than expected, the ERN will be larger than for expected outcomes. ERN studies have also localized specific functions of the ACC.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Luu04&#95;17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luu04-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The rostral ACC seems to be active after an error commission, indicating an error response function, whereas the dorsal ACC is active after both an error and feedback, suggesting a more evaluative function (for fMRI evidence, see also<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bush02&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush02-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Polli&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Polli-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Taylor06&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor06-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> ). This evaluation is emotional in nature and highlights the amount of distress associated with a certain error.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bush00&#95;5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush00-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Summarizing the evidence found by ERN studies, it appears to be the case that ACC receives information about a stimulus, selects an appropriate response, monitors the action, and adapts behavior if there is a violation of expectancy.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Luu04&#95;17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luu04-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Social_evaluation">Social evaluation</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Social evaluation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Activity in the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex (dACC) has been implicated in processing both the detection and appraisal of social processes, including <a href="/wiki/Social_exclusion" title="Social exclusion">social exclusion</a>. When exposed to repeated personal social evaluative tasks, non-depressed women showed reduced <a href="/wiki/FMRI" class="mw-redirect" title="FMRI">fMRI</a> <a href="/wiki/Blood-oxygen-level_dependent_imaging" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood-oxygen-level dependent imaging">BOLD</a> activation in the dACC on the second exposure, while women with a history of <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a> exhibited enhanced BOLD activation. This differential activity may reflect enhanced rumination about social evaluation or enhanced arousal associated with repeated social evaluation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The anterior cingulate cortex gyrus is involved in effort to help others.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Reward-based_learning_theory">Reward-based learning theory</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reward-based learning theory"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A more comprehensive and recent theory describes the ACC as a more active component and poses that it detects and monitors errors, evaluates the degree of the error, and then suggests an appropriate form of action to be implemented by the motor system. Earlier evidence from electrical studies indicate the ACC has an evaluative component, which is indeed confirmed by <a href="/wiki/FMRI" class="mw-redirect" title="FMRI">fMRI</a> studies.
The dorsal and rostral areas of the ACC both seem to be affected by rewards and losses associated with errors. During one study, participants received monetary rewards and losses for correct and incorrect responses, respectively.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bush02&#95;19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush02-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Largest activation in the dACC was shown during loss trials. This stimulus did not elicit any errors, and, thus, error detection and monitoring theories cannot fully explain why this ACC activation would occur. The dorsal part of the ACC seems to play a key role in reward-based decision-making and learning. The rostral part of the ACC, on the other hand, is believed to be involved more with affective responses to errors. In an interesting expansion of the previously described experiment, the effects of rewards and costs on ACC's activation during error commission was examined.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Taylor06&#95;21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor06-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Participants performed a version of the <a href="/wiki/Eriksen_flanker_task" title="Eriksen flanker task">Eriksen flanker task</a> using a set of letters assigned to each response button instead of arrows.
</p><p>Targets were flanked by either a congruent or an incongruent set of letters. Using an image of a thumb (up, down, or neutral), participants received feedback on how much money they gained or lost. The researchers found greater rostral ACC activation when participants lost money during the trials. The participants reported being frustrated when making mistakes. Because the ACC is intricately involved with error detection and affective responses, it may very well be that this area forms the basis of self-confidence. Taken together, these findings indicate that both the dorsal and rostral areas are involved in evaluating the extent of the error and optimizing subsequent responses. A study confirming this notion explored the functions of both the dorsal and rostral areas of the ACC involved using a saccade task.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Polli&#95;20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Polli-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Participants were shown a cue that indicated whether they had to make either a pro-saccade or an anti-saccade. An anti-saccade requires suppression of a distracting cue because the target appears in the opposite location causing the conflict. Results showed differing activation for the rostral and dorsal ACC areas. Early correct anti-saccade performance was associated with rostral activation. The dorsal area, on the other hand, was activated when errors were committed, but also for correct responses.
</p><p>Whenever the dorsal area was active, fewer errors were committed providing more evidence that the ACC is involved with <a href="/wiki/Effortfulness" title="Effortfulness">effortful</a> performance. The second finding showed that, during error trials, the ACC activated later than for correct responses, clearly indicating a kind of evaluative function.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Role_in_consciousness">Role in consciousness</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Role in consciousness"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The ACC area in the brain is associated with many functions that are correlated with conscious experience. Greater ACC activation levels were present in more emotionally aware female participants when shown short 'emotional' video clips.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Better emotional awareness is associated with improved recognition of emotional cues or targets, which is reflected by ACC activation.
</p><p>The idea of awareness being associated with the ACC is supported by some evidence, in that it seems to be the case that, when subjects' responses are not congruent with actual responses, a larger <a href="/wiki/Error-related_negativity" title="Error-related negativity">error-related negativity</a> is produced.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Luu04&#95;17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luu04-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>One study found an ERN even when subjects were not aware of their error.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Luu04&#95;17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luu04-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Awareness may not be necessary to elicit an ERN, but it could influence the effect of the amplitude of the feedback ERN. Relating to the reward-based learning theory, awareness could modulate expectancy violations. Increased awareness could result in decreased violations of expectancies and decreased awareness could achieve the opposite effect. Further research is needed to completely understand the effects of awareness on ACC activation.
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Astonishing_Hypothesis" title="The Astonishing Hypothesis">The Astonishing Hypothesis</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Francis_Crick" title="Francis Crick">Francis Crick</a> identifies the anterior cingulate, to be specific the anterior cingulate sulcus, as a likely candidate for the center of <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">free will</a> in humans. Crick bases this suggestion on scans of patients with specific lesions that seem to interfere with their sense of independent will, such as <a href="/wiki/Alien_hand_syndrome" title="Alien hand syndrome">alien hand syndrome</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Role_in_registering_pain">Role in registering pain</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Role in registering pain"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The ACC registers physical pain as shown in functional MRI studies that showed an increase in signal intensity, typically in the posterior part of area 24 of the ACC, that was correlated with pain intensity. When this pain-related activation was accompanied by attention-demanding cognitive tasks (verbal fluency), the attention-demanding tasks increased signal intensity in a region of the ACC anterior and/or superior to the pain-related activation region.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The ACC is the cortical area that has been most frequently linked to the experience of pain.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It appears to be involved in the emotional reaction to pain rather than to the perception of pain itself.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Evidence from social neuroscience studies have suggested that, in addition to its role in physical pain, the ACC may also be involved in monitoring painful social situations as well, such as exclusion or rejection. When participants felt socially excluded in an fMRI virtual ball throwing game in which the ball was never thrown to the participant, the ACC showed activation. Further, this activation was correlated with a self-reported measure of social distress, indicating that the ACC may be involved in the detection and monitoring of social situations which may cause social/emotional pain, rather than just physical pain.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Pathology">Pathology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pathology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Studying the effects of damage to the ACC provides insights into the type of functions it serves in the intact brain. Behavior that is associated with lesions in the ACC includes: inability to detect errors, severe difficulty with resolving stimulus conflict in a <a href="/wiki/Stroop_task" class="mw-redirect" title="Stroop task">Stroop task</a>, emotional instability, inattention, and <a href="/wiki/Akinetic_mutism" title="Akinetic mutism">akinetic mutism</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bush00&#95;5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bush00-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Posner98&#95;6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Posner98-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There is evidence that damage to ACC is present in patients with <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a>, where studies have shown patients have difficulty in dealing with conflicting spatial locations in a Stroop-like task and having abnormal ERNs.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Posner98&#95;6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Posner98-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Holroyd04&#95;16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holroyd04-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Participants with <a href="/wiki/ADHD" class="mw-redirect" title="ADHD">ADHD</a> were found to have reduced activation in the dorsal area of the ACC when performing the <a href="/wiki/Stroop_task" class="mw-redirect" title="Stroop task">Stroop task</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Together, these findings corroborate results from imaging and electrical studies about the variety of functions attributed to the ACC.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="OCD">OCD</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: OCD"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>There is strong evidence that this area may have a role in <a href="/wiki/Obsessive%E2%80%93compulsive_disorder" title="Obsessive–compulsive disorder">obsessive–compulsive disorder</a>. A recent study from the University of Cambridge showed that participants with OCD had higher levels of <a href="/wiki/Glutamate_(neurotransmitter)" title="Glutamate (neurotransmitter)">glutamate</a> and lower levels of <a href="/wiki/%CE%93-Aminobutyric_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Γ-Aminobutyric acid">GABA</a> in the anterior cingulate cortex, compared to participants without OCD. They used magnetic resonance spectroscopy to assess the balance of excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmission by measuring glutamate and GABA levels in anterior cingulate cortex and supplementary motor area of healthy volunteers and participants with OCD. Participants with OCD had significantly higher levels of glutamate and lower levels of GABA in the ACC and a higher Glu:GABA ratio in that region.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Recent <a href="/wiki/Seed-based_d_mapping" title="Seed-based d mapping">SDM meta-analyses</a> of <a href="/wiki/Voxel-based_morphometry" title="Voxel-based morphometry">voxel-based morphometry</a> studies comparing people with OCD and healthy controls has found people with OCD to have increased <a href="/wiki/Grey_matter" title="Grey matter">grey matter</a> volumes in bilateral <a href="/wiki/Lenticular_nucleus" class="mw-redirect" title="Lenticular nucleus">lenticular nuclei</a>, extending to the <a href="/wiki/Caudate_nucleus" title="Caudate nucleus">caudate nuclei</a>, while decreased grey matter volumes in bilateral dorsal <a href="/wiki/Medial_frontal_gyrus" title="Medial frontal gyrus">medial frontal</a>/anterior cingulate cortex.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Radua&#95;and&#95;Mataix-Cols,&#95;2009&#95;32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radua_and_Mataix-Cols,_2009-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-radua2010&#95;33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-radua2010-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These findings contrast with those in people with other anxiety disorders, who evince decreased (rather than increased) <a href="/wiki/Grey_matter" title="Grey matter">grey matter</a> volumes in bilateral <a href="/wiki/Lenticular_nucleus" class="mw-redirect" title="Lenticular nucleus">lenticular</a> / <a href="/wiki/Caudate_nucleus" title="Caudate nucleus">caudate nuclei</a>, while also decreased grey matter volumes in bilateral dorsal <a href="/wiki/Medial_frontal_gyrus" title="Medial frontal gyrus">medial frontal</a> / anterior cingulate gyri.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-radua2010&#95;33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-radua2010-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Schizophrenia_spectrum_disorders">Schizophrenia spectrum disorders</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Schizophrenia spectrum disorders"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In individuals with <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a> spectrum disorders, the anterior cingulate cortex has been found to be smaller compared to that of control subjects.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Haznedar&#95;2004&#95;34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haznedar_2004-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Fujiwara&#95;2007&#95;35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fujiwara_2007-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
<a href="/wiki/Meta-analyses" class="mw-redirect" title="Meta-analyses">Meta-analyses</a> have shown that its activity is reduced during emotion processing,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and its <a href="/wiki/Functional_connectivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Functional connectivity">functional connectivity</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Striatum" title="Striatum">striatum</a> is diminished at rest, which has been linked to cognitive rigidity.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Anxiety">Anxiety</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Anxiety"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The ACC has been suggested to have possible links with <a href="/wiki/Social_anxiety" title="Social anxiety">social anxiety</a>, along with the amygdala part of the brain, but this research is still in its early stages.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A more recent study, by the Wake Forest Baptist Medical Centre, confirms the relationship between the ACC and anxiety regulation, by revealing mindfulness practice as a meditator for anxiety precisely through the ACC.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Depression">Depression</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Depression"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The adjacent <a href="/wiki/Subgenual_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Subgenual area">subcallosal cingulate gyrus</a> has been implicated in <a href="/wiki/Major_depression" class="mw-redirect" title="Major depression">major depression</a> and research indicates that <a href="/wiki/Deep-brain_stimulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Deep-brain stimulation">deep-brain stimulation</a> of the region could act to alleviate depressive symptoms.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Although people with depression had smaller subgenual ACCs,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> their ACCs were more active when adjusted for size.  This correlates well with increased subgenual ACC activity during sadness in healthy people,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and normalization of activity after successful treatment.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Of note, the activity of the subgenual cingulate cortex correlates with individual differences in negative affect during the baseline resting state; in other words, the greater the subgenual activity, the greater the negative affectivity in temperament.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Lead_exposure">Lead exposure</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Lead exposure"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A study of brain MRIs taken on adults that had previously participated in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cincinnati_Lead_Study&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cincinnati Lead Study (page does not exist)">Cincinnati Lead Study</a> found that people that had higher levels of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> exposure as children had decreased brain size as adults. This effect was most pronounced in the ACC (Cecil et al., 2008)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and is thought to relate to the cognitive and behavioral deficits of affected individuals.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Autism">Autism</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Autism"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Impairments in the development of the anterior cingulate, together with impairments in the dorsal medial-frontal cortex, may constitute a neural substrate for socio-cognitive deficits in <a href="/wiki/Autism_spectrum_disorders" class="mw-redirect" title="Autism spectrum disorders">autism</a>, such as social orienting and <a href="/wiki/Joint_attention" title="Joint attention">joint attention</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="PTSD">PTSD</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: PTSD"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>An increasing number of studies are investigating the role of the ACC in <a href="/wiki/Post-traumatic_stress_disorder" title="Post-traumatic stress disorder">post-traumatic stress disorder</a>. PTSD diagnosis and related symptoms such as skin conductance response (SCR) to "potentially startling sounds" were found to be correlated with reduced ACC volume.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Further, childhood trauma and executive dysfunction seem to correlate with reduced ACC connectivity to surrounding neural regions.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Silveira&#95;499–509&#95;48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silveira_499–509-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In a longitudinal study, this reduced connectivity was able to predict high-risk drinking (binge drinking at least once per week for the past 12 months) up to four years later.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Silveira&#95;499–509&#95;48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silveira_499–509-48"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>48<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="General_risk_of_psychopathology">General risk of psychopathology</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anterior_cingulate_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: General risk of psychopathology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A study on differences in brain structure of adults with high and low levels of <a href="/wiki/Metacognitive_therapy#Self-Regulatory_Executive_Function_model" title="Metacognitive therapy">cognitive-attentional syndrome</a> demonstrated diminished volume of the dorsal part of the ACC in the former group, indicating relationship between cortical thickness of ACC and general risk of <a href="/wiki/Psychopathology" title="Psychopathology">psychopathology</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>49<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
