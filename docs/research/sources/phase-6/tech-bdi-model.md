</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Model for designing artificial intelligence</div>
<p>The <b>belief–desire–intention software model</b> (<b>BDI</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Modeling_language" title="Modeling language">software model</a> developed for programming <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_agent" title="Intelligent agent">intelligent agents</a>. Superficially characterized by the implementation of an agent's <i>beliefs</i>, <i>desires</i> and <i>intentions</i>, it actually uses these concepts to solve a particular problem in agent programming. In essence, it provides a mechanism for separating the activity of selecting a plan (from a plan <a href="/wiki/Library_(computing)" title="Library (computing)">library</a> or an external planner application) from the execution of currently active plans. Consequently, BDI agents are able to balance the time spent on deliberating about plans (choosing what to do) and executing those plans (doing it). A third activity, creating the plans in the first place (<a href="/wiki/Automated_planning_and_scheduling" title="Automated planning and scheduling">planning</a>), is not within the scope of the model, and is left to the system designer and programmer.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Overview">Overview</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belief%E2%80%93desire%E2%80%93intention_software_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In order to achieve this separation, the BDI software model implements the principal aspects of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bratman" title="Michael Bratman">Michael Bratman</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Belief-Desire-Intention_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Belief-Desire-Intention model">theory of human practical reasoning</a> (also referred to as Belief-Desire-Intention, or BDI). That is to say, it implements the notions of belief, desire and (in particular) intention, in a manner inspired by Bratman. 
</p><p>For Bratman, desire and intention are both pro-attitudes (mental attitudes concerned with action). He identifies commitment as the distinguishing factor between desire and intention, noting that it leads to (1) temporal persistence in plans and (2) further plans being made on the basis of those to which it is already committed. The BDI software model partially addresses these issues. Temporal persistence, in the sense of explicit reference to time, is not explored. The hierarchical nature of plans is more easily implemented: a plan consists of a number of steps, some of which may invoke other plans. The hierarchical definition of plans itself implies a kind of temporal persistence, since the overarching plan remains in effect while subsidiary plans are being executed.
</p><p>An important aspect of the BDI software model (in terms of its research relevance) is the existence of logical models through which it is possible to define and reason about BDI agents. Research in this area has led, for example, to the <a href="/wiki/Axiomatic_system" title="Axiomatic system">axiomatization</a> of some BDI implementations, as well as to <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">formal logical</a> descriptions such as Anand Rao and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Georgeff" title="Michael Georgeff">Michael Georgeff</a>'s BDICTL. The latter combines a <a href="/wiki/Modal_logic" title="Modal logic">multiple-modal logic</a> (with modalities representing beliefs, desires and intentions) with the <a href="/wiki/Temporal_logic" title="Temporal logic">temporal logic</a> <a href="/wiki/Computational_tree_logic" class="mw-redirect" title="Computational tree logic">CTL*</a>. More recently, Michael Wooldridge has extended BDICTL to define LORA (the Logic Of Rational Agents), by incorporating an action logic. In principle, LORA allows reasoning not only about individual agents, but also about communication and other interaction in a <a href="/wiki/Multi-agent_system" title="Multi-agent system">multi-agent system</a>.
</p><p>The BDI software model is closely associated with intelligent agents, but does not, of itself, ensure all the characteristics associated with such agents. For example, it allows agents to have private beliefs, but does not force them to be private. It also has nothing to say about agent communication. Ultimately, the BDI software model is an attempt to solve a problem that has more to do with plans and planning (the choice and execution thereof) than it has to do with the programming of intelligent agents. This approach has recently been proposed by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Umbrello&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Steven Umbrello (page does not exist)">Steven Umbrello</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Yampolskiy" title="Roman Yampolskiy">Roman Yampolskiy</a> as a means of designing <a href="/wiki/Self-driving_car" title="Self-driving car">autonomous vehicles</a> for human values.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="BDI_agents">BDI agents</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belief%E2%80%93desire%E2%80%93intention_software_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: BDI agents"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A BDI agent is a particular type of <a href="/wiki/Bounded_rationality" title="Bounded rationality">bounded</a> <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_agent" title="Intelligent agent">rational software agent</a>, imbued with particular <i>mental attitudes</i>, viz: Beliefs, Desires and Intentions (BDI).
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Architecture">Architecture</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belief%E2%80%93desire%E2%80%93intention_software_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Architecture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>This section defines the idealized architectural components of a BDI system.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Beliefs</b>: Beliefs represent the informational state of the agent–its beliefs about the world (including itself and other agents). Beliefs can also include <a href="/wiki/Inference_rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Inference rule">inference rules</a>, allowing <a href="/wiki/Forward_chaining" title="Forward chaining">forward chaining</a> to lead to new beliefs. Using the term <i>belief</i> rather than <i>knowledge</i> recognizes that what an agent believes may not necessarily be true (and in fact may change in the future).
<ul><li><b>Beliefset</b>: Beliefs are stored in  <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">database</a> (sometimes called a <i>belief base</i> or a <i>belief set</i>), although that is an <a href="/wiki/Implementation" title="Implementation">implementation</a> decision.</li></ul></li>
<li><b>Desires</b>: Desires represent the motivational state of the agent. They represent objectives or situations that the agent <i>would like</i> to accomplish or bring about. Examples of desires might be: <i>find the best price</i>, <i>go to the party</i> or <i>become rich</i>.
<ul><li><b>Goals</b>: A goal is a desire that has been adopted for active pursuit by the agent. Usage of the term <i>goals</i> adds the further restriction that the set of active desires must be consistent. For example, one should not have concurrent goals to go to a party and to stay at home – even though they could both be desirable.</li></ul></li>
<li><b>Intentions</b>: Intentions represent the deliberative state of the agent – what the agent <i>has chosen</i> to do. Intentions are desires to which the agent has to some extent committed. In implemented systems, this means the agent has begun executing a plan.
<ul><li><b>Plans</b>: Plans are sequences of actions (recipes or knowledge areas) that an agent can perform to achieve one or more of its intentions. Plans may include other plans: my plan to go for a drive may include a plan to find my car keys. This reflects that in Bratman's model, plans are initially only partially conceived, with details being filled in as they progress.</li></ul></li>
<li><b>Events</b>: These are triggers for reactive activity by the agent. An event may update beliefs, trigger plans or modify goals. Events may be generated externally and received by sensors or integrated systems. Additionally, events may be generated internally to trigger decoupled updates or plans of activity.</li></ul>
<p>BDI was also extended with an obligations component, giving rise to the BOID agent architecture<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> to incorporate obligations, norms and commitments of agents that act within a social environment.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="BDI_interpreter">BDI interpreter</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belief%E2%80%93desire%E2%80%93intention_software_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: BDI interpreter"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>This section defines an idealized BDI interpreter that provides the basis of SRI's <a href="/wiki/Procedural_reasoning_system" title="Procedural reasoning system">PRS</a> lineage of BDI systems:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rao&#95;1995&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rao_1995-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>initialize-state</li>
<li>repeat
<ol><li>options: option-generator (event-queue)</li>
<li>selected-options: deliberate(options)</li>
<li>update-intentions(selected-options)</li>
<li>execute()</li>
<li>get-new-external-events()</li>
<li>drop-unsuccessful-attitudes()</li>
<li>drop-impossible-attitudes()</li></ol></li>
<li>end repeat</li></ol>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Limitations_and_criticisms">Limitations and criticisms</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belief%E2%80%93desire%E2%80%93intention_software_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Limitations and criticisms"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The BDI software model is one example of a reasoning architecture for a single rational agent, and one concern in a broader <a href="/wiki/Multi-agent_system" title="Multi-agent system">multi-agent system</a>. This section bounds the scope of concerns for the BDI software model, highlighting known limitations of the architecture.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Learning</b>: BDI agents lack any specific mechanisms within the architecture to learn from past behavior and adapt to new situations.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Three attitudes</b>: Classical <a href="/wiki/Decision_theory" title="Decision theory">decision theorists</a> and planning research questions the necessity of having all three attitudes, <a href="/wiki/Distributed_artificial_intelligence" title="Distributed artificial intelligence">distributed AI</a> research questions whether the three attitudes are sufficient.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rao&#95;1995&#95;3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rao_1995-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Logics</b>: The multi-modal logics that underlie BDI (that do not have complete axiomatizations and are not efficiently computable) have little relevance in practice.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rao&#95;1995&#95;3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rao_1995-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Multiple agents</b>: In addition to not explicitly supporting learning, the framework may not be appropriate to learning behavior. Further, the BDI model does not explicitly describe mechanisms for interaction with other agents and integration into a <a href="/wiki/Multi-agent_system" title="Multi-agent system">multi-agent system</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Explicit goals</b>: Most BDI implementations do not have an explicit representation of goals.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li><b>Lookahead</b>: The architecture does not have (by design) any lookahead deliberation or forward planning. This may not be desirable because adopted plans may use up limited resources, actions may not be reversible, task execution may take longer than forward planning, and actions may have undesirable side effects if unsuccessful.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
