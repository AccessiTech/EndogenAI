<p>The <b>frontal lobe</b> is the largest <a href="/wiki/Lobes_of_the_brain" title="Lobes of the brain">lobe of the vertebrate brain</a> and the most anterior lobe of the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_hemisphere" title="Cerebral hemisphere">cerebral hemispheres</a>. The anatomical <a href="/wiki/Sulcus_(neuroanatomy)" title="Sulcus (neuroanatomy)">groove</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Central_sulcus" title="Central sulcus">central sulcus</a> separates the frontal lobe from the <a href="/wiki/Parietal_lobe" title="Parietal lobe">parietal lobe</a>, and the deeper anatomical groove called the <a href="/wiki/Lateral_sulcus" title="Lateral sulcus">lateral sulcus</a> separates the frontal lobe from the <a href="/wiki/Temporal_lobe" title="Temporal lobe">temporal lobe</a>. The most anterior ventral, orbital end of the frontal lobe is known as the frontal pole, which is one of the three so-called <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_hemisphere#Poles" title="Cerebral hemisphere">poles of the cerebrum</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-radiopaedia&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-radiopaedia-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The outer, multifurrowed surface of the frontal lobe is called the <b>frontal cortex</b>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Like all cortical tissue, the frontal cortex is a thin layer of gray matter making up the outer portion of the brain. The frontal cortex is further subdivided into several anatomical and functional structures, including those of the <a href="/wiki/Motor_cortex" title="Motor cortex">motor cortex</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Premotor_cortex" title="Premotor cortex">premotor cortex</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nonprimary_motor_cortex" title="Nonprimary motor cortex">nonprimary motor cortex</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Primary_motor_cortex" title="Primary motor cortex">primary motor cortex</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">prefrontal cortex</a> (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Dorsolateral_prefrontal_cortex" title="Dorsolateral prefrontal cortex">dorsolateral prefrontal cortex</a>).
</p><p>Located in the frontal lobe are also four principal <a href="/wiki/Gyrus" title="Gyrus">gyri</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Precentral_gyrus" title="Precentral gyrus">precentral gyrus</a> is directly anterior to the <a href="/wiki/Central_sulcus" title="Central sulcus">central sulcus</a>, running parallel to it and containing the <a href="/wiki/Primary_motor_cortex" title="Primary motor cortex">primary motor cortex</a>, which controls voluntary movements of <a href="/wiki/Cortical_homunculus" title="Cortical homunculus">specific body parts</a>. Three other frontal gyri, horizontally arranged, are the <a href="/wiki/Superior_frontal_gyrus" title="Superior frontal gyrus">superior frontal gyrus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_frontal_gyrus" title="Middle frontal gyrus">middle frontal gyrus</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_frontal_gyrus" title="Inferior frontal gyrus">inferior frontal gyrus</a>. The inferior frontal gyrus is further subdivided into the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_part_of_inferior_frontal_gyrus" title="Orbital part of inferior frontal gyrus">orbital part</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area_44" title="Brodmann area 44">triangular part</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Opercular_part_of_inferior_frontal_gyrus" class="mw-redirect" title="Opercular part of inferior frontal gyrus">opercular part</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carpenter&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and it is functionally known as the <a href="/wiki/Ventrolateral_prefrontal_cortex" title="Ventrolateral prefrontal cortex">ventrolateral prefrontal cortex</a>.
</p><p>The frontal lobe further contains most of the <a href="/wiki/Dopaminergic_pathways" title="Dopaminergic pathways">dopaminergic neurons</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dopaminergic_pathways" title="Dopaminergic pathways">Dopaminergic pathways</a> are associated with <a href="/wiki/Reward_system" title="Reward system">reward</a>, <a href="/wiki/Attention" title="Attention">attention</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short-term_memory" title="Short-term memory">short-term memory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planning" title="Planning">planning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Motivation" title="Motivation">motivation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">Dopamine</a> tends to limit and select <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">sensory information</a> coming from the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Forebrain" title="Forebrain">forebrain</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Structure">Structure</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frontal_lobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Frontal_lobe_animation.gif" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Frontal_lobe_animation.gif/250px-Frontal_lobe_animation.gif" decoding="async" width="200" height="200" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/54/Frontal_lobe_animation.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="300" /></a><figcaption>Frontal lobe (red) of left cerebral hemisphere</figcaption></figure>
<p>The frontal lobe is the largest lobe of the brain and makes up about a third of the surface area of each <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_hemisphere" title="Cerebral hemisphere">hemisphere</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carpenter&#95;3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> On the dorsal surface of each hemisphere, the <a href="/wiki/Central_sulcus" title="Central sulcus">central sulcus</a> separates the frontal lobe from the parietal lobe. The <a href="/wiki/Lateral_sulcus" title="Lateral sulcus">lateral sulcus</a> separates the frontal lobe from the <a href="/wiki/Temporal_lobe" title="Temporal lobe">temporal lobe</a>.
</p><p>The frontal lobe can be divided into a lateral, polar, orbital (above the <a href="/wiki/Orbit_(anatomy)" title="Orbit (anatomy)">orbit</a>; also called basal or <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location" title="Anatomical terms of location">ventral</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location" title="Anatomical terms of location">medial</a> part. Each of these parts consists of a particular <a href="/wiki/Gyrus" title="Gyrus">gyrus</a>:
</p>
<ul><li>Lateral part: lateral part of the <a href="/wiki/Superior_frontal_gyrus" title="Superior frontal gyrus">superior frontal gyrus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_frontal_gyrus" title="Middle frontal gyrus">middle frontal gyrus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Inferior_frontal_gyrus" title="Inferior frontal gyrus">inferior frontal gyrus</a></li>
<li>Polar part: <a href="/wiki/Frontopolar_cortex" class="mw-redirect" title="Frontopolar cortex">frontopolar cortex</a>, transverse frontopolar gyri, frontomarginal gyrus.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Orbitofrontal_cortex" title="Orbitofrontal cortex">Orbital part</a>: <a href="/wiki/Orbital_gyri" title="Orbital gyri">Lateral orbital gyrus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orbital_gyri" title="Orbital gyri">anterior orbital gyrus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Posterior_orbital_gyrus" class="mw-redirect" title="Posterior orbital gyrus">posterior orbital gyrus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medial_orbital_gyrus" class="mw-redirect" title="Medial orbital gyrus">medial orbital gyrus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gyrus_rectus" class="mw-redirect" title="Gyrus rectus">gyrus rectus</a></li>
<li>Medial part: Medial part of the <a href="/wiki/Superior_frontal_gyrus" title="Superior frontal gyrus">superior frontal gyrus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cingulate_gyrus" class="mw-redirect" title="Cingulate gyrus">cingulate gyrus</a>.</li></ul>
<p>The gyri are separated by <a href="/wiki/Sulcus_(neuroanatomy)" title="Sulcus (neuroanatomy)">sulci</a>. For example, the precentral gyrus is in front of the central sulcus, and behind the <a href="/wiki/Precentral_sulcus" title="Precentral sulcus">precentral sulcus</a>. The superior and middle frontal gyri are divided by the <a href="/wiki/Superior_frontal_sulcus" title="Superior frontal sulcus">superior frontal sulcus</a>. The middle and inferior frontal gyri are divided by the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_frontal_sulcus" title="Inferior frontal sulcus">inferior frontal sulcus</a>.
</p><p>The human frontal lobe reaches full maturity only at mid to late 20s (25-30 years of age)—in fact, the prefrontal cortex, in particular, continues to mature well into the third decade.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> A small amount of <a href="/wiki/Atrophy" title="Atrophy">atrophy</a>, however, is normal in the aging person's frontal lobe. Fjell et al. (2009), studying the rate of brain atrophy over time among 142 healthy adults aged 60–91 years compared with 122 patients with <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer&#39;s disease">Alzheimer's disease</a>, showed that there was a marked volumetric decline in those with Alzheimer's and a much smaller decline (averaging 0.5%) in the healthy group at the 1-year <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">follow-up</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  These findings replicate those of Coffey et al. (1992), whose results also showed that the frontal lobe decreases in volume approximately 0.5–1% per year.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Function">Function</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frontal_lobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Function"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The frontal lobe, which comprises several anatomical and functional structures, supports, among other things, goal-directed behavior and abstract mental representations (e.g., the phenomenal, human ability to transcend the immediate spatiotemporal context by imagining oneself in the future).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is a mistake to believe that the primary function of the frontal lobe is <i>action</i>—that it is wholly committed, for instance, to reasoning and thus the regulation of sensory phenomena such as <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Affect_(psychology)" title="Affect (psychology)">affect</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Psychological_testing" title="Psychological testing">Psychological tests</a> that measure the functional integrity of the structures belonging to the frontal lobe include <a href="/wiki/Tapping_rate" title="Tapping rate">finger tapping</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Card_Sorting_Test" title="Wisconsin Card Sorting Test">Wisconsin Card Sorting Test</a>, and measures of <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, <a href="/wiki/Numeracy" title="Numeracy">numeracy skills</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and decision making.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frontal_lobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clinical significance"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Frontal_lobe_disorder" title="Frontal lobe disorder">Frontal lobe disorder</a></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Frontal_lobe_injury" title="Frontal lobe injury">Frontal lobe injury</a></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Damage">Damage</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frontal_lobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Damage"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Damage to the frontal lobe can occur in a number of ways and result in many different consequences. <a href="/wiki/Transient_ischemic_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="Transient ischemic attacks">Transient ischemic attacks</a> (TIAs) also known as mini-strokes, and <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">strokes</a> are common causes of frontal lobe damage in older adults (65 and over). These strokes and mini-strokes can occur due to the blockage of <a href="/wiki/Hemodynamics" title="Hemodynamics">blood flow</a> to the brain or as a result of the rupturing of an <a href="/wiki/Intracranial_aneurysm" title="Intracranial aneurysm">aneurysm</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_artery" class="mw-redirect" title="Cerebral artery">cerebral artery</a>. Other ways in which injury can occur include <a href="/wiki/Traumatic_brain_injury" title="Traumatic brain injury">traumatic brain injuries</a> incurred following accidents, diagnoses such as <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer&#39;s disease">Alzheimer's disease</a> or <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson&#39;s disease">Parkinson's disease</a> (which cause <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a> symptoms), and <a href="/wiki/Frontal_lobe_epilepsy" title="Frontal lobe epilepsy">frontal lobe epilepsy</a> (which can occur at any age).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Stuss&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stuss-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Very often, frontal lobe damage is recognized in those with <a href="/wiki/Fetal_Alcohol_Spectrum_Disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorder">prenatal alcohol exposure</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Symptoms">Symptoms</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frontal_lobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Symptoms"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Common effects of damage to the frontal lobe are varied. Patients who have experienced frontal lobe trauma may know the appropriate response to a situation but display inappropriate responses to those same situations in real life <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2025)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Similarly, emotions that are felt may not be expressed in the face or voice. For example, someone who is feeling happy would not smile, and the voice would be devoid of emotion. Along the same lines, though, the person may also exhibit excessive, unwarranted displays of emotion. Depression is common in stroke patients. Also common is a loss of or decrease in motivation. Someone might not want to carry out normal daily activities and would not feel "up to it".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Stuss&#95;17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stuss-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Those who are close to the person who has experienced the damage may notice changes in behavior.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The case of <a href="/wiki/Phineas_Gage" title="Phineas Gage">Phineas Gage</a> was long considered exemplary of these symptoms, though more <a href="/wiki/Phineas_Gage#Theoretical_misuse" title="Phineas Gage">recent research</a> has suggested that accounts of his personality change have been poorly evidenced. The frontal lobe is the same part of the brain that is responsible for <a href="/wiki/Executive_functions" title="Executive functions">executive functions</a> such as planning for the future, judgment, decision-making skills, <a href="/wiki/Attention_span" title="Attention span">attention span</a>, and inhibition. These functions can decrease in someone whose frontal lobe is damaged.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Stuss&#95;17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stuss-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Consequences that are seen less frequently are also varied. <a href="/wiki/Confabulation" title="Confabulation">Confabulation</a> may be the most frequently indicated "less common" effect. In the case of confabulation, someone gives false information while maintaining the belief that it is the truth. In a small number of patients, uncharacteristic cheerfulness can be noted. This effect is seen mostly in patients with lesions to the right frontal portion of the brain.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Stuss&#95;17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stuss-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Another infrequent effect is that of <a href="/wiki/Reduplicative_paramnesia" title="Reduplicative paramnesia">reduplicative paramnesia</a>, in which patients believe that the location in which they currently reside is a replica of one located somewhere else. Similarly, those who experience <a href="/wiki/Capgras_syndrome" class="mw-redirect" title="Capgras syndrome">Capgras syndrome</a> after frontal lobe damage believe that an identical "replacement" has taken the identity of a close friend, relative, or other person and is posing as that person. This last effect is seen mostly in schizophrenic patients who also have a neurological disorder in the frontal lobe.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Stuss&#95;17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stuss-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="DNA_damage">DNA damage</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frontal_lobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: DNA damage"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In the human frontal cortex, a set of genes undergo reduced expression after age 40 and especially after age 70.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid15190254&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15190254-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  This set includes genes that have key functions in <a href="/wiki/Synaptic_plasticity" title="Synaptic plasticity">synaptic plasticity</a> important in learning and memory, <a href="/wiki/Vesicular_transport" class="mw-redirect" title="Vesicular transport">vesicular transport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondrial function</a>.  During <a href="/wiki/Ageing" title="Ageing">aging</a>, <a href="/wiki/DNA_damage_(naturally_occurring)" title="DNA damage (naturally occurring)">DNA damage</a> is markedly increased in the <a href="/wiki/Promoter_(genetics)" title="Promoter (genetics)">promoters</a> of the genes displaying reduced expression in the frontal cortex.  In cultured human neurons, these promoters are selectively damaged by <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_stress" title="Oxidative stress">oxidative stress</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid15190254&#95;21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15190254-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Individuals with <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> associated neurocognitive disorders accumulate nuclear and <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a> damage in the frontal cortex.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid22554480&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22554480-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Genetic">Genetic</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frontal_lobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Genetic"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A report from the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Mental_Health" title="National Institute of Mental Health">National Institute of Mental Health</a> says a <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a> variant of <a href="/wiki/Catechol-O-methyltransferase" title="Catechol-O-methyltransferase">(COMT)</a> that reduces dopamine activity in the <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">prefrontal cortex</a> is related to poorer performance and inefficient functioning of that brain region during working memory, tasks, and to a slightly increased risk for <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frontal_lobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Psychosurgery">Psychosurgery</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frontal_lobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Psychosurgery"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In the early 20th century, a medical treatment for <a href="/wiki/Mental_illness" class="mw-redirect" title="Mental illness">mental illness</a>, first developed by <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> <a href="/wiki/Neurologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurologist">neurologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Egas_Moniz" class="mw-redirect" title="Egas Moniz">Egas Moniz</a>, involved damaging the pathways connecting the frontal lobe to the <a href="/wiki/Limbic_system" title="Limbic system">limbic system</a>. A frontal <a href="/wiki/Lobotomy" title="Lobotomy">lobotomy</a> (sometimes called frontal leucotomy) successfully reduced distress but at the cost of often blunting the subject's emotions, volition and <a href="/wiki/Personality_psychology" title="Personality psychology">personality</a>. The indiscriminate use of this <a href="/wiki/Psychosurgery" title="Psychosurgery">psychosurgical</a> procedure, combined with its severe side effects and a <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">mortality rate</a> of 7.4 to 17 per cent,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> earned it a bad reputation. The frontal lobotomy has largely died out as a psychiatric treatment.  More precise psychosurgical procedures are still used, although rarely. They may include anterior capsulotomy (bilateral thermal lesions of the anterior limbs of the <a href="/wiki/Internal_capsule" title="Internal capsule">internal capsule</a>) or the <a href="/wiki/Bilateral_cingulotomy" title="Bilateral cingulotomy">bilateral cingulotomy</a> (involving lesions of the anterior <a href="/wiki/Cingulate_gyrus" class="mw-redirect" title="Cingulate gyrus">cingulate gyri</a>) and might be used to treat otherwise untreatable <a href="/wiki/Obsessive-compulsive_disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Obsessive-compulsive disorder">obsessional disorders</a> or <a href="/wiki/Clinical_depression" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinical depression">clinical depression</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Theories_of_function">Theories of function</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frontal_lobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Theories of function"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Theories of frontal lobe function can be separated into four categories:
</p>
<ul><li>Single-process theories, which propose that "damage to a single process or system is responsible for a number of different <a href="/wiki/Dysexecutive_syndrome" title="Dysexecutive syndrome">dysexecutive symptoms</a>"<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Multi-process theories, which propose "that the frontal lobe executive system consists of a number of components that typically work together in everyday actions (heterogeneity of function)"<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Construct-led theories, which propose that "most if not all frontal functions can be explained by one construct (homogeneity of function) such as working memory or inhibition"<sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Single-symptom theories, which propose that a specific dysexecutive symptom (e.g., confabulation) is related to the processes and construct of the underlying structures.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Other theories include:
</p>
<ul><li>Stuss (1999) suggests a differentiation into two categories according to homogeneity and heterogeneity of function.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed. (August 2025)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li>Grafman's managerial knowledge units (MKU) / structured event complex (SEC) approach (cf. Wood &amp; Grafman, 2003)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed. (August 2025)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li>Miller &amp; Cohen's integrative theory of prefrontal functioning (e.g. Miller &amp; Cohen, 2001)</li>
<li>Rolls's stimulus-reward approach and Stuss's anterior attentional functions (Burgess &amp; Simons, 2005; Burgess, 2003; Burke, 2007).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed. (August 2025)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li></ul>
<p>It may be highlighted that the theories described above differ in their focus on certain processes/systems or construct-lets.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2024)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Stuss (1999) remarks that the question of homogeneity (single construct) or heterogeneity (multiple processes/systems) of function "may represent a problem of semantics and/or incomplete functional analysis rather than an unresolvable dichotomy" (p.&#160;348). However, further research will show if a unified theory of frontal lobe function that fully accounts for the diversity of functions will be available.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Other_primates">Other primates</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Frontal_lobe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other primates"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Many scientists had thought that the frontal lobe was disproportionately enlarged in humans compared to other primates. This was thought to be an important feature of human evolution and seen as the primary reason why human cognition differs from that of other primates. However, this view in relation to great apes has since been challenged by <a href="/wiki/Neuroimaging" title="Neuroimaging">neuroimaging</a> studies. Using <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a> to determine the volume of the frontal cortex in humans, all extant ape species, and several <a href="/wiki/Monkey" title="Monkey">monkey</a> species, it was found that the human frontal cortex was not relatively larger than the cortex of other <a href="/wiki/Hominidae" title="Hominidae">great apes</a>, but was relatively larger than the frontal cortex of <a href="/wiki/Gibbon" title="Gibbon">lesser apes</a> and the monkeys.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Semendeferi&#95;29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Semendeferi-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The higher cognition of the humans is instead seen to relate to a greater connectedness given by <a href="/wiki/Nerve_tract" title="Nerve tract">neural tracts</a> that do not affect the cortical volume.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Semendeferi&#95;29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Semendeferi-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This is also evident in the <a href="/wiki/Neural_pathway" title="Neural pathway">pathways</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Large_scale_brain_networks" class="mw-redirect" title="Large scale brain networks">language network</a> connecting the frontal and temporal lobes.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Friederici&#95;30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Friederici-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
