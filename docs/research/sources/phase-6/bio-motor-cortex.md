<p>The <b>motor cortex</b> comprises interconnected fields on the posterior <a href="/wiki/Frontal_lobe" title="Frontal lobe">frontal lobe</a>—chiefly <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area" title="Brodmann area">Brodmann area</a> 4 (primary motor cortex, M1) and area 6 (premotor cortex and supplementary motor areas)—that plan, select and execute <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_action" title="Voluntary action">voluntary movements</a>. These regions transform goals into patterned activity in descending pathways to <a href="/wiki/Brainstem" title="Brainstem">brainstem</a> and spinal motor circuits, enabling dexterous eye, face and limb actions. Modern work shows overlapping, action‑type representations rather than a strictly point‑to‑point "homunculus," and highlights direct cortico‑motoneuronal projections that underwrite fine finger control. Clinically, motor‑cortical organization shapes deficits after stroke and neurodegenerative disease and guides mapping for neurosurgery and neurotechnology.
</p>

<p>Motor cortex is commonly divided into three closely interacting fields:
</p>
<ul><li>the <b>primary motor cortex</b> (M1; <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area" title="Brodmann area">Brodmann area</a> 4), which issues descending commands for fine motor control and force production;</li>
<li>the <b>premotor cortex</b> (lateral area 6), which integrates sensory cues and internal rules to prepare and select actions; and</li>
<li>the <b>supplementary motor area</b> (SMA; medial area 6), which contributes to internally generated actions, sequential operations, and bimanual coordination.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Roland&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roland-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-He95&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-He95-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Halsband1994&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Halsband1994-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Nomenclature_and_boundaries">Nomenclature and boundaries</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nomenclature and boundaries"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In classical cytoarchitectonics, <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area" title="Brodmann area">Brodmann area</a> 4 (BA4) corresponds to primary motor cortex (M1) occupying the <a href="/wiki/Precentral_gyrus" title="Precentral gyrus">precentral gyrus</a> and the anterior bank of the <a href="/wiki/Central_sulcus" title="Central sulcus">central sulcus</a>, with medial continuation in the anterior (motor) portion of the <a href="/wiki/Paracentral_lobule" title="Paracentral lobule">paracentral lobule</a>. Its posterior border abuts primary somatosensory cortex (BA3,1,2) along the lip and wall of the central sulcus; its anterior border is the precentral sulcus where area 6 begins. Receptorarchitectonic work subdivides BA4 into a posterior field (4p) concentrated along the sulcal wall and an anterior field (4a) on the gyral crown. Area 6 lies anterior to BA4 across the superior and middle frontal gyri and includes the lateral premotor cortex; on the medial wall it encompasses the supplementary and pre‑supplementary motor areas.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Brodmann1909&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodmann1909-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Geyer1996&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geyer1996-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-He95&#95;2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-He95-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Nomenclature_variants">Nomenclature variants</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nomenclature variants"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Human and non‑human primate atlases differ in labeling schemes across anterior agranular cortex. In macaques, premotor fields are often subdivided into F2/F4 (dorsal/ventral caudal) and F5/F7 (ventral/dorsal rostral), which only partly correspond to human PMd/PMv. In humans, receptorarchitectonic divisions of BA4 into 4a/4p and probabilistic maps derived from imaging produce slightly different borders than gyral/sulcal landmarks, especially near the central sulcus.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Geyer1996&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geyer1996-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-He95&#95;2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-He95-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Primary_motor_cortex_(M1)"><span id="Primary_motor_cortex_.28M1.29"></span>Primary motor cortex (M1)</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Primary motor cortex (M1)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:M1_microcircuit_model.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="Diagram of a cylindrical microcircuit model of mouse primary motor cortex showing cortical layers, neuron classes, morphologies and interconnections." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/M1_microcircuit_model.jpg/330px-M1_microcircuit_model.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="209" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/M1_microcircuit_model.jpg/500px-M1_microcircuit_model.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/M1_microcircuit_model.jpg/960px-M1_microcircuit_model.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2904" data-file-height="2023" /></a><figcaption>Simplified microcircuit model of primary motor cortex highlighting prominent layer V output neurons (including Betz cells) and their projections.</figcaption></figure>
<p>M1 contains large pyramidal neurons (Betz cells) in layer V and projects densely to spinal and cranial motor circuits via the corticospinal and corticonuclear tracts. Although Betz cells are distinctive, they form only a small proportion of corticospinal outputs; most corticospinal fibers arise from non‑Betz layer V neurons in M1 and from adjacent motor areas.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rivara&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rivara-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Toyoshima&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toyoshima-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Premotor_cortex">Premotor cortex</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Premotor cortex"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Motor_Cortex_monkey.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="Schematic map of macaque frontal cortex showing PMd and PMv subdivisions (rostral and caudal), SMA, pre‑SMA, SEF and FEF." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Motor_Cortex_monkey.jpg/330px-Motor_Cortex_monkey.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="291" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Motor_Cortex_monkey.jpg/500px-Motor_Cortex_monkey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Motor_Cortex_monkey.jpg/960px-Motor_Cortex_monkey.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1233" data-file-height="1194" /></a><figcaption>Motor representations in non‑human primate frontal cortex identified with microstimulation and recording (schematic).</figcaption></figure>
<p>Premotor cortex is commonly divided into dorsal (PMd) and ventral (PMv) sectors, each with rostral and caudal parts. PMd contributes to reach planning and selection among competing directions, whereas PMv is heavily involved in shaping the hand for grasp and in multisensory guidance of actions in peri‑personal space. These areas are part of a broader parieto‑frontal system linking dorsal visual streams with motor plans, and their boundaries lie within cytoarchitectonic area 6 lateral to BA4.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Matelli1985&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matelli1985-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Preuss1996&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Preuss1996-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-RizzLu2001&#95;10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RizzLu2001-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rizzolatti1988&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rizzolatti1988-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Mirror‑neuron_responses"><span id="Mirror.E2.80.91neuron_responses"></span>Mirror‑neuron responses</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mirror‑neuron responses"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In macaque PMv (area F5), some neurons fire both during execution of a grasp and during observation of the same action performed by others; these "mirror" responses have been proposed to contribute to action understanding and imitation. The extent and function of mirror‑like responses in humans remain debated, but convergent EEG/MEG and fMRI evidence shows action‑observation effects in premotor and parietal circuits that project to M1.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-diPellegrino1992&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-diPellegrino1992-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rizzolatti2010&#95;13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rizzolatti2010-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Eye‑movement_motor_fields_(FEF/SEF)"><span id="Eye.E2.80.91movement_motor_fields_.28FEF.2FSEF.29"></span>Eye‑movement motor fields (FEF/SEF)</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Eye‑movement motor fields (FEF/SEF)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The frontal eye field (FEF) in the precentral/premotor region and the supplementary eye field (SEF) on the dorsomedial wall form part of the motor network controlling saccades, smooth pursuit and eye–head coordination. FEF receives visual input from occipito‑temporal pathways and projects to the <a href="/wiki/Superior_colliculus" title="Superior colliculus">superior colliculus</a> and brainstem gaze centers; SEF participates in internally generated saccade sequences and performance monitoring. Microstimulation of FEF evokes fixed‑vector saccades, whereas SEF stimulation elicits context‑dependent eye movements and sequence effects.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bruce1985&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bruce1985-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Schiller2001&#95;15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiller2001-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Supplementary_motor_area_(SMA)"><span id="Supplementary_motor_area_.28SMA.29"></span>Supplementary motor area (SMA)</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Supplementary motor area (SMA)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Electrical stimulation and functional imaging implicate SMA in initiating internally generated action and in sequencing. SMA also contains a coarse, overlapping body map and sends direct corticospinal projections. Lesions or inactivation can impair movement initiation and transiently abolish bimanual coordination in non‑human primates.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Roland&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roland-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-He95&#95;2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-He95-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kermadi1997&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kermadi1997-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Cytoarchitecture_and_connectivity">Cytoarchitecture and connectivity</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cytoarchitecture and connectivity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Motor cortex is agranular isocortex with a six‑layered structure; layer IV is reduced or indistinct, whereas layer V contains the large corticospinal neurons. M1 is sometimes termed <i>area gigantopyramidalis</i> because Betz cells are especially prominent there. Premotor and SMA share a similar laminar pattern but lack Betz cells. Afferent input arrives via thalamic relays conveying basal ganglia and cerebellar output; rich corticocortical connections link PMd/PMv with <a href="/wiki/Posterior_parietal_cortex" title="Posterior parietal cortex">posterior parietal cortex</a> and SMA with <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">prefrontal cortex</a>. Efferents descend via the corticospinal and corticonuclear tracts and via brainstem motor pathways.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Standring2016&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Standring2016-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Nolte2010&#95;18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nolte2010-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Histology">Histology</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Histology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>M1, premotor cortex and SMA are agranular isocortex. Layer IV is attenuated or indistinct, while layer V contains large pyramidal neurons including Betz cells in M1. Neuron classes include corticospinal and corticobulbar projection neurons, corticocortical pyramidal cells in layers II/III and V, and diverse GABAergic interneurons (basket, chandelier, Martinotti). Cortical thickness varies across the precentral gyrus from the gyral crown to the anterior sulcal wall, paralleling shifts in input/output density and myelination. Betz cells constitute a small minority of corticospinal neurons but have exceptionally thick axons and fast conduction velocities.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kandel2013&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kandel2013-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rivara&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rivara-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Toyoshima&#95;7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toyoshima-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Descending_pathways">Descending pathways</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Descending pathways"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Motor cortical output travels in the corticospinal tract (pyramidal tract) and corticobulbar systems. Fibers originate from multiple fields: approximately one quarter from small pyramidal neurons in M1, substantial fractions from premotor and SMA, and a sizable minority from somatosensory cortex; Betz cells account for only a few percent of corticospinal axons. Many corticospinal terminals contact spinal interneurons, whereas direct cortico‑motoneuronal connections are thought to underlie fine finger control.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Toyoshima&#95;7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Toyoshima-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Heffner&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heffner-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Orofacial_and_speech_control">Orofacial and speech control</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Orofacial and speech control"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Corticobulbar projections from lateral M1 and ventrolateral premotor cortex target cranial motor nuclei through relay zones in the pontine and medullary reticular formation. Orofacial, laryngeal and tongue representations occupy the inferior precentral gyrus and adjacent opercular cortex. Direct cortico‑motoneuronal influences on <a href="/wiki/Nucleus_ambiguus" title="Nucleus ambiguus">nucleus ambiguus</a> (laryngeal) are sparse in most mammals but appear more substantial in humans and great apes, consistent with fine control of phonation and articulation. Lesions produce dysarthria and apraxia of speech; stimulation studies and functional imaging localize laryngeal motor cortex to a dorsal–ventral pair flanking the central sulcus.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Penfield1959&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penfield1959-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Simonyan2011&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simonyan2011-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Guenther2016&#95;23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guenther2016-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Motor_maps_and_coding">Motor maps and coding</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Motor maps and coding"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Rather than one‑to‑one control of individual muscles, stimulation and single‑unit studies indicate that motor cortex contains heavily overlapping representations and can specify ethologically relevant, multi‑joint actions. Extended‑duration microstimulation in monkeys evokes coordinated movements such as defensive postures or reach‑to‑grasp sequences, suggesting a map of action types arranged across cortex.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Graziano2008&#95;24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graziano2008-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Graziano2002&#95;25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graziano2002-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Cooke2004&#95;26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooke2004-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Population_coding,_dynamics_and_oscillations"><span id="Population_coding.2C_dynamics_and_oscillations"></span>Population coding, dynamics and oscillations</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Population coding, dynamics and oscillations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Population vectors, directional tuning and dynamical‑systems descriptions have been used to account for how ensembles in motor cortex evolve during reach and grasp. Beta‑band (≈13–30&#160;Hz) oscillations increase during hold periods and desynchronize around movement onset; high‑gamma activity scales with force and kinematics in electrocorticography.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Readiness_potential_and_pre‑movement_activity"><span id="Readiness_potential_and_pre.E2.80.91movement_activity"></span>Readiness potential and pre‑movement activity</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Readiness potential and pre‑movement activity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Scalp and intracranial recordings show a slow negative potential, the readiness potential, beginning up to 1–2 s before self‑initiated movement. Sources include SMA, pre‑SMA and M1, with lateralized readiness potentials reflecting effector selection.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Shibasaki2006&#95;27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shibasaki2006-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Competing_frameworks_and_control_principles">Competing frameworks and control principles</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Competing frameworks and control principles"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Several accounts describe how motor cortex specifies movement: (i) <b>muscle‑based</b> coding, in which neurons correlate with muscle activity; (ii) <b>movement‑based</b> coding, emphasizing kinematics/forces of effectors; and (iii) <b>dynamical‑systems</b> views, in which population activity flows along low‑dimensional trajectories that generate movement without requiring an explicit set‑point for each muscle. Optimal feedback control frames motor behavior as task‑level goals stabilized by feedback, with motor cortex participating in a distributed controller.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Scott2004&#95;28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scott2004-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Todorov2002&#95;29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Todorov2002-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Shenoy2013&#95;30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shenoy2013-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Churchland2012&#95;31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Churchland2012-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Development_and_plasticity">Development and plasticity</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Development and plasticity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Motor representations are shaped by development and use. Early corticospinal projections are exuberant; activity‑dependent pruning and myelination refine conduction velocity and terminal specificity through childhood and adolescence. Experience can expand or contract cortical zones devoted to particular movements, and recovery after injury may recruit premotor and somatosensory contributions to descending pathways.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Martin2005&#95;32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martin2005-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Maturation_of_corticospinal_systems">Maturation of corticospinal systems</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Maturation of corticospinal systems"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Transient bilateral projections are common in infancy; progressive myelination, synaptic pruning and strengthening of cortico‑motoneuronal connections accompany the emergence of fine manual dexterity. Diffusion MRI and TMS demonstrate increasing tract integrity and decreasing motor thresholds across childhood and adolescence.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Eyre2007&#95;33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eyre2007-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Skill_learning_and_reorganization">Skill learning and reorganization</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Skill learning and reorganization"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Skill acquisition alters representational geometry in M1 and premotor cortex, biases cortico‑motoneuronal drive toward task muscles, and modifies intracortical inhibition/facilitation. Non‑invasive stimulation (e.g., TMS, tDCS) can transiently modulate learning rates and retention.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Dayan2011&#95;34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dayan2011-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Lifespan_change">Lifespan change</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Lifespan change"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Aging is accompanied by altered recruitment of premotor and contralateral homologues during motor tasks and by changes in myelination and thickness gradients across precentral cortex; training can partially normalize these patterns.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Seidler2010&#95;35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seidler2010-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Comparative_anatomy_and_evolution">Comparative anatomy and evolution</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Comparative anatomy and evolution"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Across mammals, corticospinal organization varies with dexterity. Species with skilled, independent finger movements (e.g., humans, macaques) possess abundant cortico‑motoneuronal projections and a prominent M1 “hand knob,” whereas species with less manual dexterity rely more on propriospinal and brainstem pathways. In non‑primates, corticospinal fibers terminate largely on interneurons, while in higher primates many terminations contact motoneurons directly. The distribution and strength of CM projections correlate with the capacity for independent finger movements and tool manipulation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Heffner&#95;20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heffner-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Lemon2008&#95;36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lemon2008-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-PorterLemon1993&#95;37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PorterLemon1993-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Tool use and fine object manipulation in primates rely on parieto‑frontal networks linking anterior intraparietal areas with ventral premotor cortex and M1. Expansion of these circuits in humans is associated with increased CM projection density and greater fractional representation of distal musculature, supporting skilled grasp, tool use and praxis.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Lemon2008&#95;36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lemon2008-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-PorterLemon1993&#95;37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PorterLemon1993-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Clinical_mapping">Clinical mapping</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Clinical mapping"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Preoperative and intraoperative mapping reduce morbidity in resections near motor areas. Direct cortical stimulation during awake craniotomy identifies essential sites for movement and speech articulation; subcortical stimulation traces the course of descending fibers. Outside the operating room, transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) defines resting motor threshold, motor maps, and intracortical inhibition/facilitation using paired‑pulse protocols.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Penfield1950&#95;38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penfield1950-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rossini2015&#95;39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossini2015-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Parameters_and_safety">Parameters and safety</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Parameters and safety"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Intraoperative mapping commonly uses short trains of biphasic or monophasic pulses delivered via bipolar electrodes placed on the cortical surface (typical frequencies ~50–60&#160;Hz; train durations on the order of 1–5 s; currents in the low milliampere range adjusted to evoke responses while avoiding afterdischarges). Mapping proceeds in small spatial steps to delineate essential cortex and white‑matter pathways; electrocorticography is used to monitor afterdischarges, and stimulation is paused or medication given if they arise.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rossini2015&#95;39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossini2015-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="TMS_metrics">TMS metrics</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: TMS metrics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Resting motor threshold (RMT) is defined as the minimum stimulator output evoking motor evoked potentials (MEPs) of standard amplitude in a proportion of trials at rest; active motor threshold (AMT) is defined during slight contraction. Paired‑pulse TMS quantifies short‑interval intracortical inhibition and intracortical facilitation, while mapping protocols estimate the areal extent of corticomotor representations.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rossini2015&#95;39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossini2015-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Clinical significance"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Focal lesions of M1 produce contralateral weakness, loss of fine fractionated movements and pathological reflexes, whereas lesions affecting premotor and SMA tend to impair movement initiation, selection, praxis and bimanual coordination. Symptoms reflect the somatotopic bias of affected territories and the crossing of corticospinal fibers in the medullary pyramids.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Pathology:_ALS_and_upper_motor_neuron_disease">Pathology: ALS and upper motor neuron disease</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Pathology: ALS and upper motor neuron disease"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Upper motor neuron signs (spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski sign) reflect corticospinal and cortical dysfunction. In amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), degeneration involves layer V corticospinal neurons as well as spinal motor neurons; neurophysiology often shows corticospinal hyperexcitability early in disease. Imaging and pathology implicate precentral gyrus atrophy and corticospinal tract degeneration.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Turner2013&#95;40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner2013-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="UMN‑dominant_syndromes"><span id="UMN.E2.80.91dominant_syndromes"></span>UMN‑dominant syndromes</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: UMN‑dominant syndromes"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Primary_lateral_sclerosis" title="Primary lateral sclerosis">Primary lateral sclerosis</a> presents with progressive upper motor neuron involvement and spastic paraparesis or quadriparesis with relative sparing of lower motor neurons; abnormalities of motor cortex excitability and corticospinal conduction are typical. <a href="/wiki/Corticobasal_syndrome" title="Corticobasal syndrome">Corticobasal syndrome</a> features asymmetric apraxia, rigidity and dystonia linked to frontoparietal degeneration; premotor and parietal dysfunction contribute to impaired praxis and alien‑limb phenomena.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kandel2013&#95;19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kandel2013-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Stroke_and_recovery">Stroke and recovery</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Stroke and recovery"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Ischemic injury affecting the precentral gyrus or its descending fibers causes hemiparesis or hemiplegia. Recovery engages perilesional M1, ipsilesional premotor cortex, contralesional homotopic areas and cerebello‑thalamo‑cortical circuits. Task‑oriented therapy and neuromodulation aim to bias adaptive plasticity.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Movement_disorders_and_spasticity">Movement disorders and spasticity</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Movement disorders and spasticity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Abnormal excitability and loss of intracortical inhibition within motor cortex contribute to spasticity, dystonia and task‑specific cramps. Focal cortical dysplasias in the precentral region may cause motor seizures.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Apraxia_and_SMA_syndrome">Apraxia and SMA syndrome</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Apraxia and SMA syndrome"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Lesions of lateral premotor areas and inferior parietal cortex can produce ideomotor apraxia. Medial wall resections or stroke involving SMA often yield transient akinesia and impaired bimanual coordination (SMA syndrome) with gradual recovery.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Electrical stimulation studies by <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Fritsch" title="Gustav Fritsch">Fritsch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Hitzig" title="Eduard Hitzig">Eduard Hitzig</a> (1870) and by <a href="/wiki/David_Ferrier" title="David Ferrier">David Ferrier</a> (1870s) first demonstrated a motor representation in cortex. Experiments by <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Munk" title="Hermann Munk">Hermann Munk</a> and contemporaries debated sensory vs. motor localization. In the 20th century, intra‑operative stimulation in humans by <a href="/wiki/Wilder_Penfield" title="Wilder Penfield">Penfield</a> and colleagues produced the iconic—though simplified—“homunculus,” while later work emphasized distributed and overlapping motor maps. Contemporary studies integrate microstimulation, single‑unit and population recordings, and neuroimaging to refine these maps.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Fritsch1870&#95;41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fritsch1870-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Ferrier1876&#95;42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferrier1876-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Penfield1950&#95;38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penfield1950-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Research_and_neurotechnology">Research and neurotechnology</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Research and neurotechnology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Recordings from motor cortex have been used to control external devices in proof‑of‑concept <a href="/wiki/Brain%E2%80%93computer_interface" title="Brain–computer interface">brain–computer interface</a> (BCI) studies in humans with tetraplegia, demonstrating real‑time control of cursors and robotic limbs.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Hochberg2006&#95;43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hochberg2006-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Methods_of_study">Methods of study</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Methods of study"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Motor cortex has been investigated with lesions, electrical stimulation, intracortical microstimulation, single‑unit and local field recordings, electrocorticography, MEG/EEG, fMRI, TMS/tDCS and pharmacologic perturbations. Each technique samples different spatial and temporal scales and has distinct interpretive limits.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Methodological_limitations">Methodological limitations</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Methodological limitations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Spatial precision, sampling, and signal‑to‑noise differ markedly across modalities (e.g., fMRI’s indirect hemodynamic signal vs. ECoG’s high‑temporal‑resolution field potentials). Mapping results depend on behavioral context and analysis assumptions; causal inference generally requires stimulation or lesion methods. Non‑invasive stimulation has safety and interpretive constraints summarized in consensus guidance.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Churchland2012&#95;31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Churchland2012-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rossini2015&#95;39-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rossini2015-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History_and_premotor_cortex">History and premotor cortex</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Motor_cortex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: History and premotor cortex"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Early electrical stimulation studies established a cortical motor center: in 1870, Fritsch and Hitzig showed that stimulating parts of dog cortex produced contralateral movements,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Fritsch1870&#95;41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fritsch1870-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and Ferrier’s subsequent work in primates mapped a rough dorsoventral progression from leg to face.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Ferrier1876&#95;42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferrier1876-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Clinical stimulation and lesion studies in humans, popularized by Penfield mid‑20th century, refined the human somatotopic picture and its limits.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Penfield1950&#95;38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Penfield1950-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Debate over field boundaries (area 4 vs. area 6) persisted into the 20th century; by the 1930s–1950s a consensus emerged that cytoarchitectonic area 4 (M1) and area 6 (premotor/SMA) are functionally distinct but connected fields with partly overlapping descending outputs.
</p><p>Contemporary work divides premotor cortex into dorsal and ventral sectors, each with rostral and caudal parts (PMDr, PMDc, PMVr, PMVc), largely based on architectonic, connectivity, and stimulation criteria in macaques.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Matelli1985&#95;8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matelli1985-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Preuss1996&#95;9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Preuss1996-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> PMDc neurons are strongly engaged during reach preparation and execution, and microstimulation there can evoke coordinated shoulder–arm–hand movements resembling reaching. PMVr (classically F5) contains grasp‑related neurons and was the original site where “mirror” discharge was described in macaques; electrical stimulation on behavioral time scales in precentral regions can elicit complex, ethologically meaningful actions (e.g., defensive and hand‑to‑mouth sequences), underscoring that motor fields encode coordinated actions rather than single muscles.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rizzolatti1988&#95;11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rizzolatti1988-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Graziano2002&#95;25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Graziano2002-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Cooke2004&#95;26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cooke2004-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
