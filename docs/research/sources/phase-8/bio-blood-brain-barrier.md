<p>The <b>blood–brain barrier</b> (<b>BBB</b>) is a highly selective <a href="/wiki/Semipermeable_membrane" title="Semipermeable membrane">semipermeable</a> border of <a href="/wiki/Endothelium" title="Endothelium">endothelial cells</a> that regulates the transfer of solutes and chemicals between the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory system</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>, thus protecting the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> from harmful or unwanted substances in the <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-daneman&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daneman-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The blood–brain barrier is formed by endothelial cells of the <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillary wall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astrocyte" title="Astrocyte">astrocyte</a> end-feet ensheathing the capillary, and <a href="/wiki/Pericytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Pericytes">pericytes</a> embedded in the capillary <a href="/wiki/Basement_membrane" title="Basement membrane">basement membrane</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This system allows the passage of some small molecules by <a href="/wiki/Passive_transport" title="Passive transport">passive diffusion</a>, as well as the selective and active transport of various nutrients, ions, organic anions, and macromolecules such as glucose and <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> that are crucial to neural function.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The blood–brain barrier restricts the passage of <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>, the diffusion of <a href="/wiki/Solute" class="mw-redirect" title="Solute">solutes</a> in the blood, and <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">large</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hydrophile" title="Hydrophile">hydrophilic</a> molecules into the <a href="/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid" title="Cerebrospinal fluid">cerebrospinal fluid</a>, while allowing the diffusion of <a href="/wiki/Hydrophobe" title="Hydrophobe">hydrophobic</a> molecules (O<sub>2</sub>, CO<sub>2</sub>, hormones) and small non-polar molecules.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-pmid33208141&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid33208141-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Cells of the barrier actively transport <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic</a> products such as glucose across the barrier using specific <a href="/wiki/Membrane_transport_protein" title="Membrane transport protein">transport proteins</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Stamatovic&#95;2008&#95;179–192&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stamatovic_2008_179–192-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The barrier also restricts the passage of peripheral immune factors, like signaling molecules, antibodies, and immune cells, into the central nervous system, thus insulating the brain from damage due to peripheral immune events.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Specialized brain structures participating in sensory and secretory integration within brain <a href="/wiki/Neural_circuit" title="Neural circuit">neural circuits</a>—the <a href="/wiki/Circumventricular_organ" class="mw-redirect" title="Circumventricular organ">circumventricular organs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Choroid_plexus" title="Choroid plexus">choroid plexus</a>—have in contrast highly permeable capillaries.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kaur&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaur-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Structure">Structure</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Blood_Brain_Barriere.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Blood_Brain_Barriere.jpg/250px-Blood_Brain_Barriere.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="139" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Blood_Brain_Barriere.jpg/500px-Blood_Brain_Barriere.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="1152" data-file-height="640" /></a><figcaption>Astrocytes surrounding capillaries in the brain (artistic rendition)</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Blood_vessels_brain_english.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Blood_vessels_brain_english.jpg/250px-Blood_vessels_brain_english.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="155" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Blood_vessels_brain_english.jpg/500px-Blood_vessels_brain_english.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="868" data-file-height="537" /></a><figcaption>Sketch showing constitution of blood vessels inside the brain</figcaption></figure>
<p>The BBB results from the selectivity of the <a href="/wiki/Tight_junctions" class="mw-redirect" title="Tight junctions">tight junctions</a> between the endothelial cells of brain capillaries, restricting the passage of solutes.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-daneman&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daneman-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  At the interface between blood and the brain, endothelial cells are adjoined continuously by these tight junctions, which are composed of smaller subunits of <a href="/wiki/Transmembrane_protein" title="Transmembrane protein">transmembrane proteins</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Occludin" title="Occludin">occludin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Claudins" class="mw-redirect" title="Claudins">claudins</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/CLDN5" title="CLDN5">Claudin-5</a>), <a href="/wiki/Junctional_adhesion_molecule" title="Junctional adhesion molecule">junctional adhesion molecule</a> (such as JAM-A).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Stamatovic&#95;2008&#95;179–192&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stamatovic_2008_179–192-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Each of these tight junction proteins is stabilized to the endothelial cell membrane by another protein complex that includes scaffolding proteins such as <a href="/wiki/Tight_junction_protein_1" class="mw-redirect" title="Tight junction protein 1">tight junction protein 1</a> (ZO1) and associated proteins.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Stamatovic&#95;2008&#95;179–192&#95;6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stamatovic_2008_179–192-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The BBB is composed of endothelial cells restricting passage of substances from the blood more selectively than endothelial cells of capillaries elsewhere in the body. <a href="/wiki/Astrocyte" title="Astrocyte">Astrocyte</a> cell projections called astrocytic feet (also known as "<a href="/wiki/Glia_limitans" title="Glia limitans">glia limitans</a>") surround the endothelial cells of the BBB, providing biochemical support to those cells.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The BBB is distinct from the quite similar <a href="/wiki/Choroid_plexus#Function" title="Choroid plexus">blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier</a>, which is a function of the choroidal cells of the <a href="/wiki/Choroid_plexus" title="Choroid plexus">choroid plexus</a>, and from the <a href="/wiki/Blood-retinal_barrier" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood-retinal barrier">blood-retinal barrier</a>, which can be considered a part of the whole realm of such barriers.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Not all vessels in the human brain exhibit BBB properties. Some examples of this include the <a href="/wiki/Circumventricular_organs" title="Circumventricular organs">circumventricular organs</a>, the roof of the third and fourth <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_system" title="Ventricular system">ventricles</a>, capillaries in the pineal gland on the roof of the <a href="/wiki/Diencephalon" title="Diencephalon">diencephalon</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pineal_gland" title="Pineal gland">pineal gland</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-peering&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peering-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-gross1&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gross1-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Development">Development</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The BBB appears to be functional by the time of birth. <a href="/wiki/P-glycoprotein" title="P-glycoprotein">P-glycoprotein</a>, a <a href="/wiki/ATP-binding_cassette_transporter" class="mw-redirect" title="ATP-binding cassette transporter">transporter</a>, exists already in the embryonal endothelium.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Measurement of brain uptake of various blood-borne solutes showed that newborn endothelial cells were functionally similar to those in adults,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Braun&#95;2006&#95;147–152&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Braun_2006_147–152-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> indicating that a selective BBB is operative at birth.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Function">Function</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Function"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Neuroimmune_system" title="Neuroimmune system">Neuroimmune system</a></div>
<p>The blood–brain barrier acts effectively to protect brain tissue from circulating <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> and other potentially toxic substances.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Blood-brain&#95;barrier&#95;dysfunction&#95;in&#95;15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blood-brain_barrier_dysfunction_in-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Accordingly, <a href="/wiki/Blood-borne_disease" title="Blood-borne disease">blood-borne infections</a> of the brain are rare.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-daneman&#95;1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daneman-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">Infections</a> of the brain that do occur are often difficult to treat. <a href="/wiki/Antibodies" class="mw-redirect" title="Antibodies">Antibodies</a> are too large to cross the blood–brain barrier, and only certain <a href="/wiki/Antibiotics" class="mw-redirect" title="Antibiotics">antibiotics</a> are able to pass.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Raza&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raza-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In some cases, a drug has to be administered directly into the cerebrospinal fluid where it can enter the brain by crossing the <a href="/wiki/Blood-cerebrospinal_fluid_barrier" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier">blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Circumventricular_organs">Circumventricular organs</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Circumventricular organs"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Circumventricular_organs" title="Circumventricular organs">Circumventricular organs</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Circumventricular_organs" title="Circumventricular organs">Circumventricular organs</a> (CVOs) are individual structures located adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_ventricle" title="Fourth ventricle">fourth ventricle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Third_ventricle" title="Third ventricle">third ventricle</a> in the brain, and are characterized by dense capillary beds with <a href="/wiki/Vascular_permeability" title="Vascular permeability">permeable</a> endothelial cells unlike those of the blood–brain barrier.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-peering&#95;11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peering-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-gross1&#95;12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gross1-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Included among CVOs having highly permeable capillaries are the <a href="/wiki/Area_postrema" title="Area postrema">area postrema</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subfornical_organ" title="Subfornical organ">subfornical organ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vascular_organ_of_the_lamina_terminalis" class="mw-redirect" title="Vascular organ of the lamina terminalis">vascular organ of the lamina terminalis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Median_eminence" title="Median eminence">median eminence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pineal_gland" title="Pineal gland">pineal gland</a>, and three lobes of the  <a href="/wiki/Pituitary_gland" title="Pituitary gland">pituitary gland</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-peering&#95;11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peering-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-miyata&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miyata-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Permeable capillaries of the sensory CVOs (area postrema, subfornical organ, vascular organ of the lamina terminalis) enable rapid detection of circulating signals in systemic blood, while those of the secretory CVOs (median eminence, pineal gland, pituitary lobes) facilitate transport of brain-derived signals into the circulating blood.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-peering&#95;11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peering-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-gross1&#95;12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gross1-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Consequently, the CVO permeable capillaries are the point of bidirectional blood–brain communication for <a href="/wiki/Neuroendocrine" class="mw-redirect" title="Neuroendocrine">neuroendocrine</a> function.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-peering&#95;11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peering-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-miyata&#95;19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miyata-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Design&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Design-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Specialized_permeable_zones">Specialized permeable zones</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Specialized permeable zones"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The border zones between brain tissue "behind" the blood–brain barrier and zones "open" to blood signals in certain CVOs contain specialized hybrid capillaries that are leakier than typical brain capillaries, but not as permeable as CVO capillaries. Such zones exist at the border of the area postrema—<a href="/wiki/Nucleus_tractus_solitarii" class="mw-redirect" title="Nucleus tractus solitarii">nucleus tractus solitarii</a> (NTS),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-nts&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nts-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and median eminence—<a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Arcuate_nucleus" class="mw-redirect" title="Arcuate nucleus">arcuate nucleus</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Design&#95;20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Design-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-ctr&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctr-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> These zones appear to function as rapid transit regions for brain structures involved in diverse neural circuits—like the NTS and arcuate nucleus—to receive blood signals which are then transmitted into neural output.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Design&#95;20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Design-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-nts&#95;21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nts-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The permeable capillary zone shared between the median eminence and hypothalamic arcuate nucleus is augmented by wide pericapillary spaces, facilitating bidirectional flow of solutes between the two structures, and indicating that the median eminence is not only a secretory organ, but may also be a sensory organ.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Design&#95;20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Design-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-ctr&#95;22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctr-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Therapeutic_research">Therapeutic research</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Therapeutic research"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="As_a_drug_target">As a drug target</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: As a drug target"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The blood–brain barrier is formed by the brain capillary endothelium and excludes from the brain 100% of large-molecule neurotherapeutics and more than 98% of all small-molecule drugs.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-hersh&#95;23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hersh-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Overcoming the difficulty of delivering therapeutic agents to specific regions of the brain presents a major challenge to treatment of most brain disorders.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-sweeney&#95;24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sweeney-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In its neuroprotective role, the blood–brain barrier functions to hinder the delivery of many potentially important diagnostic and therapeutic agents to the brain. Therapeutic molecules and antibodies that might otherwise be effective in diagnosis and therapy do not cross the BBB in adequate amounts to be clinically effective.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-sweeney&#95;24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sweeney-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> To overcome this problem some peptides able to naturally cross the BBB have been widely investigated as a drug delivery system.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Mechanisms for drug targeting in the brain involve going either "through" or "behind" the BBB. Modalities for <a href="/wiki/Drug_delivery_to_the_brain" title="Drug delivery to the brain">drug delivery to the brain</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dosage_form" title="Dosage form">unit doses</a> through the BBB entail its disruption by <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmotic</a> means, or biochemically by the use of vasoactive substances, such as <a href="/wiki/Bradykinin" title="Bradykinin">bradykinin</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or even by localized exposure to <a href="/wiki/HIFU" class="mw-redirect" title="HIFU">high-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU)</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Other methods used to get through the BBB may entail the use of endogenous transport systems, including carrier-mediated transporters, such as glucose and amino acid carriers, receptor-mediated <a href="/wiki/Transcytosis" title="Transcytosis">transcytosis</a> for <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Transferrin" title="Transferrin">transferrin</a>, and the blocking of <a href="/wiki/Efflux_(microbiology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Efflux (microbiology)">active efflux transporters</a> such as <a href="/wiki/P-glycoprotein" title="P-glycoprotein">p-glycoprotein</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-sweeney&#95;24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sweeney-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Some studies have shown that <a href="/wiki/Vector_(molecular_biology)" title="Vector (molecular biology)">vectors</a> targeting BBB transporters, such as the <a href="/wiki/Transferrin_receptor" title="Transferrin receptor">transferrin receptor</a>, have been found to remain entrapped in brain endothelial cells of capillaries, instead of being ferried across the BBB into the targeted area.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-sweeney&#95;24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sweeney-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Intranasal_administration">Intranasal administration</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Intranasal administration"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The brain can be targeted non-invasively via the nasal passage. The drugs that remain in the passage after mucociliary clearance, enter the brain via three pathways: (1) Olfactory nerve-olfactory bulb-brain; (2) Trigeminal nerve-brain; and (3) Lungs/ Gastrointestinal tract-blood–brain <sup id="cite&#95;ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The first and second methods involve the nerves, so they use the neuronal pathway and the third is via systemic circulation. However, these methods are less efficient to deliver drugs as they are indirect methods.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Nanoparticles">Nanoparticles</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nanoparticles"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nanoparticles_for_drug_delivery_to_the_brain" title="Nanoparticles for drug delivery to the brain">Nanoparticles for drug delivery to the brain</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">Nanotechnology</a> is under preliminary research for its potential to facilitate the transfer of drugs across the BBB.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-sweeney&#95;24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sweeney-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Silva&#95;32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silva-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Capillary endothelial cells and associated <a href="/wiki/Pericytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Pericytes">pericytes</a> may be abnormal in tumors and the blood–brain barrier may not always be intact in brain tumors.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Silva&#95;32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silva-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Other factors, such as <a href="/wiki/Astrocytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Astrocytes">astrocytes</a>, may contribute to the resistance of brain tumors to therapy using nanoparticles.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Fat-soluble molecules less than 400 <a href="/wiki/Dalton_(unit)" title="Dalton (unit)">daltons</a> in mass can freely diffuse past the BBB through <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipid</a>-mediated passive diffusion.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Damage_in_injury_and_disease">Damage in injury and disease</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Damage in injury and disease"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The blood–brain barrier may become damaged in certain <a href="/wiki/Neurological_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurological disease">neurological diseases</a>, as indicated by <a href="/wiki/Neuroimaging" title="Neuroimaging">neuroimaging</a> studies of <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer&#39;s disease">Alzheimer's disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amyotrophic_lateral_sclerosis" class="mw-redirect" title="Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis">amyotrophic lateral sclerosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epilepsy" title="Epilepsy">epilepsy</a>, ischemic stroke,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Blood-brain&#95;barrier&#95;dysfunction&#95;in&#95;15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blood-brain_barrier_dysfunction_in-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Traumatic_brain_injury" title="Traumatic brain injury">brain trauma</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-sweeney&#95;24-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sweeney-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Systemic_disease" title="Systemic disease">systemic diseases</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Liver_failure" title="Liver failure">liver failure</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-daneman&#95;1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daneman-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Effects such as impaired glucose transport and endothelial degeneration may lead to metabolic dysfunction within the brain, and an increased permeability of the BBB to <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">proinflammatory</a> factors, potentially allowing antibiotics and <a href="/wiki/Phagocytes" class="mw-redirect" title="Phagocytes">phagocytes</a> to move across the BBB.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-daneman&#95;1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daneman-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-sweeney&#95;24-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sweeney-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> However, in many neurodegenerative diseases, the exact cause and pathology remains unknown. There is also increasing evidence that a healthy <a href="/wiki/Gut_microbiome" class="mw-redirect" title="Gut microbiome">gut microbiome</a> is necessary to maintain BBB integrity, both in <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_nervous_system" title="Development of the nervous system">development</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aging_brain" title="Aging brain">aging</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Braniste&#95;et&#95;al&#95;38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Braniste_et_al-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Zemmel&#95;et&#95;al&#95;39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zemmel_et_al-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Aburto&#95;&amp;&#95;Cryan&#95;40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aburto_&amp;_Cryan-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Hoffman&#95;et&#95;al&#95;41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoffman_et_al-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It is still unclear whether BBB dysfunction in disease is a causative agent, a result of the disease, or somewhere in the middle. 
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="History">History</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>An 1898 study observed that low-concentration "<a href="/wiki/Bile_salt" class="mw-redirect" title="Bile salt">bile salts</a>" failed to affect behavior when injected into the blood of animals. Thus, in theory, the salts failed to enter the brain.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Two years later, <a href="/wiki/Max_Lewandowsky" title="Max Lewandowsky">Max Lewandowsky</a> may have been the first to coin the term "blood–brain barrier" in 1900, referring to the hypothesized semipermeable membrane.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There is some debate over the creation of the term <i>blood–brain barrier</i> as it is often attributed to Lewandowsky, but it does not appear in his papers. The creator of the term may have been <a href="/wiki/Lina_Stern" title="Lina Stern">Lina Stern</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Stern was a Russian scientist who published her work in Russian and French. Due to the language barrier between her publications and English-speaking scientists, this could have made her work a lesser-known origin of the term.
</p><p>All the while, <a href="/wiki/Bacteriologist" title="Bacteriologist">bacteriologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ehrlich" title="Paul Ehrlich">Paul Ehrlich</a> was studying <a href="/wiki/Staining" title="Staining">staining</a>, a procedure that is used in many <a href="/wiki/Histology" title="Histology">microscopy studies</a> to make fine biological structures visible using chemical dyes.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-saunders&#95;45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saunders-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> As Ehrlich injected some of these dyes (notably the <a href="/wiki/Aniline_dye" class="mw-redirect" title="Aniline dye">aniline dyes</a> that were then widely used), the dye stained all of the <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a> of some kinds of animals except for their brains.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-saunders&#95;45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-saunders-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> At that time, Ehrlich attributed this lack of staining to the brain simply not picking up as much of the dye.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>However, in a later experiment in 1913, <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Goldmann" title="Edwin Goldmann">Edwin Goldmann</a> (one of Ehrlich's students) injected the dye directly into the <a href="/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid" title="Cerebrospinal fluid">cerebrospinal fluid</a> of animal brains. He found then the brains did become dyed, but the rest of the body did not, demonstrating the existence of a compartmentalization between the two. At that time, it was thought that the blood vessels themselves were responsible for the barrier, since no obvious membrane could be found.
</p>
