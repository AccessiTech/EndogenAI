</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Mapping senses to the central nervous system</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Neuroanatomy" title="Neuroanatomy">neuroanatomy</a>, <b>topographic map</b> is the ordered projection of a sensory surface (like the retina or the skin) or an effector system (like the musculature) to one or more structures of the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>.  Topographic maps can be found in all <a href="/wiki/Sensory_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensory system">sensory systems</a> and in many <a href="/wiki/Motor_system" title="Motor system">motor systems</a>.
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Visual_system">Visual system</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Topographic_map_(neuroanatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Visual system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Retinotopy" title="Retinotopy">Retinotopy</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Visual_system" title="Visual system">visual system</a> refers to the part of the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> that allows an organism to see. It interprets information from visible light to build a representation of the world.  The ganglion cells of the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> project in an orderly fashion to the <a href="/wiki/Lateral_geniculate_nucleus" title="Lateral geniculate nucleus">lateral geniculate nucleus</a> (LGN) of the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a> and from there to the primary <a href="/wiki/Visual_cortex" title="Visual cortex">visual cortex</a> (V1); adjacent spots on the retina are represented by adjacent <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a> in the lateral geniculate nucleus and the <a href="/wiki/Primary_visual_cortex" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary visual cortex">primary visual cortex</a>. The term for this pattern of projection is <i>topography</i>. There are many types of topographic maps in the visual cortices, including <a href="/wiki/Retinotopic" class="mw-redirect" title="Retinotopic">retinotopic</a> maps, <a href="/wiki/Ocular_dominance" title="Ocular dominance">ocular dominance</a> maps and orientation maps. Retinotopic maps are the easiest to understand in terms of topography. Retinotopic maps are those in which the image on the retina is maintained in the cortices (V1 and the LGN). In other words, if a specific region of the cortices was damaged, that individual would then have a blind spot in the real world, they would not be able to see the bit of the world that corresponded to the retina damage. Orientation maps are also topographic. In these maps there are cells which have a preference to a certain orientation, the maximum firing rate of the cell will be achieved at that preference. As the orientation is moved away from, the firing rate will drop. An orientation map is topographic because neighboring <a href="/wiki/Neural_tissue" class="mw-redirect" title="Neural tissue">neural tissues</a> have similar orientation preferences.
</p><p>The term <i>retinotopic</i> refers to the maintenance of the particular order of afferent connections from the retina along the afferent pathway via sub-cortical structures to V1 and other cortical visual areas. The primary visual cortex (V1, <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area" title="Brodmann area">Brodmann's area</a> 17) is the first cortical area to receive visual input.  The <a href="/wiki/Stria_of_Gennari" class="mw-redirect" title="Stria of Gennari">stria of Gennari</a> – a set of heavily <a href="/wiki/Myelinated" class="mw-redirect" title="Myelinated">myelinated</a>, horizontally projecting <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">axons</a> within the termination zone of lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) input to V1 – provides an anatomical marker particular to V1.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Development">Development</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Topographic_map_(neuroanatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Chemoaffinity_hypothesis" title="Chemoaffinity hypothesis">chemoaffinity hypothesis</a>, chemical labels are distributed in a graded fashion across the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tectum" class="mw-redirect" title="Tectum">tectum</a>.  This allows each <a href="/wiki/Retinal_ganglion_cell" title="Retinal ganglion cell">retinal ganglion cell</a> to recognize its proper termination site.  Experiments with artificially created compound eyes in <i><a href="/wiki/Xenopus" title="Xenopus">Xenopus</a></i> demonstrate that not only the ganglion cells but also their axons carry these specificities.  Axons must be able to communicate with each other to ensure that ones with the same positional tags innervate the same area of the <a href="/wiki/Superior_colliculus" title="Superior colliculus">superior colliculus</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Categories_of_retinotopic_maps">Categories of retinotopic maps</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Topographic_map_(neuroanatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Categories of retinotopic maps"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><b>First-order representations</b> are those in which adjacent points of the same hemifield always map to adjacent columns in the contralateral cortex.  An example of this would be the map in <a href="/wiki/Primary_visual_cortex" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary visual cortex">primary visual cortex</a> (V1).
</p><p><b>Second-order representations</b>, also known as a field discontinuity map, are maps that are organized such that it appears that a discontinuity has been introduced in either the <a href="/wiki/Visual_field" title="Visual field">visual field</a> or the retina. The maps in V2 and other <a href="/wiki/Extrastriate_cortex" title="Extrastriate cortex">extrastriate cortex</a> are second-order representations.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Auditory_system">Auditory system</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Topographic_map_(neuroanatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Auditory system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tonotopy" title="Tonotopy">Tonotopy</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Auditory_system" title="Auditory system">auditory system</a> is the sensory system for hearing in which the brain interprets information from the frequency of sound waves, yielding the perception of tones.  Sound waves enter the <a href="/wiki/Ear" title="Ear">ear</a> through the auditory canal.  These waves arrive at the <a href="/wiki/Eardrum" title="Eardrum">eardrum</a> where the properties of the waves are transduced into vibrations.  The vibrations travel through the bones of the <a href="/wiki/Inner_ear" title="Inner ear">inner ear</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Cochlea" title="Cochlea">cochlea</a>.  In the cochlea, the vibrations are transduced into electrical information through the firing of hair cells in the organ of Corti.  The <a href="/wiki/Organ_of_Corti" title="Organ of Corti">organ of Corti</a> projects in an orderly fashion to structures in the <a href="/wiki/Brainstem" title="Brainstem">brainstem</a> (namely, the <a href="/wiki/Cochlear_nuclei" class="mw-redirect" title="Cochlear nuclei">cochlear nuclei</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_colliculus" title="Inferior colliculus">inferior colliculus</a>), and from there to the medial geniculate nucleus of the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a> and the primary <a href="/wiki/Auditory_cortex" title="Auditory cortex">auditory cortex</a>. Adjacent sites on the <a href="/wiki/Organ_of_Corti" title="Organ of Corti">organ of Corti</a>, which are themselves selective for the <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> frequency, are represented by adjacent neurons in the aforementioned CNS structures.  This projection pattern has been termed <a href="/wiki/Tonotopy" title="Tonotopy">tonotopy</a>.
</p><p>The tonotopic layout of sound information begins in the <a href="/wiki/Cochlea" title="Cochlea">cochlea</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Basilar_membrane" title="Basilar membrane">basilar membrane</a> vibrates at different positions along its length depending upon the frequency of the sound. Higher frequency sounds are at the base of the cochlea, if it were unrolled, and low frequency sounds are at the apex.  This arrangement is also found in the <a href="/wiki/Auditory_cortex" title="Auditory cortex">auditory cortex</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Temporal_lobe" title="Temporal lobe">temporal lobe</a>.  In areas that are tonotopically organized, the frequency varies systematically from low to high along the surface of the cortex, but is relatively constant across cortical depth.  The general image of topographic organization in <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> is multiple tonotopic maps distributed over the surface of the cortex.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Somatosensory_system">Somatosensory system</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Topographic_map_(neuroanatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Somatosensory system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Somatotopic_arrangement" title="Somatotopic arrangement">Somatotopic arrangement</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">somatosensory system</a> comprises a diverse range of receptors and processing centers to produce the perception of touch, temperature, <a href="/wiki/Proprioception" title="Proprioception">proprioception</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nociception" title="Nociception">nociception</a>.  Receptors are located throughout the body including the <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a>, epithelia, internal organs, <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_muscle" title="Skeletal muscle">skeletal muscles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joint" title="Joint">joints</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cutaneous_receptors" class="mw-redirect" title="Cutaneous receptors">cutaneous receptors</a> of the skin project in an orderly fashion to the <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>, and from there, via different afferent pathways (<a href="/wiki/Dorsal_column-medial_lemniscus_tract" class="mw-redirect" title="Dorsal column-medial lemniscus tract">dorsal column-medial lemniscus tract</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spinothalamic_tract" title="Spinothalamic tract">spinothalamic tract</a>), to the <a href="/wiki/Ventral_posterior_nucleus" title="Ventral posterior nucleus">ventral posterior nucleus</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a> and the primary <a href="/wiki/Postcentral_gyrus" title="Postcentral gyrus">somatosensory cortex</a>. Again, adjacent areas on the skin are represented by adjacent neurons in all aforementioned structures.  This projection pattern has been termed <a href="/wiki/Somatotopy" class="mw-redirect" title="Somatotopy">somatotopy</a>.
</p><p>One common diagram of the somatotopic map is the <a href="/wiki/Cortical_homunculus" title="Cortical homunculus">cortical homunculus</a>.  This illustration is a fairly accurate representation of how much cortical area represents each body part or region.  It also maps what part of the cortex represents each region of the body.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Motor_system">Motor system</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Topographic_map_(neuroanatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Motor system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Unlike the topographic maps of the senses, the neurons of the <a href="/wiki/Motor_cortex" title="Motor cortex">motor cortex</a> are <a href="/wiki/Efferent_nerve_fiber" title="Efferent nerve fiber">efferent neurons</a> that exit the brain instead of bringing information to the brain through <a href="/wiki/Afferent_nerve_fiber" title="Afferent nerve fiber">afferent</a> connections.  The <a href="/wiki/Motor_system" title="Motor system">motor system</a> is responsible for initiating voluntary or planned movements (<a href="/wiki/Reflex" title="Reflex">reflexes</a> are mediated at the <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a> level, so movements associated with a reflex are not initiated by the motor cortex).  The activation from the motor cortex travels through <a href="/wiki/Betz_cell" title="Betz cell">Betz cells</a> down the <a href="/wiki/Corticospinal_tract" title="Corticospinal tract">corticospinal tract</a> through <a href="/wiki/Upper_motor_neuron" title="Upper motor neuron">upper motor neurons</a>, terminating at the anterior horn of the <a href="/wiki/Grey_matter" title="Grey matter">grey matter</a> where <a href="/wiki/Lower_motor_neuron" title="Lower motor neuron">lower motor neurons</a> transmit the signal to peripheral motor neurons and, finally, the voluntary <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscles</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Movements_not_requiring_use_of_topographic_maps">Movements not requiring use of topographic maps</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Topographic_map_(neuroanatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Movements not requiring use of topographic maps"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>There are several instances of movements that do not require the participation of topographic maps, or even the participation of the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>.  One instance would be (as already stated) in some <a href="/wiki/Reflex" title="Reflex">reflexes</a>.  Reflexes usually are mediated at the level of the spinal cord through <a href="/wiki/Reflex_arc" title="Reflex arc">reflex arcs</a>.  In humans, mono-, oligo-, and poly-synaptic reflex arcs, propriospinal <a href="/wiki/Interneuron" title="Interneuron">interneuron</a> systems, and internuncial gray matter neurons collectively participate continuously to produce spinal cord reflex that activates muscle.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The higher motor centers of <a href="/wiki/Octopus" title="Octopus">octopuses</a> (large brained  <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a>) are notable for organizing (unlike <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>) highly skilled movements without the use of somatotopic maps of their bodies.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Olfactory_system">Olfactory system</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Topographic_map_(neuroanatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Olfactory system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_system" title="Olfactory system">olfactory system</a> is the sensory system used for <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Olfaction">olfaction</a>, or the sense of smell.  It detects volatile, airborne substances.
</p><p>Most sensory systems spatially segregate afferent input from primary sensory neurons to construct a topographic map that defines the location of a sensory stimulus within the environment, as well as the quality of the stimulus itself.  Unlike other sensory systems, the topography in the olfactory system is not dependent on spatial properties of the stimuli.  Relieved of the requirement to map the position of an olfactory stimulus in space, the olfactory system employs spatial segregation of sensory input to encode the quality of an <a href="/wiki/Aroma_compound" class="mw-redirect" title="Aroma compound">odorant</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Auffarth&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Auffarth-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The topographic map revealed in the olfactory system differs in quality from the orderly representation inherent in the retinotopic, tonotopic, or somatotopic sensory maps.  Olfactory neurons differ from one another by the nature of the receptor that they possess. However, the olfactory sensory neurons to which olfactory bulb neurons are connected are also distributed across the receptor sheet (the olfactory epithelium) depending on their chemical preferences. Locations on the olfactory epithelium and the olfactory bulb are correlated <sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> so, as with other sensory systems, the topographic map in the brain is linked to the structure of the peripheral organ. This principle is called rhinotopy.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Auffarth&#95;7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Auffarth-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Individual olfactory sensory <a href="/wiki/Neurons" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurons">neurons</a> express only one of the thousand receptor <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a>, such that neurons are functionally distinct.  <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">Cells</a> expressing a given receptor in the olfactory epithelium are randomly dispersed within 1 of 4 broad zones.  Sensory neurons extend a single unbranched axon to the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_bulb" title="Olfactory bulb">olfactory bulb</a> such that the projections from neurons expressing a specific receptor converge on 2 out of the 1800 <a href="/wiki/Glomerulus_(olfaction)" title="Glomerulus (olfaction)">glomeruli</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>  The pattern of convergence is absolute and invariant in all individuals in a species.  The bulb therefore provides a spatial map that identifies which of the numerous receptors have been activated within the sensory epithelium so that the quality of an olfactory stimulus is encoded by specific combination of glomeruli activated by a given odorant (combinatorial code).
</p><p>While refinement of the bulbar topographic code relies on activity, the development occurs partly without apparent contribution from activity-dependent processes. Mice lacking the olfactory cyclic nucleotide-gated ion channel fail to exhibit odor-evoked electrophysiological responses in the sensory epithelium, but the pattern of convergence of like axons in the bulb is unaltered in these mutant mice, arguing strongly that olfactory experience is not necessary for the establishment or refinement of the topographic map.
</p><p>These findings, however, do not exclude a role for activity-dependent processes in the maintenance or potential plasticity of the map after it is established.  For example, neurons that do not express functional odorant receptors have been observed to have a significantly shorter half-life.  Activity-dependent processes may therefore be essential for the survival of the olfactory neurons postnatally, and in that manner, may alter the input to individual glomeruli, altering the sensitivity to individual odors.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Gustatory_system">Gustatory system</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Topographic_map_(neuroanatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gustatory system"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gustatory_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Gustatory system">gustatory system</a> is the sensory system for <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">taste</a>.  Like olfaction, taste requires a process of <a href="/wiki/Chemoreception" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemoreception">chemoreception</a>.  The receptors for taste are <a href="/wiki/Taste_buds" class="mw-redirect" title="Taste buds">taste buds</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">tongue</a>.  The tongue contains taste receptors, as well as mechanoreceptors.  Afferents from taste receptors and mechanoreceptors of the tongue access different ascending systems in the brainstem.  However, it is uncertain how these two sources of information are processed in cortex.  The primary gustatory cortex (G) is located near the somatotopic region for the tongue (S1), in the <a href="/wiki/Insular_cortex" title="Insular cortex">insular cortex</a> deep in the <a href="/wiki/Lateral_fissure" class="mw-redirect" title="Lateral fissure">lateral fissure</a> with the secondary taste areas in the <a href="/wiki/Operculum_(brain)" title="Operculum (brain)">opercula</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The peripheral taste system likely maintains a specific relationship between taste bud cells selectively responsive to one taste quality and the <a href="/wiki/Ganglion" title="Ganglion">ganglion cells</a> signaling that particular quality.  This explains the response specificity of some individual taste nerve, particularly because sweet, amino acid, and bitter receptors are expressed in distinct populations of taste cells.  Although anatomical evidence for such an exclusive relationship is lacking at the level of single receptor and ganglion cells, the relationship between single buds and their innervating ganglion cells is tractable neuroanatomically.  In taste, attempts to identify a spatial representation of taste receptors or taste qualities have revealed only an indistinct functional topography in the brain.  Nevertheless, taste ganglion cells must distribute peripheral fibers to particular receptor cell types and disseminate impulses centrally in a structurally organized manner.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Benefits">Benefits</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Topographic_map_(neuroanatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Benefits"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>For <a href="/wiki/Neurons" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurons">neurons</a>, it is metabolically costly to make long connections, and the trade-off of connection distance is time.  Thus, it is good design to group neurons together that are to be highly interconnected.  Multiple topographic maps is a feature that is advantageous because it allows maps of different sizes that would accommodate varying levels of acuity and details in signals.  A more detailed map has more neurons that would take up more area than a more global map, which would require fewer connections.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Techniques">Techniques</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Topographic_map_(neuroanatomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Techniques"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A variety of techniques have been used to establish the topographic maps in the brain.  The existence of topographical maps was shown early by electrical stimulation of the cortex, tracing patterns of epileptic seizures, stimulation sequences, and impairments due to lesions.  Details in the maps came later through <a href="/wiki/Microelectrode" title="Microelectrode">microelectrode</a> stimulation and recording techniques became commonly used in demonstrating somatotopic maps and later in the auditory and visual systems, both cortically and in subcortical structures such as the <a href="/wiki/Colliculus_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Colliculus (disambiguation)">colliculi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geniculate_nucleus_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Geniculate nucleus (disambiguation)">geniculate nuclei</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Single-unit_recording" title="Single-unit recording">Single-cell recording</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transcranial_magnetic_stimulation" title="Transcranial magnetic stimulation">transcranial magnetic stimulation</a> (TMS), electrical stimulation of the cortex, and <a href="/wiki/Functional_magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Functional magnetic resonance imaging">functional magnetic resonance imaging</a> (fMRI) are some of the techniques used to study maps in the brain. Many of the existing topographic maps have been further studied or refined using fMRI.  For example, <a href="/wiki/David_H._Hubel" title="David H. Hubel">Hubel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Torsten_Wiesel" title="Torsten Wiesel">Wiesel</a> originally studied the retinotopic maps in the primary visual cortex using single-cell recording.  Recently, however, imaging of the retinotopic map in the cortex and in sub-cortical areas, such as the lateral geniculate nucleus, have been improved using the fMRI technique.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
