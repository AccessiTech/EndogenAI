					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-subjectpageheader">
</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Automatic process by which the brain adjusts to stimuli</div>
<p><b>Sensory gating</b> describes neural processes of filtering out redundant or irrelevant <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(psychology)" title="Stimulus (psychology)">stimuli</a> from all possible environmental stimuli reaching the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>. Also referred to as gating or filtering, sensory gating prevents an overload of information in the higher-order centers of the brain. Sensory gating can also occur in different forms through changes in both perception and sensation, affected by various factors such as "<a href="/wiki/Arousal" title="Arousal">arousal</a>, recent stimulus exposure, and selective attention."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Although sensory gating is largely automatic, it also occurs within the context of attention processing as the brain selectively seeks for goal-relevant information.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Previous studies have shown a correlation between sensory gating and different <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive</a> functions, but there is not yet a solid evidence implying that the relationship between sensory gating and cognitive functions are modality-independent.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Cocktail_party_effect">Cocktail party effect</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_gating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cocktail party effect"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cocktail_party_effect" title="Cocktail party effect">cocktail party effect</a> illustrates how the brain inhibits input from environmental stimuli, while still processing sensory input from the attended stimulus. The cocktail party effect demonstrates sensory gating in hearing, but the other senses also go through the same process protecting primary cortical areas from being overwhelmed.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Neural_regions_involved">Neural regions involved</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_gating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Neural regions involved"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Information from sensory receptors make their way to the brain through <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a> and synapse at the thalamus. The <a href="/wiki/Pulvinar_nuclei" title="Pulvinar nuclei">pulvinar nuclei</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a> plays a major role in <a href="/wiki/Selective_attention" class="mw-redirect" title="Selective attention">attention</a>, and has a major role in filtering out unnecessary information in regards to sensory gating. In a proven clinical study, it has been found out that the two stimuli (S1 and S2) are transported within 500ms between the clicks and 8 seconds between the pairs, in which S1 is known to generate a trace of memory that lingers presumably in the hippocampal region while the S2 the arrives later to be compared with the first stimuli as it gets inhibited if provided with no new information. (Both S1 and S2 are commonly referred to auditory stimuli caused by the machines used to test sensory gating.) The <a href="/wiki/Pulvinar_nuclei" title="Pulvinar nuclei">pulvinar nuclei</a> in the thalamus function as the gatekeeper, deciding which information should be inhibited, and which should be sent to further cortical areas.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The CNS (Central Nervous System), after the pulvinar nuclei deems the information to be irrelevant, acts as an essential inhibitory mechanism that prevents the information from flowing into higher cortical centers.
</p><p>Sensory gating is mediated by a network in the brain which involves the <a href="/wiki/Auditory_cortex" title="Auditory cortex">auditory cortex</a> (AC), <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">prefrontal cortex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a>, as well as the olfactory cortex, which plays a part in sensory gating phenomenon. Other areas of the brain associated with sensory gating include the <a href="/wiki/Amygdala" title="Amygdala">amygdala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Striatum" title="Striatum">striatum</a>, medial <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">prefrontal cortex</a>, and mid-brain dopamine cell region (<a href="/wiki/GABAergic" title="GABAergic">GABAergic</a> neurons only). Research on sensory gating has been primarily occurring in cortical areas where the stimulus is consciously identified because it is a less invasive means of studying sensory gating. Studies on rats also show the brain stem, thalamus, and primary auditory cortex play a role in sensory gating for auditory stimuli.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Techniques_for_measurement">Techniques for measurement</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_gating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Techniques for measurement"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Paired-click_paradigm">Paired-click paradigm</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_gating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paired-click paradigm"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The paired-click paradigm is a common non-invasive technique used to measure sensory gating, a type of <a href="/wiki/Event-related_potential" title="Event-related potential">event-related potential</a>. For normal sensory gating, if a person hears a pair of clicks within 500 ms of one another, the person will gate out the second click because it is perceived as being redundant. Evidence of the gating can be seen in the P50 wave, occurring in the brain 50 ms after the click. Low values of the P50 wave indicate that sensory gating has occurred. High values of the P50 wave indicate a lack of sensory gating. Individuals with <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a> only reduce the amplitude of S2 by 10–20%, whereas individuals without schizophrenia reduce the amplitude of S2 by 80–90%.
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Other_techniques">Other techniques</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_gating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other techniques"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">Electroencephalography</a> (EEG) and <a href="/wiki/Magnetoencephalography" title="Magnetoencephalography">magnetoencephalographies</a> (MEG) are used to measure brain responses and are common techniques for studying sensory gating. One type of EEG measure used for sensory gating research is the <a href="/wiki/Event-related_potential" title="Event-related potential">event-related potential</a> (ERP). EEG research on sensory gating shows that gating starts almost immediately after receiving a stimulus. <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">Positron emission tomography</a> (PET) studies have shown that an increased need to gate information is accompanied by increased engagement of the thalamus. P50 wave testing is one of many auditory event-related potential studies.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Sensory_gating_deficits_and_mental_illness">Sensory gating deficits and mental illness</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_gating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sensory gating deficits and mental illness"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Schizophrenia">Schizophrenia</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_gating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Schizophrenia"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A large interest in sensory gating research is directed at improving deficits among people diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a>. People with schizophrenia often have deficits in gating the <a href="/wiki/Event-related_potential" title="Event-related potential">neuronal response</a> of the <a href="/wiki/P50_(neuroscience)" title="P50 (neuroscience)">P50 wave</a>, which is why P50 is the most widespread method of diagnosis. The test is conducted through having the patients hear two uniform sounds with an interval of 500 milliseconds. While the patients are hearing the sound, an <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">EEG</a> cap is used to measure the brain activity in response to those sounds. A normal subject shows a decrease in brain activity while hearing a second sound, while a subject showing equal brain activity to the first sound is more likely to have schizophrenia. Since people with schizophrenia can often have an <a href="/wiki/Sensory_overload" title="Sensory overload">overload</a> of attended stimuli, the P50 wave may serve a critical role in illuminating sensory gating at a neurological level.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Currently the test has been conducted on mice, and results have been identical to human subjects in that brain activity has decreased on the second sound. In the second experiment, scientists placed internal electrodes in the auditory regions of the brain. It was found that by the time the second sound occurred, a drop in brain activity had already initiated from the brainstem. The discovery of the filter effect activating as soon as the brainstem perceives a sound was carried out on mice with the "22q11 deletion syndrome," a syndrome associated with schizophrenia in humans.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:1&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The continuing study, to be verified, suggests that the filter system is indeed in the brainstem, offering hope for finding the neurological source of schizophrenia.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Post-Traumatic_Stress_Disorder_(PTSD)"><span id="Post-Traumatic_Stress_Disorder_.28PTSD.29"></span>Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_gating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Those with <a href="/wiki/Post-traumatic_stress_disorder" title="Post-traumatic stress disorder">PTSD</a> also exhibit impaired sensory gating. Compared to those with Generalized Anxiety Disorder and control groups, those with PTSD show high sensory hyperactivity and impaired sensory disinhibition.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Studies on the effect of PTSD on P50 gating have produced mixed results, with some finding a similar pattern to schizophrenia,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> some finding it was limited to auditory stimulation only,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and others finding no effect.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:2&#95;9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Impaired gating of N100 and P200 has also been observed.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:2&#95;9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Drug_influences">Drug influences</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_gating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Drug influences"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Nicotine">Nicotine</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_gating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Nicotine"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>One reason people report they like smoking cigarettes is <a href="/wiki/Nicotine" title="Nicotine">nicotine's</a> ability to aid their selective attention.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The nicotine causes the receptors to release <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>, which slows sensory inhibition causing a suppression of a subsequent stimuli. Due to its effect, nicotine can correct sensory gating deficits for individuals with schizophrenia (80% of individuals with schizophrenia smoke up to 30 cigarettes/day),<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:0&#95;3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> although the effects only last about 30 minutes since the <a href="/wiki/Nicotinic_acetylcholine_receptor" title="Nicotinic acetylcholine receptor">nicotine receptors</a> desensitize quickly. Some research suggests that the same <a href="/wiki/Self-medication" title="Self-medication">self-medication</a> is present among those with <a href="/wiki/Attention_deficit_hyperactivity_disorder" title="Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder">attention deficit hyperactivity disorder</a> (ADHD)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> as well as those on the <a href="/wiki/Autism_spectrum" class="mw-redirect" title="Autism spectrum">autism spectrum</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2024)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Sensory_gating_and_creativity">Sensory gating and creativity</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sensory_gating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sensory gating and creativity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Some research shows evidence of a connection between sensory gating and creative thinking. One experiment conducted in 2015 suggests that so-called "leaky" attention spans in people with high levels of psychopathology may lead to increased creativity. During the study, the researchers discovered that creative people tend to show reduced sensory gating, filtering out sound less than the normal subjects. Reduced ability to inhibit secondary information caused a wider range of unfiltered stimuli to come through a conscious brain, enabling a creative person to integrate different ideas, rendering creative thinking. The experiment was carried out by 97 participants, whose creativity was measured through recording their achievements and conducting a <a href="/wiki/Divergent_thinking" title="Divergent thinking">divergent thinking</a> test. After, sensory gating was measured through both the EEG and auditory clicks. The results proved that people with creative achievements did indeed show a reduced latent inhibition compared to the average subjects. Thus, the study showed evidence of a correlation between creativity and sensory gating with reduced filtering proving to be a mechanism for receiving a larger range of stimuli leading to more creativity.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
