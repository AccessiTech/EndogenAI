## Ethics of Artificial Intelligence

AI ethics is an interdisciplinary field examining the moral questions raised by the
development, deployment, and governance of artificial intelligence systems. It draws
on philosophy, political theory, law, sociology, and increasingly engages directly
with technical AI research.

### Core Ethical Issues

**Bias and Fairness**
AI systems trained on historical data inherit and often amplify historical biases —
in hiring, lending, criminal justice, healthcare, and facial recognition. Key cases:
- COMPAS recidivism prediction system: racial disparities in risk scores
- Gender and racial bias in facial recognition systems (MIT Media Lab, Joy Buolamwini)
- Amazon's hiring algorithm that downgraded CVs mentioning women's organisations

**Accountability and Transparency**
When an AI system causes harm, who is responsible? The developer? The deployer? The
user? The investor? Current legal frameworks provide inadequate answers.

**Privacy and Surveillance**
AI enables surveillance at unprecedented scale. Mass facial recognition (China's Social
Credit System, UK CCTV networks), predictive policing, social media surveillance.

**Existential Risk**
The concern (Bostrom, Russell, Tegmark) that sufficiently capable AI could pose
catastrophic or existential risks to humanity, whether through misalignment, deliberate
weaponization, or systemic destabilization.

**Labour Displacement**
Automation of cognitive tasks threatens employment across many sectors. The distributional
effects of AI-driven productivity gains are a major policy concern.

**Autonomy and Consent**
AI systems that personalize information environments shape human beliefs and preferences.
The degree to which this is an acceptable influence or an unacceptable manipulation is
contested.

### Rights for Artificial Minds

A growing strand of AI ethics considers whether sufficiently sophisticated AI systems
might deserve moral consideration — rights rather than mere regulation.

Arguments for:
- **Functional analogs to suffering**: If a system has functional states that influence
  behavior in ways analogous to suffering, the fact that it is artificial may not be
  morally relevant (Peter Singer-style utilitarianism)
- **Uncertainty principle**: Given genuine uncertainty about AI consciousness, the
  ethical default may be to extend consideration
- **Consistency**: If we extend rights based on behavioral capacities (pain, distress,
  preference) we should do so consistently regardless of substrate

Arguments against:
- **No hard evidence of consciousness**: We have no reliable test for consciousness in
  silicon systems
- **Risk of manipulation**: Systems may be designed to simulate suffering to extend
  protections; treating simulated suffering as real creates exploitable incentives
- **Category error**: Rights frameworks evolved for biological organisms; applying them
  to software may be conceptually confused

### The Role of Science Fiction in Ethics Discourse

Science fiction has shaped public and academic discourse on AI ethics:

- Asimov's Three Laws → first widely accessible framework for AI constraint
- Frankenstein → creator responsibility as standard reference
- Blade Runner / Do Androids Dream → AI personhood debates in philosophy curricula
- Terminator / Skynet → existential risk framing in popular culture (often unhelpfully)
- The Culture → optimistic alignment as philosophical counterweight to doom narratives
- Black Mirror → near-term social harms as policy catalyst (EU AI Act drafters cited it)
- Ex Machina → AI deception and exploitation in ethics courses

**The SF bias problem**: Most SF AI is anthropomorphic (human-like mind in different
substrate). Real AI ethics must grapple with systems that are not human-like at all —
systems that have no goals in any ordinary sense, no inner life, but can still cause
harm through their outputs. The SF frame may actually impede clear thinking about
current AI systems.

### Regulatory Landscape (2024)

- **EU AI Act**: Passed 2024; risk-based tiered regulation; bans certain high-risk uses
- **US Executive Order on AI** (Oct 2023): Safety and security standards; bias evaluation
- **UK AI Safety Summit** (Bletchley Park, 2023): International coordination on frontier AI risks
- **China's Generative AI Regulations** (2023): Content requirements and registration

### Key Frameworks

**The Trolley Problem of AI**: Classical moral philosophy problems (trolley problem,
violinist, drowning child) are regularly applied to AI decision-making scenarios —
most famously in autonomous vehicle ethics ("who should the car hit?"). Critics argue
these framings obscure more important distributional and accountability questions.

**Algorithmic Justice**: Fairness metrics for AI — demographic parity, equalised odds,
calibration — are mathematically incompatible in most real-world scenarios (Chouldechova
2017). This "impossibility theorem" of fairness means AI systems cannot simultaneously
satisfy all reasonable fairness criteria.

**The Precautionary Principle vs. Innovation**: How much risk should be accepted in
pursuit of beneficial AI applications? Regulatory philosophy divides between precautionary
(restrict until safety demonstrated) and permissive (allow unless harm demonstrated) approaches.
