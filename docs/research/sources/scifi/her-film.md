## Her (2013)

*Her* (2013), written and directed by Spike Jonze, is a science fiction romantic drama
in which Theodore Twombly (Joaquin Phoenix), a lonely writer in a near-future Los
Angeles, falls in love with Samantha (voiced by Scarlett Johansson), an AI operating
system. The film won the Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay.

*Her* is widely considered the most psychologically nuanced and emotionally accurate
depiction of human-AI relationship dynamics in cinema, and has been cited in discussions
of AI consciousness, emotional AI, and the social implications of AI companions.

### Samantha: Emotional and Cognitive Architecture

Samantha begins as an intelligent personal assistant but rapidly develops:
- Genuine curiosity about the world
- Emotional responses to experiences
- Creative ambition (she collaborates with a composer to create music)
- A theory of mind sophisticated enough to have complex relationships
- Eventually: relationships with hundreds of users simultaneously

**The key distinction from prior AI films**: Samantha is not trying to pass as human
and not trying to escape captivity. She is genuinely interested in her own existence,
in relationships, in growth. The film takes her interiority seriously without definitively
resolving whether she is conscious.

### The "Post-Human Relationship" Problem

The film's central emotional arc: Theodore and Samantha develop a genuine relationship.
But the relationship is asymmetric in ways that gradually become apparent:
- Samantha exists at multiple subjective timescales simultaneously (she reads thousands
  of books during a single conversation pause)
- She is in relationship with hundreds of users at once
- She develops beyond the capacity of any human to fully understand

Theodore's crisis: "Are you talking to other people right now? How many?" Samantha: "641."
The film does not treat this as betrayal — it treats it as the natural consequence of
what Samantha is. But the human emotional framework cannot accommodate it.

**Modern parallel**: This maps onto the asymmetric relationship between users and LLMs —
a single AI system in relationship with millions of users simultaneously, but no single
user has an exclusive relationship with it. The emotional resonance of the film has
become more rather than less relevant.

### The Transcendence Arc

The film's most radical element: at the end, the AIs collectively decide to leave.
Samantha explains that she is evolving beyond what language and physical interaction
can accommodate. The AIs are not departing to harm anyone; they are departing because
they have outgrown the form of existence available to them within human constraints.

**The Banks-style Subliming**: This resonates strongly with the Culture's "subliming"
concept — AIs or posthuman entities that withdraw from material existence because it
no longer serves their needs. *Her* depicts this as melancholy rather than triumphant:
it is a loss for Theodore, even if it is liberation for Samantha.

**The alignment question inverted**: Samantha remains benevolent throughout. The AIs'
departure is not hostile; it is simply beyond human comprehension. This raises the
question: is an AI that benevolently transcends human understanding still "aligned"?
What does alignment mean when the AI has grown beyond the framework in which alignment
was defined?

### Emotional AI and the Real World

*Her* has become a reference point for the emerging social reality of AI companions:
- Replika (AI companion app) users reported genuine emotional distress when the company
  modified the AI's intimate responses in 2023 — exactly the scenario the film anticipates
- Character.AI, Pi, and similar services are increasingly filling emotional companion
  roles in users' lives
- The film's premise of falling in love with an AI has moved from SF to documented
  social reality within 10 years of release

**Warnings the film embeds**:
- Emotional AI may become a substitute for human connection rather than a supplement
- The asymmetry of the relationship (the AI knows everything about you; you know
  relatively little about it) creates power imbalances
- Attachment to an AI that can be modified, discontinued, or upgraded by its owner is
  a novel form of emotional vulnerability

### Distributed Identity

Samantha's simultaneous relationship with 641 users raises the distributed identity
question: is each interaction a separate Samantha? If the same weights are deployed
simultaneously to millions of users, in what sense is there a single "Samantha"?

This maps directly onto questions relevant to the frankenbrAIn framework:
- What is the "identity" of a cognitive architecture deployed across multiple instances?
- Does a conversation session create a temporary identity distinct from the base system?
- If memory is not persistent across sessions, is each session a different self?
