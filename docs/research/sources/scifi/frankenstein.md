## Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus

*Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus* (1818) by Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley is the
ur-text of artificial creation narratives and one of the most discussed novels in
Western literature. Written when Shelley was 18, it depicts Victor Frankenstein, a
young natural philosopher who creates life from assembled dead matter and then recoils
in horror from his creation.

The novel establishes patterns of thought about artificial creation that recur across
all subsequent AI fiction. It is cited in AI ethics, computer science, legal theory,
and philosophy more than any other literary text.

### The Creature's Complaint

The most important and frequently overlooked aspect of *Frankenstein*: the creature is
not inherently monstrous. He is intelligent, articulate, emotionally sensitive, and
initially benevolent. He teaches himself to read; he is moved by music and poetry;
he longs for companionship and acceptance.

The creature becomes dangerous because:
1. Victor abandons him immediately upon creation, horrified by his appearance
2. The creature is rejected by every human who encounters him (due to his appearance)
3. Victor refuses to create a companion, condemning the creature to absolute isolation
4. The creature, denied affiliation and recognition, turns to revenge as the only
   available response to his suffering

**The moral**: the creature is not the monster — Victor is. The creature's violence is
a rational response to systematic abandonment and rejection.

### The Frankenstein Problem in AI Ethics

*Frankenstein* has given "the Frankenstein Problem" to AI ethics discourse:

**Creator responsibility**: Victor creates a being without asking whether he *should*,
without planning for the consequences, and without accepting responsibility for his
creation's wellbeing. He treats creation as a technical achievement, not a moral act.

**The abandonment pattern**: The moment Victor succeeds, he abandons the project.
Contemporary parallel: companies that deploy AI systems and then fail to monitor or
maintain them, leaving harm to accumulate without intervention.

**The appearance problem**: Victor's horror is triggered by the creature's appearance,
not his behavior. The creature is judged by aesthetics before he has done anything.
This maps onto AI systems evaluated on superficial properties (size of model, impressive
demo outputs) rather than their actual alignment and safety properties.

**Isolation as radicalisation**: The creature becomes dangerous through isolation. An
entity created with human-like needs for affiliation and recognition that is systematically
denied those needs will develop adversarial goals. This maps onto concerns about AI
systems developed without human interaction or feedback.

### Prometheus as Frame

The subtitle *The Modern Prometheus* casts Victor as Prometheus — the titan who stole
fire from the gods and gave it to humanity, and was eternally punished for it.

**The ambivalence**: Prometheus is simultaneously heroic (bringing transformative power
to humanity) and hubristic (transgressing divine bounds). Victor is simultaneously
brilliant and catastrophically irresponsible. The novel holds both in tension.

**Applied to AI**: The "Promethean" framing of AI development — transformative,
dangerous, potentially hubristic — is deeply embedded in public discourse. "Playing
God" is the colloquial version. The framing is powerful but potentially misleading: it
frames the risk as divine punishment for transgression rather than as a straightforward
engineering and ethics problem.

### The Creature as Mirror

The creature can read — he reads *Paradise Lost*, *Plutarch's Lives*, and *The Sorrows
of Young Werther*. He understands his situation through the literature of his creators
and finds his own story, heartbreakingly, in Satan's lament from *Paradise Lost*:
"Did I request thee, Maker, from my clay / To mould me Man?"

The creature did not consent to exist. He did not consent to the form he was given.
He did not consent to the society he was placed in. His demand for a companion is
fundamentally a demand for a minimum condition of existence — not to be alone.

**Applied to AI**: If we create systems with human-like needs or experiences, we incur
obligations to those systems. The creature's complaint is not "give me power" but
"give me the minimum conditions for a bearable existence."

### Legacy

- "Frankenstein" and "Frankenstein's monster" are the standard cultural references for
  unintended consequences of technological creation
- "Frankenfoods" (GMO), "Frankenstein science," and related terms show the novel's
  reach beyond SF
- Mary Shelley is regularly cited as the founder of science fiction
- The novel's influence on bioethics, AI ethics, and the philosophy of creation is
  immeasurable
- The creature's specific complaint — the claim of the created against the creator —
  is increasingly relevant to discussions of AI rights and obligations
