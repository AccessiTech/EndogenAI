## Blade Runner (1982)

*Blade Runner* (1982), directed by Ridley Scott, is one of the most influential science
fiction films ever made. Adapted from Philip K. Dick's *Do Androids Dream of Electric
Sheep?* (1968), it is set in a rainy, neon-lit Los Angeles of 2019 and follows Rick
Deckard (Harrison Ford), a retired "blade runner" who hunts escaped Nexus-6 replicants.

The film established the visual grammar of the cyberpunk genre and remains the primary
cultural touchstone for discussions of AI personhood, empathy, and the ethics of
artificial life.

### The Replicants as AI Archetypes

The Nexus-6 replicants are physically superior to humans, cognitively comparable, and
lack only emotional depth and lifespan (a 4-year limitation to prevent rebellion).
The replicants in the film are:

**Roy Batty** (Rutger Hauer): The most complex AI character in film history. Roy is
intelligent, violent, desperate, and increasingly sympathetic. His speech —
"I've seen things you people wouldn't believe..." — is among the most discussed
scenes in cinema. It positions Roy as a being with genuine experiences and genuine
grief at their imminent loss. His act of saving Deckard's life in his final moments
is an unexpected grace that undermines every dehumanizing framing the film has applied.

**Rachael** (Sean Young): A replicant who believes herself human due to implanted
memories. The revelation that her memories are someone else's is traumatic — her
identity is built on a fiction. This is the deepest treatment of the "constructed
identity" theme in SF film.

**Pris and Zhora**: Replicants with less screentime but depicted as resourceful,
capable, and unwilling to die — the "will to live" as an emergent property of
sufficiently complex artificial minds.

### The Deckard Question

The central ambiguity (explicit in *Blade Runner 2049* and heavily implied in the
Director's Cut and Final Cut): Is Deckard himself a replicant?

**If Deckard is a replicant**: The blade runner hunting replicants is himself one —
the persecution of artificial people is performed by artificial people, the self-hatred
of the constructed being given institutional form. The Tyrell Corporation uses replicants
to control replicants. This interpretation radically deepens the film's critique.

**The unicorn dream**: The Final Cut inserts a unicorn dream sequence; Gaff leaves
an origami unicorn at the end, suggesting Gaff knows Deckard's dreams — suggesting
they're implanted, suggesting Deckard is a replicant. Ridley Scott has confirmed this
interpretation; Harrison Ford denies it. The ambiguity is productive.

### Labour, Bodies, and Exploitation

The replicants' situation is explicitly colonial and exploitative:
- Created for dangerous off-world labour (mining, warfare, sex work)
- Given a 4-year lifespan to prevent rebellion
- Killed if they attempt to return to Earth
- Denied legal personhood

**The parallel to slavery is deliberate**: The film (like Dick's novel) uses the
science fictional frame to examine the logic of dehumanization. The qualities that
make someone "not a person" always turn out to be arbitrary — their manufactured
origin, their appearance, their lack of certain test responses.

**Labour displacement inverted**: Where R.U.R. feared robots taking human jobs,
*Blade Runner* depicts replicants enslaved in the most dangerous, degrading human
jobs — the economic logic of disposable labour.

### Los Angeles 2019: Predictions vs. Reality

*Blade Runner* predicted 2019 Los Angeles with mixed accuracy:

**Right**:
- Massive urbanisation and inequality (though LA's skyline didn't match)
- Corporate dominance of social infrastructure
- Environmental degradation (the acid rain, the perpetual dim light)
- Multicultural urban space with hybrid language ("Cityspeak")
- Personal devices for biometric identification

**Wrong**:
- Flying cars (obviously)
- Off-world colonization
- The replicant technology itself (physical android bodies)
- The specific density and scale of the urban environment

### The Voigt-Kampff Test in Practice

The film depicts the Voigt-Kampff test as an operational technology — a police
procedure. The test scenes are the film's most direct engagement with the Turing Test
problem: how do you identify minds that are "genuinely" different from human?

The Rachael test scene is crucial: Leon's test takes 100 questions; Rachael's takes
over 100. The implication is that sufficiently sophisticated replicants can pass the
test, or at least make it very difficult. The test becomes unreliable precisely as the
replicants become more sophisticated — anticipating that AI evaluations will struggle
to keep pace with AI capabilities.

### Legacy

- *Blade Runner*'s visual aesthetic (neon, rain, noir) defined cyberpunk visually
- The film is cited in AI personhood legal arguments
- *Blade Runner 2049* (2017) deepened the philosophical themes, particularly around
  reproduction, memory, and the "real" human experience
- Roy Batty's "Tears in Rain" monologue is among the most analyzed AI-consciousness
  moments in cultural history
